# Chapter 24 — Case: Claritix — Context at the Speed of the Alert

*An anomaly-intelligence startup brand built around a single move — auto-correlating KPI anomalies with external signals (cloud status, deployment logs, news feeds) before the engineer has to investigate — anchored on a real audit story (47 alerts, 4 real, 1 missed at $200K cost) and the explicit *explanation layer nobody owns*.*

## Situation

Every observability vendor in 2026 — Datadog, Dynatrace, New Relic, Grafana, PagerDuty — solves the detection half of the problem. A spike happens, an alert fires, an engineer is paged. The deck reframes the gap precisely: *Most teams don't have a data problem. They have an interpretation problem.* The 90-minute investigation cost per alert, the alert fatigue at scale (47 alerts in one week at a mid-size insurance company; 43 noise, 4 real; one *silent drift* in claims processing latency missed for eleven days at a $200K cost in delayed settlements), and the *context-lives-in-people's-heads* problem that data engineering leaders cite as institutional-knowledge churn — these are the conditions Claritix was built into. The strategic position the brand argues is that the *explanation layer* is uncontested: every incumbent owns detection, none owns the auto-correlated context that turns an alert into a decision. Path B startup-brand framing applied to a product with a verifiable target market and a defensible competitive map.

## Architecture

The pipeline is four stages composed end-to-end with sub-ten-second target latency. **Stage 1 — Detect.** Real-time KPI monitoring against the [NAB benchmark anomaly dataset](https://github.com/numenta/NAB) with configurable threshold sensitivity. **Stage 2 — Correlate.** External-signal matching against TechCrunch, VentureBeat, NewsAPI, and cloud-status feeds; the move that distinguishes Claritix from all five named incumbents is that this stage exists at all. **Stage 3 — Explain.** GPT-4o-mini generates a context-rich narrative around each anomaly — the deck's example reads *"CPU +47% above baseline. Correlated: TechCrunch-reported AWS degradation at 9:02 AM."* The explanation cites its sources by name. **Stage 4 — Act.** Engineers receive answers, not just alerts. Zero manual investigation needed.

The streaming pipeline is Kafka-powered ingestion at sub-second cadence across KPI streams. The orchestration runs through n8n. The interface is Streamlit. The reasoning layer is GPT-4o-mini deliberately scoped — the model's job is bounded to *generate a narrative explanation given correlated signals*, not to produce raw judgments without evidence. The live demonstration is hosted at [madison-framework-xselbtfvfdctrdgkv5fkem.streamlit.app](https://madison-framework-xselbtfvfdctrdgkv5fkem.streamlit.app) [verify], with NAB benchmark data driving the anomaly traces and live external-signal matching driving the explanation pass.

The brand surfaces are coordinated. The [marketing site at claritix.netlify.app](https://claritix.netlify.app) hosts the landing page with full brand identity and an interactive anomaly demo. The [LinkedIn at ankit-deopurkar](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ankit-deopurkar) carries brand-consistent header and featured content. ## Design rationale

The architectural commitment that earns the system's name is **the explanation step lives on a different code path from the detection step**. Stage 1 (detection on NAB benchmark data) and Stage 2 (external-signal correlation against named feeds) are both deterministic — no LLM. Only Stage 3 invokes GPT-4o-mini, and the prompt is bounded by Stage 2's correlated evidence rather than asked to produce raw causal claims. This is the architectural decorrelation pattern Chapter 7 of the theory spine argues for: do not ask the model to verify itself. Detection produces a signal; correlation produces grounded evidence; explanation produces the prose narrative. The narrative is honest about its sources because the prompt cannot synthesize sources the correlation step did not retrieve.

The **cited-and-verifiable explanation layer** is the second consequential design move. The deck commits explicitly: *every explanation cites its sources. Cloud status, news feed, deployment log — you always see where the context came from. Black boxes kill trust.* That commitment maps directly onto Chapter 7's Fact Check List Pattern at the brand level. The user-facing surface preserves the SUPPORTED / INSUFFICIENT EVIDENCE distinction by always showing the source links alongside the narrative. An engineer who does not trust the AI-generated explanation can verify it against the named TechCrunch article, VentureBeat post, or AWS-status entry in one click.

The **competitive whitespace argument rendered as a 2×2** is the third design move. The deck's competitive-landscape slide places five incumbents and Claritix on a *Detection ↔ Explanation* axis crossed with a *Generic / Enterprise ↔ Practitioner-Built* axis. Datadog, Grafana, PagerDuty, Dynatrace, and New Relic cluster in the *Detection / Generic* quadrant. Claritix occupies *Explanation / Practitioner-Built* alone. The visual rendering is the brand's strategic argument made before any prose is read: *no incumbent owns the explanation layer. Claritix enters an uncontested position.* That is the *blue ocean* claim the brand explicitly stakes.

## Trade-offs

The cited-explanation discipline is the brand's load-bearing pillar and is brittle. Stage 3 narratives that fail to surface source citations would damage the *trust through transparency* commitment more than any false-positive in Stage 1 detection would. The fix is the architectural separation: Stage 3 cannot produce a narrative without Stage 2's correlated evidence as input. The *90 min → 9 sec* claim is the brand's headline metric and depends on the external-feed APIs (TechCrunch, VentureBeat, NewsAPI, cloud-status endpoints) remaining available at predictable latency; the chapter's discussion of degraded modes (graceful fallback to detection-only when correlation feeds are slow or down) is the most consequential next-revision target. The $200K *cost-of-one-missed-anomaly* claim is grounded in the Overcoming the Monster brand story and sourced to a specific incident at a mid-size insurance company; that source is anonymized appropriately for storytelling but [verify] — confirm the figure and incident type are documented at the README level so the brand's central anchor metric carries a verifiable provenance. The *47 alerts, 4 real, 1 missed* breakdown is similarly anchored to the same incident.

Both are appropriately scoped as next-and-later rather than as shipped, which is the *honest reporting* discipline the case template requires.
