# Chapter 28 — Case: TriagePilot — Automate the Exhaustion, Not the Empathy

*A multi-modal customer-support-triage startup brand built on a deliberate inversion of the conversational-AI category — automate the back-room ticket sorting, leave the empathetic conversation to humans — anchored on a transparent *glass-box* orchestration architecture argued explicitly against the *black-box* AI chatbots the brand competes against.*

## Situation

Customer-support teams in 2026 face an automation-shaped problem the conversational-AI category has not solved well. The dominant pattern — Zendesk-and-Intercom-style chatbots and increasingly capable LLM front doors — tries to *automate the conversation*: deflect customers at the first touch, resolve simple queries without human escalation, reduce headcount on the front line. The deck argues that pattern is structurally wrong. The exhausting work in customer support is not the conversation; it is the back-office triage that decides where each ticket goes — *who reads it, who routes it, who responds*. The deck names the consequence: *50% agent turnover from burnout*; engineers spending *80% of their shift doing soul-crushing manual data entry*. TriagePilot inverts the category. The deck commits to the inversion in a tagline: *Stop trying to automate the conversation. Start automating the exhaustion.* The architectural commitment that follows — *automate the back-room, leave the empathy to humans* — is the brand's structural argument and the differentiator against every black-box conversational-AI competitor.

## Architecture

The pipeline is a multi-modal orchestration engine that ingests support tickets containing both text and image content, classifies and routes them via deterministic logic, and surfaces the routing decision with full audit trace. **Stage 1 — Multi-Modal Data Acquisition.** Ingestion nodes simultaneously process unstructured text and attached visual evidence (screenshots of 500 errors, broken UI elements). Text Processing extracts core entities, urgency keywords, and overall sentiment. Vision Processing scans attached images to verify claims or identify known system error codes. **Stage 2 — Deterministic Glass-Box Orchestration.** The deck commits explicitly: *instead of relying on opaque LLM confidence scores, TriagePilot utilizes a transparent, node-based routing logic built on n8n*. Every routing decision is auditable. Support directors can click on any processed ticket and see the exact sentiment score, image tag, or keyword that triggered the routing sequence. **Stage 3 — Customizable Workflows.** Routing rules adjust visually via n8n nodes, requiring no hard-coding to update. **Stage 4 — Empathy at Scale.** The robotic sorting cuts manual triage latency from an average of 14 minutes per ticket to under 2 seconds. Human agents receive tickets pre-tagged with context, visual verification, and priority status, preserving their mental bandwidth for the empathetic work the system explicitly does not automate.

## Design rationale

The architectural commitment that earns the system's name is **glass-box orchestration as competitive moat**. The conversational-AI category competes on model capability and conversational fluency; TriagePilot competes on auditability. The named competitive scenario on the deck makes the argument concrete: *a VIP client submits an urgent 500-error screenshot during a massive Q4 product launch. Standard AI chatbots fail to read the image, misroute the ticket, the client threatens to churn. TriagePilot reads the screenshot instantly and routes to Tier 3 Billing in 1.2 seconds.* The case-study scenario is doing two jobs at once. It demonstrates the multi-modal capability (vision + text simultaneously processed); and it demonstrates the deterministic-routing transparency (the support director can audit why the ticket went to Tier 3 Billing rather than being told *the AI decided* without explanation).

The **node-based n8n orchestration as a non-LLM commitment** is the second consequential design move. The deck names this as Stage 2's central commitment. Most current customer-support automation collapses ticket ingestion, classification, and routing into a single LLM prompt with confidence-score gating — which is fast to build, opaque to audit, and produces hallucination-shaped routing failures the support director cannot diagnose after the fact. TriagePilot's deterministic-routing layer is rendered as visual n8n workflow nodes that can be inspected, modified, and tested without the model-prompt-tuning loop. The LLM-driven components (text-entity extraction, sentiment scoring, vision-tag identification) feed into the deterministic-routing layer rather than replacing it. This is Chapter 7's Fact Check List Pattern argued for the routing problem: do not ask the model to make routing decisions; let the model produce structured features that deterministic logic routes against.

The **enterprise-dark-mode visual identity** is the third design move. The deck names the theme: *Premium Dark Mode (Enterprise-Grade Tech)* with Canvas Slate, Neon Lime, and Node Indigo as the named colors and Inter + JetBrains Mono as the type system. The aesthetic is unusual for customer-support tooling — the category default is friendly-bright-blue (Zendesk, Intercom, HelpScout) — and the dark-mode register filters the audience deliberately toward the *Skeptical Enterprise CTO* persona who *refuses to deploy black-box AI*. The visual system is doing the same work as the architectural commitment: *this brand is calibrated for the buyer who cares about audit trails, not for the buyer who wants a friendly chatbot interface*.

## Trade-offs

The *automate the exhaustion, not the conversation* positioning filters the buyer-market deliberately. Customer-support buyers shopping for conversational-AI front doors will read TriagePilot as not-the-right-product; that is a deliberate choice and is doing real positioning work, but it constrains the addressable market to operations-and-back-office-automation buyers within the broader customer-support tooling category. The *95% faster routing* and *14 minutes → under 2 seconds* claims are the brand's load-bearing metrics and depend on the n8n workflow's execution latency staying within the targeted envelope as ticket volume scales. The 1.2-second VIP-client scenario is presented as a Quest-framework storytelling moment rather than as a production-measured metric; documentation at the README level naming the test conditions, ticket-payload size, and vision-model latency would convert the storytelling into a verifiable benchmark. The *glass-box* claim is the brand's most defensible commitment and is brittle: any routing decision the system cannot trace back to the n8n node that produced it would damage the *architectural transparency* pillar more than any other voice failure.
