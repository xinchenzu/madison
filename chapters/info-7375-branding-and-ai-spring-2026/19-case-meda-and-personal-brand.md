# Chapter 19 — Case: MEDA and the Outsider's Engineering Discipline

*A data-engineer-and-AI-builder personal brand built on a clinical-domain flagship — MEDA, validated on 5K+ records and 100+ benchmark scenarios — with the *outsider advantage* named explicitly as the brand's structural argument.*

**Author:** Ananya Thathigowdara Nagaraja
**Editor:** Nik Bear Brown

## Situation

A spring 2026 Northeastern MS candidate carrying production data work at Twinly (Boston, March–August 2025), eDocs Solutions (Mumbai, August 2023 – July 2024), Jio Platforms (remote, March–June 2023), and K-AKA Technology (Bengaluru, November 2022 – February 2023) faced a positioning problem the chapter calls out plainly: *data engineer* and *data analyst* are different lanes the labor market parses separately, and the candidate who has held both is usually read as having drifted rather than as having integrated. The brand task was to make the integration legible. The defining moment the brand uses to anchor it is biographical and load-bearing: *the first dashboard I built was never opened. Two weeks of SQL extracts, cleaned data, workflow diagrams. The team nodded, said 'great,' and went back to their spreadsheets.* The transformation that followed — from chart-builder to pipeline-fixer — became the brand's structural argument. *I had failed at the pipeline, not the visualization. Broken infrastructure means nobody trusts the data.*

## Architecture

The brand resolves to a positioning sentence — *I turn messy, high-volume data into clean pipelines and clear answers — from architecture to the boardroom* — and an UVP that names the unusual fluency: *full-stack data professional who architects the pipeline AND owns the narrative it produces*. Five pillars carry the load curve. **Engineering Rigor.** 2M+ records per day at Twinly; AWS, Airflow, Snowflake, dbt named as working vocabulary. **Insight Drives Action.** 42% query performance gain at Twinly; 18% faster decisions at eDocs; every metric tied to a business consequence. **Full-Stack Versatility.** Pipelines, dashboards, ML models, and AI tools framed as one continuous capability. **AI-Augmented Solving.** MEDA — 90%+ benchmark accuracy on 5K+ clinical records, with the explicit framing *builds and deploys AI, doesn't prompt-wrap it*. **Cross-Functional Communication.** Three product teams at Jio; healthcare-executive KPIs at eDocs; the discipline that lets the technical work survive the boardroom.

The visual identity is a four-color system named explicitly on the deck: Deep Navy `#1A1A2E`, Brand Navy `#2C3E6B`, Signal Red `#C0392B`, Cloud Blue `#EAF0FB`. Georgia for display (an editorial-weight serif unusual for an engineering portfolio and doing real work to read as *boardroom-ready*), Calibri for body, Courier New for mono. The brand-rule slide states the discipline plainly: *every claim is a number*. The visual rule and the verbal rule reinforce each other.

The flagship is **MEDA** — a Medicine and Data Assistant clinical-decision-support tool deployed at [meda-clinical-tool.streamlit.app](https://meda-clinical-tool.streamlit.app) [verify]. The pipeline runs Python plus the OpenAI API targeting GPT-3.5; reported performance is 90%+ benchmark accuracy on 5K+ anonymized records, validated against 100+ benchmark test scenarios, with 60% reduction in drug lookup time vs the manual baseline and 30% fewer classification errors. The deck states the failure-mode discipline explicitly: *graceful degradation — zero silent failures*. That sentence is doing two things at once. It is a technical commitment (the system fails visibly when it cannot answer) and it is the brand's central argument made operational (the engineer who treats data errors in a clinical context as a category that *cannot* fail silently).

The deployment surfaces are a [Vercel-deployed portfolio](https://www.example.com) [verify exact URL — deck names it as Vercel/v0.dev], the [Substack at ananyant16.substack.com](https://ananyant16.substack.com) (two articles published — *Why Most Data Teams Are Solving the Wrong Problem* and *I Used to Make Charts. Then I Realized Nobody Was Using Them.*), [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ananya-n-thathigowdara) (two live posts, infographic + article share, ten reactions reported [verify]), and [GitHub at github.com/Ananya1ani](https://github.com/Ananya1ani) with the MEDA repo and project documentation. Email contact at ananyant166@gmail.com. The brand is positioned for *Data Engineer, Analytics Engineer, Senior Data Analyst, Applied Data Scientist* roles in *Healthcare Tech, FinTech, SaaS, and Series A–C startups*.

## Design rationale

The architectural commitment that earns the brand its name is **the outsider advantage as engineering discipline**. The second brand story names the move directly: *I came into clinical data as an engineer, not a doctor. That meant I asked questions insiders had stopped asking.* The pillar structure operationalizes the claim — engineering rigor and AI-augmented solving applied to a domain where insiders have, the deck argues, normalized failure modes that a structural newcomer would not normalize. *In healthcare, data errors hurt people. No 'approximately correct.'* The MEDA flagship validates the claim with measurable artifacts: 5K+ records validated, 100+ benchmark scenarios, 90%+ accuracy — and the explicit *zero silent failures* commitment that names the failure mode the design refuses to permit.

The **Georgia-as-display-font move** is the second consequential design choice. The default for engineering portfolios is a sans-serif at every weight (Inter, Plus Jakarta Sans, Space Grotesk); Georgia is unusual and risky — it can read as anti-technical to a default engineering reviewer. The bet is that the brand's two-audience framing (data-driven hiring managers *and* startup CTOs / Heads of Data who present to boards) makes the editorial-serif weight an asset rather than a liability. The hiring manager who has watched data engineers fail at the boardroom-translation step reads Georgia as *here is someone who can already write at that register*. The Calibri body and Courier New mono balance the editorial top with the technical-execution bottom.

The **two-storytelling-framework structural argument** is the third design move. *From Mumbai to Milliseconds* uses the Hero's Journey to compress the biographical arc (chart-builder → pipeline-fixer → integrator). *What Medicine Taught Me About Data* uses Overcoming the Monster to name the engineering anti-pattern (reports that contradicted each other, dashboards built but never opened, executives deciding on gut feel because the numbers couldn't be trusted). The two frames cover different terrain — career arc and engineering discipline — and together they argue the brand's central claim more durably than either would alone.

## Trade-offs

The Georgia-display-font deviation filters the audience deliberately. Engineering reviewers expecting the Inter-Plus-Jakarta-Sans default may read the choice as unfamiliar; the bet is that the audience that values boardroom-translation reads it as a deliberate signal of register. The five-pillar maximalism risks pillar fatigue at the visual level — the deck addresses this by tying each pillar to a specific named project or metric, which keeps the load supported. The MEDA *5K+ records validated* and *100+ benchmark scenarios* claims need the validation set's construction documented at the GitHub README level so the *zero silent failures* commitment can be checked against the test suite. The first LinkedIn post is reported at *7 reactions, 3 weeks*, and the cumulative LinkedIn engagement is listed as ten reactions — the brand reports the small numbers honestly, which is consistent with the *every claim is a number* discipline. The brand depends on the streamlit.app endpoint remaining live; when streamlit cold-starts cost the demo's first impression, the GitHub repo is the degraded-mode story.

## Outcomes and revisions

The brand is shipped at the level of artifacts a hiring manager can verify. Portfolio deployed [verify URL], LinkedIn optimized with two live posts, Substack live with two published articles, GitHub populated with the MEDA repo. MEDA is reported live at meda-clinical-tool.streamlit.app with the four headline metrics (90%+ accuracy, 5K+ records, 100+ scenarios, 60% drug-lookup reduction). The four prior production-and-internship roles are listed with named outcomes (Twinly 2M+ records/day; eDocs 21% rework reduction, 18% faster decisions, 14% data quality improvement; Jio 500K+ records analyzed, 62% usage variation explained; K-AKA 30% faster reporting). The market-test outcomes — interviews, offers, role acceptance for the April 2026 graduation — are not reported here; the brand is shipped, the test runs in the months following submission. The MEDA validation-set construction (how the 5K+ anonymized records were sourced, how 100+ benchmark scenarios were chosen, who labelled ground truth) would benefit from explicit documentation at the README level for the post-submission hardening period.
