# Chapter 36 — Case: AdverseAI — Drug Safety Intelligence as Public Infrastructure

*A drug-safety-intelligence startup brand built around a single democratization move — public FDA data plus public adverse-event records turned into research-ready output in under two minutes at one-fifth of a cent per query — anchored on a deliberate enterprise-replacement positioning and a Quest-framework origin grounded in Spring 2020 public-health research.*

## Situation

131 million Americans take a prescription drug every single day [verify primary source]. The safety data is entirely public — the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) hosts 17.5 million records free to download, the CADEC corpus annotates colloquial adverse-event language, and PubMed indexes the research literature that grounds clinical interpretation. The structural problem is not data access. It is data access at scale, by people without enterprise budgets. Veeva and IQVIA serve pharma giants at $50,000-and-up annual licenses with 18-month implementation timelines [verify figures] — the deck argues these tools are *structurally unable to serve* the long-tail audience: 500,000-plus graduate students in pharmacy, public health, and clinical sciences; 10,000-plus pre-Series-A small-biotech teams making go/no-go compound decisions; thousands of educators in accelerator programs, bootcamps, and policy-advocacy work. AdverseAI's brand task is to render the public data as research-ready infrastructure — *one input, three sources, instant results* — and to argue the long-tail market is the underserved category the enterprise incumbents structurally cannot enter.

## Architecture

The pipeline is a three-source integration engine targeting a 1.93-second-per-record processing latency with a $0.002-per-query cost ceiling. **Stage 1 — Concurrent API Queries.** The user enters a drug name; the engine simultaneously queries the FDA FAERS API, the CADEC corpus, and the PubMed literature index. **Stage 2 — Schema Normalization.** Three heterogeneous data sources normalize into a common adverse-event schema (preserving provenance — every record carries its source). **Stage 3 — AI Severity Scoring.** Records score on a 1–5 severity scale based on clinical significance, frequency, and cross-source corroboration. The configurable critical-threshold lets researchers calibrate the severity gate to their use case. **Stage 4 — Citation-Ready Output.** Color-coded source cards render with summary statistics, critical alerts, and exportable formats (dashboard view, CSV, JSON, auto-emailed PDF report) — every data point traceable to its source via inline PubMed DOI citations.

The user-facing surface is the architecture's *Simplicity* pillar made operational. Open [fda-adverse-event-ui.onrender.com](https://fda-adverse-event-ui.onrender.com), no login. Enter any drug name (Metformin, Lisinopril, the deck's example set). Configure record count and critical threshold optionally. Click *Start Analysis*. Results in under two minutes, exportable in four formats. The deployment surfaces are coordinated: the Render-hosted live tool, the [Vercel marketing site at adverseai-site.vercel.app](https://adverseai-site.vercel.app), the [Substack at substack.com/@manishasahu](https://substack.com/@manishasahu), and the [LinkedIn at manishasahu5](https://www.linkedin.com/in/manishasahu5).

## Design rationale

The architectural commitment that earns the brand its name is **the long-tail-as-underserved-market argument made structurally**. The deck's competitive-edge slide answers the reasonable skeptic's question — *Why can't Veeva or IQVIA just build a free tier?* — with the structural-incentive answer: *their business model is enterprise contracts; a free tool cannibalizes their $50K+ accounts; they're structurally unable to serve this market*. The argument is not that AdverseAI builds better technology than the incumbents (the incumbents have more engineering resources by orders of magnitude). The argument is that the incumbents *cannot* serve this market without destroying their own pricing power, and AdverseAI's free-forever-for-individual-researchers commitment is therefore the structural moat. The freemium path the deck commits to (free for individual researchers; institutional licenses for universities and accelerators wanting API access, batch analysis, custom dashboards) is calibrated for a market the incumbents have explicitly not entered.

The **multi-source integration as architectural-not-feature differentiator** is the second consequential design move. The deck argues three named moats: *multi-source integration (FAERS + CADEC + PubMed) is non-trivial; severity scoring methodology refined on real research use cases; community — 2,400+ researchers already using it* [verify community figure]. The first moat is the architecturally important one. FAERS alone produces raw event records without clinical context; CADEC alone produces colloquial-language annotations without structured volume; PubMed alone produces research literature without user-reported events. Each source on its own is incomplete. The integration — concurrent queries, schema normalization, cross-source corroboration in the severity scoring — is what produces research-ready output in under two minutes. A competitor entering the long-tail market would have to rebuild the three-source integration before delivering equivalent research-ready output, which is the technical moat the structural moat sits on top of.

The **Quest-framework origin grounded in lived public-health work** is the third design move. The deck names the inciting moment — *Spring 2020. A public health project. Three data sources — all disconnected. $50K tools or nothing.* The conflict slide names the persisting problem at scale: *three weeks to analyze one drug. Every researcher had the same story. Different drugs, same broken process.* The insight: *public data + repeatable patterns = AI can bridge the gap. Not enterprise. A research accelerator.* The resolution: *AdverseAI. One pipeline. Three sources. Under 2 minutes. $0.002 per query. Free forever.* The four-step Quest frame is calibrated for an audience that has lived the same three-weeks-per-drug frustration, and the *free forever* commitment is the brand's most ambitious single positioning claim — sustainable only if the freemium institutional-license path produces enough revenue to cover the per-query infrastructure cost at scale.

## Trade-offs

The free-forever-for-individuals commitment is the brand's load-bearing differentiator and is brittle. The $0.002-per-query cost ceiling depends on FAERS / CADEC / PubMed API availability at predictable rate limits and on the AI-severity-scoring inference cost staying within envelope; an API-pricing change or a model-routing decision that doubles per-query inference cost would push the unit economics into territory where free-forever is not sustainable. The fix at the architecture level is explicit cost-budget gating per query and graceful degradation when an upstream API rate-limits — visible to the researcher rather than silent. The 98% accuracy and 2,400+ active researchers figures are the brand's headline traction claims and need primary-source documentation: the validation-set construction for the accuracy figure (which records, against what ground truth, labelled by whom) and the active-researcher cohort definition (registered users vs returning users vs published-citation count). The $150K-for-8%-equity ask with a 40% engineering / 35% growth / 25% operations allocation is appropriately scoped at the early-stage-pitch level. The 1.93-second-per-record speed claim depends on the concurrent-API architecture not being throttled by any of the three external dependencies; the next-revision target is documented degraded-mode behavior for each source's rate-limit and outage scenarios.
