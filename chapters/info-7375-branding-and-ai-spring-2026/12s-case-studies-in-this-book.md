# Chapter 12s — Case Studies in This Book

*A reading map for the chapters that follow, and a note about AI that applies to all of them.*

---

The chapters that follow are case studies. Each one applies the book's framework — the Madison frame, the Jungian archetype work, the PRD discipline, the visual and storytelling systems — to a specific brand, person, or product. The cases are deliberately heterogeneous. Some are personas (engineers, designers, founders shaping their professional presence). Some are products (analytics platforms, security tools, multi-agent services). Some sit between the two, where a person's brand and a product's brand are the same thing because the work is the person.

You will not read all of them. You should not read all of them. Pick the cases whose subject most resembles what you are working on — by domain, by audience, by stage — and read those carefully. Then pick two cases that look unlike your situation and read those, because the framework moves are easier to see when the surface is unfamiliar.

## How to read a case

A case study in this book is not a recipe. It is a worked exercise — the same kind of exercise a math textbook offers when it walks through a solved problem before asking you to solve a new one. The structure of each case follows the framework. The interest is in *which* framework moves the case emphasizes and *why* — and what the case did with the moves the book describes in general but every brand must instantiate in particular.

Read each case looking for three things. First, the strategic choice the case is making — what the brand stands for, what it refuses, who it is for and who it is not. Second, the specific evidence the case offers for the choice — the artifacts that show the brand doing the thing it claims to be. Third, the failure modes the case avoided and the failure modes it remains exposed to.

You do not have to agree with the cases. Some of them will make choices you would not make for your brand. That is fine. The case studies are a corpus of decisions. You are training your eye for which decisions hold up and which ones the framework only papered over.

## How to write your own

If you are writing your own case study as part of this course, the structure is in the chapters that follow. Each case is roughly the same length, follows roughly the same sections, and ends with the same kind of artifact — a positioning statement, a visual system sketch, a launch plan.

The exercise is not to imitate the cases. The exercise is to apply the framework to your subject with the same discipline the cases apply it. Your case should be at least as specific as the cases here, and your strategic choices should be at least as defensible.

## A note about AI

The cases that follow were produced in a workshop where AI is both subject and tool. Every case engages branding for the AI era. Every case was drafted with AI assistance to some degree. The note that applies to all of them is the same — which is the point of putting it here rather than in each case file.

A brand strategy is a commitment about what a brand will refuse to do. The model has nothing to refuse. It will produce a brand positioning statement for any brief, in any voice, for any persona or product, in seconds. The output will be grammatically professional, structurally complete, and strategically empty — because strategy requires the refusal that the model has no stake in.

Where the model genuinely helps in case work: producing strategic alternatives so the case author can see what their brand *could* be before deciding what it *will* be. Three or four candidate positionings, side by side, generated quickly, make the strategic choice visible as a choice. The model is good at the divergent phase.

Where the model genuinely helps in case work: pressure-testing a draft case by asking it the questions a hostile reader would ask. *What is this brand refusing? What is the strongest case that this archetype assignment is wrong? Where would this positioning collapse under competitive pressure?* The model surfaces objections cleanly even when its own positive recommendations are weak.

Where the model genuinely helps in case work: stress-testing the case for genericness. Paste the draft positioning and ask the model how many adjacent brands could claim the same position. If the answer is more than a few, the positioning is not specific enough yet, and the model has helped you see that without writing the new version for you.

Where the model does damage in case work: writing the case's strategic commitments. A model-drafted positioning is the average of every adjacent positioning the model has seen. It will read as plausible and it will not commit to anything. The reader who is grading the case — or the customer who is choosing between brands — can feel the lack of commitment even when they cannot name it.

Where the model does damage in case work: producing the case's specific evidence. The artifacts a case offers — the founder's actual story, the product's actual differentiator, the team's actual capabilities, the customer's actual response — are local facts the model can only fabricate. Fabricated specifics are the most dangerous failure mode in case writing because they are the most fluent.

Where the model does damage in case work: smoothing the prose. A case voice that has been smoothed by the model toward generic professionalism is no longer the voice that distinguishes one brand from another. The texture of the prose is part of what the case is teaching about the brand.

The rule that covers all three: the model can sharpen a case you are writing. It cannot author one. The strategic commitments, the specific evidence, and the voice are what make each case the case it is — and the model has no commitments, no evidence, and no voice of its own. When you read the cases that follow, you are reading the product of authors who used the model and remained responsible for the result. When you write your own, the same discipline applies. Use the model. Be responsible for what you ship.
