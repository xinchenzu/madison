# Chapter 35 — Case: SignalScout — Your Taste, on Autopilot

*An AI-research-curation startup brand built around a single inversion of the AI-tool category — the market outsources writing; SignalScout outsources reading — anchored on a contextual multi-armed bandit that learns the user's taste across three named evaluator agents (Skeptic, Scout, Operator) and renders the brand's pillars as architecture constraints rather than decoration.*

## Situation

AI product managers and frontier-AI investment analysts in 2026 face an information-volume problem that the AI-content-generation category has accidentally amplified rather than solved. arXiv cs.AI publishes more than 840 papers per week; aggregated across all AI sources the deck reports 4,300+ papers per month. Every existing tool — chatbots, aggregators, AI-branding tools — produces *more content from content*. None hold an opinion on the user's behalf. The deck names the structural argument plainly: *the market is flooded with tools that outsource writing. SignalScout outsources reading.* The user-facing problem is not information access (information is everywhere); it is decision velocity (the analyst still has no position on Constitutional AI 2.0 when the CEO asks). Two named personas anchor the brand. *Maya — AI PM at a Series C startup, 28–35*, opens 47 unread Slack messages and eight links every morning and walks into standup without a position. *Jordan — Senior Research Analyst at an AI-focused VC, 30–42*, makes fewer, higher-stakes calls on fundraise-worthy companies and finds bespoke research expensive while generic AI tools fail to take a stand. The brand task is to build the verdict tool, not the content tool — and to argue the inversion structurally so the buyer parses SignalScout against a category that does not yet exist.

## Architecture

The pipeline is a three-evaluator parallel scoring engine wrapped in a contextual multi-armed bandit that learns which evaluator to trust per user per item. **Stage 1 — Three Parallel Evaluators.** *Skeptic* judges rigour, penalises buzzwords, rewards reproducibility (baseline 40/100). *Scout* hunts novelty, rewards weak signals from frontier labs (baseline 50/100). *Operator* judges shippable, rewards deployable techniques this quarter (baseline 45/100). All three score every paper in parallel under a 2-second wall-clock target. **Stage 2 — Contextual Bandit.** A 15-dimension feature vector encodes the (user, item) context. Contextual Thompson Sampling balances exploration and exploitation — the engine does not always pick the user's historically-trusted evaluator, because the user's taste on *agent papers* may diverge from their taste on *alignment papers*. **Stage 3 — Action Selection.** The bandit chooses which evaluator's score to surface as the verdict for this user on this item. **Stage 4 — Posterior Update.** Feedback (user accepts the verdict, rejects it, or modifies it) updates the posterior via rank-1 Bayesian update; the deck reports *sublinear regret, verified in CI tests*. **Stage 5 — Persistent Taste Memory.** Bandit state persists per user across sessions; the deck names the resulting capability plainly: *SignalScout describes you back to yourself*. The example output: *"You reward Scout 2.3× more on items tagged 'agents.' Skeptic's picks correlate with your at 64%."*

The output surface is calibrated to the *Bloomberg terminal, not ChatGPT* register: a daily ranked email brief — ranked, reasoned, personal — with an Ink Black header band and the SignalScout logo lockup. Three evaluator *mood cards* render in the brand's three-color hierarchy (Azure Blue / Aquamarine / Ink Black) so the user reads instantly which evaluator the bandit chose for each item. Every number — scores, latencies, reward gap — renders in JetBrains Mono. The brand commits to the rule explicitly: *numbers earn monospace*.

The deployment surfaces are coordinated. The [live agent at signalscout.streamlit.app](https://signalscout.streamlit.app) is the working tool a user can run papers through in real time. ## Design rationale

The architectural commitment that earns the brand its name is **the four pillars rendered as architecture constraints, not as decoration**. The deck names the rule on the methodology slide: *"Decision over content" → one ranked brief, not an endless feed. "Accountability of taste" → every score has a visible rationale. "Personal over generic" → bandit state is persistent per user. "Silence over noise" → UI reduces to ranked verdicts, nothing else.* Most cohort startup brands list pillars as values; this brand names each pillar as a structural constraint that determined a specific architectural decision. The contextual bandit exists because *Personal over generic* required a per-user state that compounds; the daily email exists because *Decision over content* required a single ranked surface; the visible rationale exists because *Accountability of taste* required transparency at the score level; the reduced UI exists because *Silence over noise* required removing every interaction surface that did not move toward verdict. The pillars are the architecture's constraints; the architecture is the pillars made operational. That is the discipline Chapter 9 of the theory spine names as *visual identity governed by brand strategy* applied to product behavior rather than to visual surface alone.

The **verdict-tool category-creation argument** is the second consequential design move. The deck's competitive table places SignalScout against three named alternatives — Chatbots (*ask → answer*), Aggregators (*aggregate → summarise*), AI Branding Tools (*generate → produce*) — across five dimensions: Mode, Output, Personalisation, Memory, Proof of Learning. SignalScout occupies *Evaluate → decide / Verdict / Diverges per user / Learns you, compounds / 14× random after 500 decisions* — the only column with a non-zero entry on Memory and Proof of Learning. The argument is structural: the three incumbent categories were built for content-volume, not for taste-personalisation. A chatbot cannot become a verdict tool by adding features; the architecture is wrong. SignalScout therefore competes on a category axis that does not yet exist in the buyer's mental model, which is the strategic position the brand stakes.

The **scar-tissue origin story** is the third design move. The Karpathy-tweet anecdote anchors the brand on a specific recognition the user is meant to share: *I don't want to read Karpathy's read of the paper. I want to know if the paper is worth my read.* The Hero's-Journey frame names the inciting incident — *realising my "opinions" were six hops from the source; my taste was borrowed, not built* — and resolves on the trials: *six evaluator prompts, four bandit rewrites, three months arguing with my own tool.* The closing line — *products that come from scar tissue feel inevitable. Not invented* — is the brand's philosophical commitment about why this architecture and why this product. The story is calibrated for an audience that has lived the same six-hops-from-the-source frustration.

## Trade-offs

The bandit's *sublinear regret* claim depends on persistent feedback; users who do not consistently rate items ( or ) on the daily brief reduce the bandit's posterior-update signal and slow the personalization curve below the *30 decisions to start, 500 decisions to outperform baseline* commitments the deck reports. The fix at the product level is feedback frictionlessness — the deck argues a one-click rate-the-verdict surface in the email — and the calibration discipline is the rank-1 Bayesian update producing usable signal even on partial feedback. The 14× better-than-random and +444 cumulative reward-gap metrics are the brand's load-bearing performance claims and are reported as *tested in simulation* on the deck; the next-revision target is the same metrics on real-user feedback over a multi-month deployment, which the deck appropriately does not yet claim. The three-evaluator architecture is committed at three named agents; expanding to a fourth or fifth evaluator would change the bandit's exploration cost meaningfully and would damage the *silence over noise* commitment that constrains the UI to ranked verdicts. The verdict-tool category-creation argument requires the buyer-market to parse SignalScout separately from chatbots and aggregators rather than as a feature within an existing tool; the test runs in actual buyer conversations over the post-submission period.
