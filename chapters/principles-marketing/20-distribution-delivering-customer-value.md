# Chapter 20 — Distribution: Delivering Customer Value

*The Place P. Whatever else the firm does to its product, price, and promotion, the customer can't buy what they can't access.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define marketing channels and explain why intermediaries add value to products.
2. Distinguish direct from indirect channels and identify which channel structures work for different products.
3. Apply the three target market coverage strategies — intensive, selective, exclusive — to a real product.
4. Distinguish vertical, horizontal, multichannel, and omnichannel marketing systems.
5. Walk the channel-member selection, management, motivation, and conflict-handling processes.
6. Define supply chain management (SCM) and identify its five functions.
7. Define logistics and identify its major functions (warehousing, inventory management, transportation).
8. Evaluate distribution decisions for sustainability — carbon footprint, sustainable sourcing.

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **B2B markets** from Chapter 6 — particularly resellers, wholesalers, brokers — and the **value ratio** $V = B/P$ from Chapter 2. Distribution affects $B$ (where customers can access) and $P$ (channel margins increase price).

## Why This Chapter Matters

The Place P is often the least visible to customers and the most consequential. A product that's perfectly designed, fairly priced, and brilliantly promoted but unavailable when the customer wants to buy might as well not exist.

By the end, you'll be able to read any distribution strategy and assess channel choice, coverage strategy, and sustainability footprint.

## Concept 1 — Marketing Channels

### The motivating puzzle

**Outstanding Foods**, founded by **Dave Anderson and Bill Glaser**, makes **PigOut** — a vegan, bacon-flavored chip. To reach both vegans and non-vegans, they needed retail distribution that reached both audiences.

Outstanding Foods' solution: a **broker network** for nationwide launch, distributing through both **natural grocery stores** (vegan-aligned) and **mainstream grocery stores** (broader audience). The product's natural ingredients met **Whole Foods' strict food standards**, opening that channel as well.

The puzzle: how does a firm choose channels that reach the right customers without overspending on coverage that doesn't convert?

### The mechanism — what marketing channels are

**Marketing channels** (or distribution channels) are systems of people, organizations, and activities that move goods and services from producers to consumers. Ownership of goods transfers from one channel member to the next. The goal: deliver value to the final consumer by making products available.

**Direct channels** — manufacturer to consumer, no intermediaries. A local pizza shop selling pizzas directly to customers.

**Indirect channels** — manufacturer through one or more intermediaries to consumer. The same pizza shop selling frozen pizzas through grocery stores has added an indirect channel.

**Channel intermediaries** include:
- **Wholesalers** — buy in bulk from manufacturers, sell to retailers (Chapter 6).
- **Retailers** — sell to end consumers (Chapter 6, Chapter 21).
- **Brokers** — match buyers and sellers without taking ownership.
- **Agents** — represent manufacturers (sometimes multiple non-competing) to retailers.

Each intermediary adds value (logistics, market access, relationships) and adds margin requirements that affect the final price.

### Common misconceptions

**"Cutting out the middleman saves money."** Sometimes. Often the intermediary's services (logistics, customer relationships, market access) cost more for the firm to replicate than the intermediary's margin.

**"Direct channels are always better."** Direct works when the firm has the resources to handle distribution. For most consumer products, indirect channels are more efficient at scale.

## Concept 2 — Channel Design and Coverage

### The motivating puzzle

A new soft drink wants nationwide distribution. The brand's options: get into every grocery store, every convenience store, every vending machine (intensive); pick a few high-end natural grocers (exclusive); or land in major chain grocers (selective).

Each choice produces different revenue, different brand positioning, different unit economics. The decision is consequential.

### The mechanism — three target market coverage strategies

**Intensive distribution.** Distribute through every possible intermediary. Customers find the product everywhere. **Used for convenience products** (Chapter 12) where customers won't wait or substitute. Coca-Cola and most CPG categories use intensive distribution.

**Selective distribution.** Distribute through a smaller number of carefully chosen intermediaries. Used when the firm wants to balance reach with intermediary quality. Many consumer electronics and apparel brands use selective.

**Exclusive distribution.** Distribute through a single intermediary or very few. Used for premium positioning, luxury goods, or specialized B2B categories. Some luxury watches and high-end automobiles use exclusive distribution.

The choice is constrained by the product type (convenience requires intensive; specialty allows exclusive) and the brand positioning.

### The mechanism — channel system types

**Vertical marketing system (VMS).** Manufacturer, wholesaler, retailer act as a unified system — sometimes through ownership, sometimes through contracts. McDonald's franchisor-franchisee relationship is a contractual VMS.

**Horizontal marketing system.** Two or more firms at the same channel level cooperate. A bank in a grocery store partnership.

**Multichannel marketing system.** Firm uses multiple distribution channels to reach different segments. A brand selling through retail stores, its own website, and Amazon is multichannel.

**Omnichannel marketing system.** Multichannel + integrated. Customer experience flows seamlessly across channels — start a purchase on phone, complete in store, return through any channel. Modern retail standard.

### Common misconceptions

**"More distribution is always better."** Wrong. Premium brands lose equity from over-distribution. Selectivity preserves positioning.

**"Omnichannel is just multichannel."** Multichannel uses multiple channels. Omnichannel integrates them so the customer experience is consistent across all of them.

## Concept 3 — SCM and Logistics

### The motivating puzzle

A consumer orders a product on a Tuesday afternoon and receives it Wednesday morning. Behind that delivery: warehouse picking, inventory tracking, package preparation, last-mile delivery, possible returns processing. All hidden from the customer; all consequential for unit economics and customer satisfaction.

### The mechanism — supply chain management

**Supply chain management (SCM)** = managing all members and activities from raw material procurement through finished-goods distribution.

**Five SCM functions:**

1. **Purchasing** — procuring raw materials and components.
2. **Operations** — transforming materials into products.
3. **Logistics** — moving materials and products through the system.
4. **Resource management** — managing capacity, labor, capital.
5. **Information workflow** — coordinating data across the chain.

**Dave's Killer Bread** illustrates SCM tied to brand promise. The brand's promise — *"the best bread you've ever tasted, power-packed with organic whole grain nutrition"* — starts with selecting **certified organic suppliers** who can consistently deliver high-quality grains. The supply chain delivers on the brand promise; without aligned suppliers, the marketing claim breaks.

### The mechanism — logistics

**Logistics** = the planning, organizing, and controlling of physical movement of materials and products.

**Warehousing.** Stocking, maintaining, controlling products while they await next steps. Includes receiving, storing, picking, packing, shipping. Some warehousing requires temperature control (perishables).

**Inventory management.** Determining stock levels, reorder points, and turnover rates. Too much inventory ties up capital; too little produces stockouts.

**Transportation.** Moving products between locations. Modes: truck, rail, ship, air, pipeline. Each has different cost, speed, and capacity profiles.

**Third-party logistics (3PL).** Outsourcing logistics to specialized providers. Allows firms to scale without building in-house infrastructure.

### Common misconceptions

**"Logistics is just shipping."** Logistics covers warehousing, inventory, transportation, and the information systems that coordinate them.

**"Faster is always better."** Faster shipping costs more (financially and environmentally). The right speed depends on customer expectations and unit economics.

## Integration — Sustainability in Distribution

Distribution decisions have outsized environmental impact. Two specific tensions:

### Carbon footprint vs. speed

Customer expectations have shifted toward fast shipping (Amazon Prime's two-day delivery normalized expectations). Faster shipping requires more frequent transportation, more fuel, more carbon. Sustainability and speed conflict.

**Sustainable channels** balance speed against environmental impact. Some firms offer slower-but-greener shipping options at lower prices. Others consolidate orders to reduce total trips. Customer willingness to wait for greener options is rising in some segments.

### Sustainable sourcing

**Sustainable sourcing** = considering suppliers' social, ethical, and environmental performance. Benefits: improved reputation, brand protection, access to consumers who choose based on sustainability (Chapters 5, 9, 10's data).

The integration with $V = B/P$: sustainability commitments raise $B$ for the growing segment of consumers who care, often justifying premium pricing. They also raise costs. The trade-off is the firm's choice.
