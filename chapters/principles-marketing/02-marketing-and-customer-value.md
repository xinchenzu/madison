# Chapter 2 — Marketing and Customer Value

*The ratio in your head, the levers in the firm's hands, and the room they both operate in.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define marketing precisely — not as a synonym for advertising, but as the work of building, communicating, and delivering value.
2. Compute customer value as a ratio ($V = B/P$) and use it to explain why one product wins where a comparable one doesn't.
3. Identify the four layers of customer value (functional, monetary, social, psychological) embedded in any real purchase decision.
4. Distinguish the five levels of customer need — stated, real, unstated, delight, and secret — and explain why answering only the stated need usually isn't enough.
5. Decompose any product's marketing strategy into its four elements (product, price, place, promotion) and evaluate whether those elements are internally consistent.
6. Apply the three-environment framework (internal, microenvironment, macroenvironment) to predict where a firm's next marketing constraint will come from.
7. Trace the evolution from the production concept to the societal marketing concept and explain what changed — and why — at each step.
8. Evaluate a marketing claim for ethical integrity using the distinction between puffery, honest promotion, and deceptive practice.

## Prerequisites

This chapter assumes no prior marketing knowledge. If you can describe a purchase you made recently and say roughly why you made it, you have everything you need to start.

The one technical prerequisite is comfort with a simple fraction. The central model of this chapter is $V = B/P$ — benefits divided by price. If that notation looks unfamiliar, ask an LLM to explain what a ratio is before continuing. It will take three minutes and you will need the concept throughout the book.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Every business decision eventually touches a customer. A pricing decision, a product redesign, a choice of distribution channel, a social media campaign — all of them are bets about what a customer values and what will move them. This chapter gives you the vocabulary and the models to make those bets deliberately instead of accidentally.

The frameworks here — the value ratio, the 4Ps, the three environments — are not advanced marketing theory. They are the baseline. Every more sophisticated idea in this book and in the field is built on top of them. Get these wrong and everything downstream goes wrong with them. Get them right and you have a map that works across industries, product categories, and decades of market change.

We will test each framework against real examples. We will find the edges where each model breaks down. We will be honest about what the models hide. That is the practice of thinking in marketing — not memorizing definitions, but building models you can actually use.

## Concept 1 — Value Is a Ratio the Customer Computes, Not a Feature You Announce

### The motivating puzzle

Watch the commercial first. It is 2020. A boy walks onto a misted training field at dawn, and Michael Jordan is waiting for him in a varsity jacket. Serena Williams steps in from the side, racquet on her shoulder. Lionel Messi appears in a kit. Usain Bolt jogs up last. The voiceover calls it GOAT Camp — a mythical place where future *greatest of all time* athletes train with the actual ones. The camera cuts to the boy taking a long pull from a yellow bottle.

The ad does not, at any point, name the drink. It does not have to.

Now the number. In the United States sports drink market, the brand in that bottle holds **67.7 percent market share**. The next-largest competitor, Coca-Cola's Powerade, has 13.7 percent. Coca-Cola's BodyArmor trails at 9.3 percent. Two of the three leading brands belong to the same parent company. Gatorade outsells both of them combined, almost five to one.

That is not how categories normally work. Markets that mature usually flatten — entrants cut into the leader, share spreads out, the winner's lead erodes. Gatorade has held its lead for fifty-five years. The drink was first formulated by researchers at the University of Florida in 1965.

<!-- → IMAGE: Side-by-side shelf photo of Gatorade, Powerade, and BodyArmor — label the market-share numbers beneath each brand. Student should see the scale of the gap before reading the explanation. -->

The question this chapter is built to answer is: why? Gatorade is not dramatically cheaper than Powerade. Gatorade is not made from superior ingredients that no other company can access. In a blind taste test, many consumers cannot reliably distinguish them. And yet the ratio is nearly five to one.

The answer lives in a model so simple it almost doesn't look like a model.

### The mechanism — $V = B / P$

Customer value is a fraction:

$$V = \frac{B}{P}$$

Where $B$ is the benefits the customer perceives and $P$ is the price they pay.

The word *perceives* is doing most of the work in that sentence. $B$ is not a feature list. $B$ is a *feeling about a feature list* — the customer's internal judgment about what the product is worth to them, before they look at the price tag.

It seems obvious until you try to use it. So let's try.

Take two sports drinks. Same fluid volume, same electrolyte content, same calories, objectively equivalent on every functional dimension. If $B$ were features, the value ratio would be determined entirely by price — buy whichever is cheaper. But that is not what happens in the real market. Gatorade, at roughly the same price as Powerade, sells five times as much. Something inside $B$ is different.

That something has four layers.

### The four layers of $B$

Marketers decompose the perceived benefit into four distinct types of value:

**Functional value** is what the product *does*. Gatorade hydrates you and replaces electrolytes. So does Powerade. Both earn the same functional value — approximately.

**Monetary value** is what the price *feels like* relative to what the customer thinks the product is worth. This is not the same as the price itself. A $4 coffee from a specialty roaster can feel like a bargain to a customer who values origin and craft; a $2 coffee from the same shop can feel expensive to a customer who just wants caffeine. Monetary value is the customer's assessment of worth relative to price, which is a perception, not a calculation.

**Social value** is how owning the product *connects you to others*. The GOAT Camp commercial is almost entirely a social-value argument. It says: this is what elite athletes drink. The customer who packs Gatorade in their gym bag is making a statement — about who they aspire to be, about what their training means. The drink is a signal.

**Psychological value** is how owning the product *makes you feel about yourself*. The GOAT Camp customer isn't just signaling to others that they train seriously. They are telling themselves. There is an internal story — *I am someone who does this right* — and Gatorade is a prop in that story.

<!-- → INFOGRAPHIC: Stack the four value layers (functional → monetary → social → psychological) as a pyramid. Label each layer with the Gatorade example. Student should see that the functional layer is the smallest driver of the purchase, not the largest. -->

All four layers sit inside the $B$ in the ratio. A brand that moves only functional value and ignores the other three is competing on a fraction of the available terrain. Gatorade's fifty-five-year lead is not a product lead. It is a $B$ lead — built layer by layer across fifty-five years of marketing investment.

### The two levers for moving $V$

The equation hands you a clear set of options. To increase $V$ — to make the customer more likely to buy — you can:

1. **Raise $B$** — add a benefit, real or perceived. Better formula. Celebrity endorsement. Stronger brand story. Free shipping. Loyalty rewards.
2. **Cut $P$** — lower the price.

These look symmetric in the equation but are profoundly asymmetric in practice.

Cutting $P$ is fast, visible, and immediately reversible — but it costs you margin on every sale and is trivially easy for competitors to copy. If you cut price and your competitor cuts price, you have both lost margin and nothing has changed in the customer's perceived value.

Raising $B$ is slow, expensive, and hard to copy. No competitor can buy Gatorade's fifty-five years of sports associations. They can buy an ad campaign. They can sponsor a tournament. But the accumulation of meaning — the GOAT Camp commercial landing the way it does because of what Gatorade has meant for five decades — is not available for purchase at any price.

The firms that sustain dominant positions almost always do $B$ work, not $P$ work. They raise the numerator instead of dropping the denominator. Gatorade is not 67.7 percent of the market because it is cheaper. It is 67.7 percent of the market because its $B$ is enormous.

### The five layers of customer need

The $B$ in the ratio is complicated further by the fact that customers are surprisingly unreliable reporters of what they want. Marketers have learned to listen for five distinct layers of need:

**Stated needs** are what the customer says out loud. *I want a sports drink.* This is the request as typed into Amazon search.

**Real needs** are the specific version of the stated need that actually matters. *I want something that hydrates faster than water and doesn't taste medicinal.* The customer may not say this, but it is what they mean.

**Unstated needs** are what the customer expects but would never think to specify. *I expect the cap to reseal without leaking.* No customer puts this in a product review when it works; everyone mentions it when it doesn't.

**Delight needs** are what would genuinely surprise the customer in a positive way. *A Gatorade flavor I can't get anywhere else.* The customer didn't ask for it and wouldn't have thought to ask — but when they find it, they talk about it.

**Secret needs** are what the customer will not admit, even to themselves. *I want to feel like a serious athlete.* This is the GOAT Camp argument. No customer will type "makes me feel like Michael Jordan" into a product search. But it is doing real work in the purchase decision.

A marketer who answers only the stated need leaves four layers on the table. This is the structural reason that "just ask customers what they want and give them that" produces so many products nobody buys. The customer told the truth as far as they could see it. The truth that mattered was four layers down.

### Worked example — pricing the backpack

A backpack manufacturer is launching a new commuter bag. Production cost is $30 per unit. Market research shows that customers in the target segment perceive the bag's benefits as roughly equivalent to a competitor's bag retailing at $80. Two pricing scenarios:

**Scenario 1 — Match the competitor.** Price at $80.

$$V_1 = \frac{B}{80}$$

The customer perceives benefits worth $80 and pays $80. The value ratio is $\frac{80}{80} = 1.0$. The new bag offers no value advantage over the competitor. It wins on shelf placement, brand recognition, or luck. Margin per unit: $50.

**Scenario 2 — Undercut slightly.** Price at $70.

$$V_2 = \frac{B}{70} \approx \frac{80}{70} \approx 1.14$$

The customer perceives the same benefits but pays less. The value ratio is 14 percent higher than the competitor's. The customer has a reason to prefer the new bag. Margin per unit drops to $40 — a loss of $10 per unit.

The model doesn't tell you which scenario to choose. It tells you the *exact trade-off* you're making: $10 less per unit in exchange for a 14 percent value advantage. Whether that trade is worthwhile depends on how many more units the value advantage generates.

**General lesson:** The equation forces vague intuitions — "we should be competitive on price" — into specific questions: *how much margin are you willing to trade for how much value advantage?* A model that makes the question specific is doing its job, even if it doesn't answer the question for you.

### Common misconceptions

**"Value is what we say it is."** The most persistent mistake executives make with $V = B/P$ is treating $B$ as something the firm sets through advertising. The firm sets *inputs to perception* — the product's features, the ad campaign, the packaging. The ratio itself is computed in the customer's head. Marketing can influence perception; it cannot dictate it.

**"Lower price always means higher value."** $V = B/P$ makes this look true — cut the denominator, the ratio rises. But $P$ also affects $B$. A lower price can signal lower quality, which drops perceived benefits and may drop $V$ even as the literal price falls. Luxury goods are the clearest case: a $500 watch sold at $100 may be perceived as defective or counterfeit. The relationship between $P$ and $B$ is not independent.

## Concept 2 — The Marketing Mix Is Four Levers That Must Pull Together

### The motivating puzzle

In May 2022, the fourth season of *Stranger Things* arrived on Netflix, and JanSport released five backpacks themed to the show — the Hawkins town crest, the Hellfire Club, the Upside Down. Premium-priced relative to JanSport's standard line. Sold through JanSport's own channels and carefully selected partners. Promoted almost entirely by the cultural moment the show created — JanSport's own marketing pointed at the wave; it did not manufacture the wave.

The collaboration worked. The backpacks sold out. The brand got press coverage it could not have bought.

Now ask: why did it work? The product is a backpack. JanSport sells backpacks. So does every other brand in the category. What was different?

Everything was different — all at once, and all in the same direction. That coherence is what this concept teaches.

### The mechanism — the 4Ps

A firm's marketing decisions cluster into four categories. Together they are called **the marketing mix**, or the **4Ps**:

**Product** is what is being offered — the good, the service, the experience, the bundle. Product decisions include what features to build, how to design the packaging, what the warranty covers, what variants to offer.

**Price** is what the customer pays — not only the dollar number, but the structure of the payment. Discounts. Financing. Subscription versus one-time purchase. A product priced at $100 with twelve-month zero-interest financing is a different offer than the same product at $100 cash. Both are $100. Neither is the same offer.

**Place** is where the customer can get the product. This is *not* the firm's location; it is the *customer's access*. A Toyota sold at a dealership and a Toyota configured and ordered online at 11 p.m. from a couch are two different place strategies for the same car. Place includes every channel through which the product moves from the firm to the customer: retail stores, wholesalers, the firm's own website, third-party marketplaces, vending machines, pop-up shops.

**Promotion** is everything the firm does to tell customers the product exists and persuade them to want it. Advertising. Public relations. Social media. Influencer partnerships. In-store displays. Email campaigns. The GOAT Camp commercial.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Four-quadrant diagram with the 4Ps labeled. Add two arrows from each P to each adjacent P — showing that each lever affects the others. Not a simple grid; a connected system. -->

### The trade-off — every P exerts pressure on the other three

The four levers are not independent. They pull on one another, and the direction matters.

If you set a premium price, your product has to deliver genuinely premium perceived value — or the price becomes a lie the customer pays once and never forgives. Your place has to be selective — you do not put a $4,000 watch in a vending machine next to a $1.50 candy bar, because the context poisons the signal. Your promotion has to communicate quality, scarcity, craftsmanship — not volume and convenience.

If you compete on low price, your place has to be wide — mass-market retailers, Amazon, everywhere — because volume is what makes low margin viable. Your promotion can be louder, price-led, discount-forward. Your product can be simpler; features that don't justify cost premium get cut.

The 4Ps are a posture. All four have to lean the same direction for the customer to read them as a coherent offer. A premium-priced product on Amazon next to a $9 knockoff is doing two of its Ps in opposite directions. The customer does not experience the contradiction as a marketing problem; they experience it as confusion about what the brand is. Confusion is death.

### Worked example — JanSport × *Stranger Things* season 4

Run the four Ps on the collaboration.

**Product.** Five backpacks, each themed to a different element of the show. The functional benefit — carrying your stuff — is identical to any other JanSport. The social and psychological value has been completely replaced. The bag is now a fandom signal; the customer is buying *membership in the world of the show*, not just a backpack. $B$ has been rebuilt from scratch on top of the same functional chassis.

**Price.** Premium relative to the standard JanSport line. This is the correct move given the $B$ rebuild. The fan merchandise market prices on fandom intensity, not on production cost. Fans of cultural collaborations pay more without hesitation because the alternative is not "a cheaper backpack" — the alternative is "not having this backpack," which is unacceptable to the target customer.

**Place.** JanSport's own retail and direct-to-consumer channels, plus carefully selected partners. Not flooded into discount stores. The selectivity is part of the product — scarcity reinforces fandom value. A fan item that is everywhere is no longer special.

**Promotion.** Almost entirely borrowed from Netflix's launch of the season. JanSport did social posts and PR, but the engine was the cultural moment. The show created demand; JanSport pointed at the demand and said *we are also here*. This is the correct promotional strategy when you have no budget that can compete with Netflix's launch spend and no brand equity with the target audience that can compete with the show's.

Now test the consistency. If JanSport had priced the bags at $40 (standard price, cheap signal), the premium $B$ built by the collaboration would have collapsed. Fan merchandise that feels cheap reads as a cash-grab, not a celebration. If JanSport had put the bags in every supermarket, the scarcity signal would have evaporated. If the bags had been generic JanSport designs in Hawkins colors (weak product execution), fans would have noticed and said so, loudly, on the same social media platforms that made the collaboration visible.

The four had to lock together. They did. That is why it worked.

### The hidden layer — four kinds of utility

Inside the 4Ps, particularly inside *place* and *product*, are four kinds of usefulness that explain why access matters as much as it does.

**Form utility** is the product as a finished thing. You do not want raw cotton and thread; you want the finished shirt. The manufacturer creates form utility when it transforms inputs into something a customer can actually use.

**Time utility** is having the product available *when* the customer wants it. 24/7 pharmacies. Two-hour grocery delivery. A vending machine in a hospital corridor at 2 a.m. Time utility is often what the customer is actually paying for in convenience-premium categories.

**Place utility** is having the product *where* the customer wants it. The taco truck that parks outside the concert venue at 11 p.m. is delivering place utility. Uber Eats is a place-utility business wearing a food-delivery costume.

**Possession utility** is making it easy to *own* the product. Auto loans. Buy now, pay later. Returns within thirty days. Possession utility removes the barriers between the customer's desire and the customer's ownership — which is a real service, not a financing trick.

### Common misconceptions

**"Promotion is marketing."** Most people, asked what marketing is, will answer "advertising" or "selling." Both live inside *promotion* — one P of four. The other three Ps do most of the actual work, quietly. Promotion gets the credit because it is the only P the customer sees being made. The product decision was made months ago. The pricing decision was made in a spreadsheet. The channel decision was made in a meeting the customer was not invited to. The commercial is what shows up on their phone.

**"The 4Ps are a checklist."** Students often treat the marketing mix as four boxes to fill in separately. The discipline is not filling the boxes. The discipline is seeing whether the boxes are *consistent* — whether they tell one coherent story to one coherent customer.

## Concept 3 — The Environment Is the Room the Levers Operate In

### The motivating puzzle

In 2021, three large American companies — Tesla, Hewlett Packard Enterprise, and Oracle — announced they were moving their corporate headquarters out of California. Two named Texas as the new home. The stated reasons: lower housing costs, lower tax rates, fewer regulations.

None of those three reasons is typically labeled a marketing reason. But every one of them is a marketing reason. Here is why.

Every marketing decision the firm makes — what to price a product, how to distribute it, what segment to target — runs on a cost structure. The cost structure determines what prices are viable, what margins are available, what investments in $B$-raising the firm can afford. Move the headquarters to a state that costs less to operate in, and the marketing options expand. Not because the customer changed. Because the room the firm operates in changed.

### The mechanism — three concentric environments

A firm makes decisions inside three nested environments, each of which constrains which marketing moves are even available.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Three concentric rings — inner ring labeled "Internal Environment (5Ms + Culture)," middle ring labeled "Microenvironment (Suppliers, Intermediaries, Customers, Competitors, Public)," outer ring labeled "Macroenvironment (PESTLE)." This diagram belongs here, before the text unpacks each ring, so the student has the architecture before the detail. -->

**The internal environment** is what the firm controls. Marketers group these resources into the **5 Ms**:

- **Minds** — the people. Their skills, knowledge, creativity, and capacity.
- **Minutes** — the time. Deadlines, production schedules, how fast the firm can move.
- **Machinery** — the equipment and technology. What the firm can actually build or deliver.
- **Materials** — the inputs. What the firm buys to make what it sells.
- **Money** — the financial resources. What the firm can invest.

The 5 Ms are the firm's resources *and* its constraints simultaneously. A firm with strong minds and no money can plan strategies it cannot execute. A firm with money and weak minds can execute strategies that turn out to be wrong.

The internal environment also includes **organizational culture** — "the way we do things around here." Culture shows up in the marketing message whether the firm manages it or not. Zappos built its market position partly on a culture bet: the company paid new employees $2,000 to quit after their four-week training if they were unhappy, on the theory that a grudging employee would poison customer service more than a vacancy would. The customer heard the difference. The brand story and the operational reality locked together.

The mirror image: in 2020, *The Ellen DeGeneres Show* faced public allegations of a toxic workplace — sexual misconduct, intimidation, racism, with eleven employees speaking publicly. Ratings dropped. The show ended two years later. The marketing message — a warm, accessible daytime host — was contradicted by the internal reality, and once the contradiction was visible, the brand could not absorb it. Internal culture is a marketing variable. It is just one that most firms don't manage intentionally until it becomes a crisis.

**The microenvironment** is the small ring of actors immediately around the firm — close enough that the firm interacts with them regularly, but outside the firm's direct control. Five categories:

**Suppliers** provide what the firm needs to make what it sells. If two of your three suppliers merge and raise the price of the component you cannot manufacture in-house, you have a marketing problem: your cost structure just shifted, and your pricing options shifted with it.

**Market intermediaries** move the product from the firm to the customer. Walmart and Target are intermediaries between thousands of brands and millions of customers. Walmart's decision to stock or pull a product is a marketing decision — one that Walmart made, for Walmart's reasons, with real consequences for the brand.

**Customers** are who the firm ultimately serves. Knowing where they are — which segments, which channels, which geographies — is the prerequisite for every other microenvironment decision.

**Competitors** determine what space is available. Knowing where competitors are strong tells you where to find the customers they are not serving well.

**General public** is everyone else with an opinion: communities, journalists, advocacy organizations, regulatory bodies. The public's perception of a firm — its reputation — is a marketing asset that lives in the public's head, not on the firm's balance sheet.

**The macroenvironment** is the large outer ring — forces the firm cannot change but must navigate. The standard scan uses the acronym **PESTLE**:

**Political** — trade policy, regulatory environment, government stability, tax structure. The California-to-Texas relocations were a political-economic move.

**Economic** — inflation, employment rates, interest rates, consumer purchasing power. In 2022, simultaneous inflation, supply-chain disruption, and the economic consequences of Russia's invasion of Ukraine all hit consumer spending power at once. No individual firm caused that. Every firm had to navigate it.

**Social** — demographics, cultural norms, changing attitudes. Baby boomers (born 1946–1964) represent roughly a quarter of the U.S. population. The rise of active-adult retirement communities — Del Webb and its peers — is a direct downstream consequence of that one demographic fact.

**Technological** — new capabilities that change what's possible. Starbucks repositioned coffeehouses as places to work (not just drink coffee) in part because free public Wi-Fi became technologically and economically viable. The firm did not invent Wi-Fi. The firm noticed that a new technology had created a new customer need (a place to work outside the office) and built a marketing posture to capture it.

**Legal** — employment law, advertising regulation, intellectual property. The federal Lanham Act, for instance, governs false advertising claims and allows competitors to sue for damages. Legal environment changes what you are allowed to say in a promotion.

**Environmental** — climate change, raw material constraints, carbon footprint expectations. Increasingly, customers use environmental practices as a filter for perceived brand value. Ignoring environmental factors is becoming a $B$-reducing decision in many categories.

### The trade-off — controllable versus uncontrollable

The 5 Ms are the firm's. It can change them directly. The microenvironment is close — the firm can influence it through relationships, contracts, and negotiation, but cannot move it by fiat. The macroenvironment is essentially fixed for any one firm at any one moment.

This sounds like the outer rings matter less because the firm controls them less. The opposite is true. The macroenvironment matters most *because* it is hardest to change. It determines which moves are even possible before the firm makes a single decision.

A firm with a superbly optimized internal environment, running headfirst into a macroeconomic recession, will lose. A firm with mediocre internals, riding a strong macroenvironment tailwind — a favorable demographic wave, a technology shift in its favor, a competitor falling apart — will win for a decade. The outer ring sets the ceiling.

The practical discipline this implies: **spend most of your decision-making energy on what you control, but most of your attention on what you cannot.**

### Worked example — Tesla moves to Texas

Run the three environments on Tesla's 2021 headquarters relocation.

**Macroenvironment shifts that pulled Tesla toward Texas.** Texas's lower state income tax and lighter regulatory load (Political/Legal). Lower housing costs and lower wage pressure in the Texas talent market (Economic). The pandemic-era normalization of distributed work, which loosened the geographic assumption that a tech company had to be in the Bay Area (Technological/Social). None of these was within Tesla's control. All of them changed what was possible.

**Microenvironment shifts downstream of the relocation.** A different talent pool (different available Minds). Different vendor networks (different Materials and supply-chain relationships). The customer base doesn't change — a Tesla buyer in California doesn't stop buying because the factory is in Texas — but the regulatory environment for new Gigafactory construction changes significantly.

**Internal environment effects.** The 5 Ms shift: more hiring budget per head (Money), larger campus potential (Machinery), different labor supply (Minds). The internal environment didn't *cause* the move; the macroenvironment did. The internal effects are downstream.

**Marketing payoff.** The direct marketing implications are indirect but real. Lower operating costs mean the existing product can generate more margin, or the next product can be priced lower to open a new segment, or the firm can invest more in $B$-raising activities. The macroenvironment move expanded the marketing option set. That is the whole point of the environment scan: figure out which marketing decisions just became possible — or impossible — because of something outside the firm.

### Common misconception — PESTLE is not a worksheet

Students encounter PESTLE as an assignment: fill in each box, hand it in. The discipline is not filling the boxes. For most real decisions, four of the six factors will be irrelevant and one will dominate. A cosmetics brand assessing whether to enter the Chinese market cares enormously about the Political and Social factors and barely at all about the Environmental factor in the short term. A shipping company buying a new fleet cares enormously about the Environmental factor (emissions regulations) and less about the Social factor.

PESTLE is a scan, not a checklist. The skill is identifying which one or two factors will actually move your decision — and giving those factors the attention they deserve while not wasting time on the four that don't apply.

## Integration — The Levers, the Ratio, and the Room, Together

Here is the full picture assembled.

The customer carries a ratio in their head: $V = B/P$. The $B$ is layered across four value types (functional, monetary, social, psychological) and shaped by five levels of need (stated, real, unstated, delight, secret) the customer may not be able to articulate cleanly.

The firm pulls four levers — product, price, place, promotion — to move $V$ in its favor. The levers are coupled; pulling one without thinking about the other three produces a mix that tells the customer two different stories at once, which is worse than either story told alone.

The levers operate inside three nested environments — internal (5 Ms and culture), microenvironment (suppliers, intermediaries, customers, competitors, public), macroenvironment (PESTLE) — that constrain which moves are even available.

How does a firm actually run this loop, day after day? The field has settled on a five-step process:

1. **Understand the marketplace and customers.** Who are they? What is their $B$? Which of the five need layers matter most in this category?
2. **Develop a customer-driven marketing strategy.** Choose a target market — a specific segment, not "everyone" — and write a **value proposition**: a one-sentence promise of what the firm will deliver that the competition won't. DuckDuckGo's value proposition is essentially "Tired of being tracked online? We can help." HelloFresh's is "Take the stress out of mealtime." Both compress the firm's $B$ into a sentence the customer can carry and repeat. A value proposition is not a tagline. It is a contract.
3. **Deliver high customer value.** Pull the levers. Run the mix. Make sure the four Ps are telling the same story.
4. **Grow profitable customer relationships.** This is where **customer relationship management (CRM)** lives — the systems and strategies firms use to track, manage, and deepen interactions with customers over time. Salesforce has held the top CRM market-share position for eight consecutive years. The business logic behind CRM is stark: customers who come back are dramatically cheaper to sell to than new customers. Roughly **84 percent of customers say loyalty programs incentivize them to stay with a brand**; **66 percent say rewards programs change their actual spending behavior**. Loyalty is the cheapest growth lever a firm has. It is also the slowest to build.
5. **Capture customer value as profit.** **Customer equity** — the total lifetime value of all the firm's customer relationships combined — is the metric that connects the marketing investment to the balance sheet. High customer equity means the firm has built something that will generate revenue in the future. Market valuations track it because investors understand that the next decade's revenue is already partly decided by the relationships the firm has built.

### A brief history — how the marketing concept evolved

This five-step loop did not arrive fully formed. It was built over roughly 175 years of trial and error, and each step in the evolution reveals something about what the previous generation got wrong.

**1850s — the production concept.** In 1850, a watch company in Waltham, Massachusetts became the first in the United States to mass-produce watches using division of labor. Fifteen years earlier, Samuel Colt had begun mass-producing revolvers. Goods that had been hand-made one at a time now came off a line. Households that couldn't previously afford a clock now could. The implicit marketing posture was: *if we build it cheaply enough and put it in front of enough people, they will buy it*. Availability was the problem; availability was the solution.

**1920s–1950s — the product concept.** With availability solved, customers started comparing. Features and quality became the margin. The firm that made the better product won. The failure mode was quiet but fatal: firms fell so in love with their products that they stopped paying attention to what customers actually needed. Theodore Levitt named the failure in his 1960 essay *Marketing Myopia*: American railroads lost passengers not because demand for transportation declined, but because railroads thought they were in the railroad business rather than the transportation business. They optimized the product and ignored the customer.

**1950s–1970s — the sales concept.** Mass production outran demand. Supply exceeded what customers were spontaneously willing to buy. Firms pivoted to aggressive selling: push hard, advertise loud, close the deal. It worked for as long as customers had no alternatives and no good way to share negative experiences with one another.

**The marketing concept — present.** The marketing concept inverted the logic of everything before it. Instead of making something and finding someone to buy it, the marketing concept says: *start with the customer's needs, then build the product*. The 4Ps and $V = B/P$ both assume this orientation. It is why this textbook spends its opening chapters on customer value before it ever explains a product decision.

**The societal marketing concept — emerging.** The most recent layer adds a third variable: the firm must balance the customer's wants, its own capabilities, *and* society's long-term interests. **TOMS Shoes**, founded in 2006, built a company on this model — for every pair sold, the company donates a pair to a child in need. TOMS reports more than **95 million pairs delivered** to children in **82 countries**. The donation is not separate from the marketing. The donation *is* the marketing — it is how TOMS communicates a $B$ the customer can feel good about carrying.

### The ethics boundary

The 4Ps and $V = B/P$ give the firm enormous leverage to shape $B$ in the customer's head. There is a persistent temptation to inflate $B$ dishonestly — to claim benefits that do not exist, to imply endorsements that were never given, to manufacture urgency that isn't real.

Marketers call the inflated version **puffery**: exaggeration past the point of accuracy. Living Essentials, LLC — the maker of 5-Hour Energy — advertised its product as "doctor-recommended" and superior to traditional caffeine. The claims were found misleading. The company was ordered to pay **$4.3 million in damages**.

This is not merely a legal risk. It is a mathematical one. Mintel research found that **56 percent of U.S. consumers stop buying** from companies they believe are unethical. Twenty-nine percent share their negative experience via social media — which means the loss propagates. One trust failure, now visible to everyone, kills more $B$ than ten good campaigns can rebuild.

The ethical obligations read like a list of obvious things: be transparent, respect data privacy, respond to customer concerns, do not exaggerate, do not make false comparative claims. The federal **Lanham Act** allows competitors to sue for false advertising, which means false comparative claims carry legal exposure, not just reputational exposure.

What is harder than the list is the discipline. Ethics is not a campaign you run in Q4. It is the boundary inside which every other marketing decision happens. $V = B/P$ gives you the math. Ethics defines the domain over which the math is allowed to operate. Cross the boundary and the model breaks — not in the equation, but in the customer's trust, which is the only place it actually runs.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Dissect a purchase decision.** Think of a product you bought in the last week. Write two to three sentences for each of the following:

- The stated need that brought you to the product.
- The real need underneath the stated need.
- The secret need, if there was one. (If you're not sure, that's honest — name what you're uncertain about.)

Then identify which of the four value types (functional, monetary, social, psychological) drove the purchase most. Was the most important value type the obvious one? If not, what does that tell you about how the product is marketed?

*Tests: Objective 3, Objective 4. Difficulty: Low.* This exercise forces the student to apply abstract vocabulary to lived experience — the gap between what they think drove the decision and what the framework reveals is the pedagogy.

**2. Compute the ratio.** Choose two products in the same category — two laptops, two coffees, two gym memberships. List the perceived benefits to *you* for each product across all four value types. Estimate $B$ on a scale from 0 to 100 for each product.

Compute $V_1 = B_1 / P_1$ and $V_2 = B_2 / P_2$.

Did you buy, or would you buy, the higher-$V$ product? If not, what does the gap tell you? Either the model is missing a value type, your $B$ estimate was off, or the model is genuinely incomplete for this category. All three are worth naming.

*Tests: Objective 2. Difficulty: Low-medium.* The point is not the arithmetic but the discrepancy — which forces the student to interrogate either the model or their own perception.

**3. Identify the marketing concept.** Describe in two to three sentences the marketing orientation — production concept, product concept, sales concept, marketing concept, or societal marketing concept — of each of the following:

- A neighborhood hardware store that stocks whatever the owner thinks is useful and expects customers to ask if they need help finding it.
- An app company that releases a new feature every two weeks regardless of whether users asked for it, because the engineers enjoy building it.
- A direct-mail insurance company that sends physical mailers every week and follows up by phone whether or not the recipient expressed interest.

*Tests: Objective 7. Difficulty: Low.* Forces recognition of the concepts in disguised form, not just in the textbook's own examples.

### Application

**4. Audit the consistency of a marketing mix.** Find a product you think is mismarketed — the packaging signals premium but the price is discount; the advertising is aspirational but the in-store experience is mediocre; the website is beautiful and the physical product is disappointing.

Document the mismatch in specific terms: which P contradicts which other P, and how would a customer experience the contradiction? Then propose a fix: either move two Ps to align with the other two, or move all four to a different coherent posture. Be specific — not "improve the product" but "change the material of the carry strap and add a lifetime repair guarantee, which would justify moving the price to $90 and removing it from mass-market retail."

*Tests: Objectives 5 and 3. Difficulty: Medium.* The 4Ps are easy to learn separately and hard to see as a system. This exercise forces the system view.

**5. Run a PESTLE scan — tightly.** Pick a brand whose macroenvironment is changing — fast fashion, electric vehicles, streaming services, or any category where something external is clearly shifting. Write one sentence per PESTLE factor identifying the largest current pressure on the brand.

Then circle the one or two factors that actually dominate and cross out the rest. For the factors that dominate: is the firm's current marketing running with the change or against it? Name one specific marketing decision the firm made in the last year that responds — or fails to respond — to the dominant macro factor.

*Tests: Objective 6. Difficulty: Medium.* Forcing the student to cross out four of six factors is the pedagogical act — it breaks the "fill in every box" habit that makes PESTLE a checklist instead of a scan.

**6. Write a value proposition.** You are advising a small local coffee shop that is competing against a Starbucks two doors down. The shop is not cheaper than Starbucks. It is not dramatically more premium. Its differentiation, as far as you can tell, is that the owner knows every regular customer by name, remembers their order, and the shop hosts three local-musician performances per month.

Write two value propositions for the shop — one sentence each. Each should compress the shop's $B$ advantage into a phrase the customer can carry. Then evaluate each: which need layer (stated, real, unstated, delight, secret) does each proposition address? Which is stronger, and why?

*Tests: Objectives 1, 2, 4. Difficulty: Medium.* Forces integration of the value ratio with the needs framework in an applied context where the right answer is genuinely debatable.

### Synthesis

**7. Connect the environments to the mix.** Take the JanSport × *Stranger Things* collaboration from Concept 2. Run the three-environment scan on JanSport's decision to enter the collaboration:

- Internal environment: What 5 Ms would JanSport have needed to execute this? Which was likely the most constrained?
- Microenvironment: Which microenvironment actor was most critical to the collaboration's success — and most outside JanSport's control?
- Macroenvironment: Which PESTLE factor best explains why branded entertainment collaborations became viable marketing strategies in the first place?

Then connect your environment analysis to the 4Ps: which one P of the mix was most directly shaped by an environment factor you identified? Show the chain.

*Tests: Objectives 5 and 6. Difficulty: Medium-high.* The chain from environment to mix decision is the integration this chapter is building toward.

**8. Trace the evolution through one brand.** Pick a brand that has existed for at least thirty years. Document how its marketing orientation has shifted across its history — which concept it started with, where it is now, and what in the external environment drove each shift. Use at least two of the five marketing concepts. Be specific about the decisions and the years.

*Tests: Objective 7. Difficulty: Medium-high.* Forces historical and contextual reasoning rather than definition recall.

### Challenge

**9. The ethics stress test.** A startup is launching a new protein bar. Its internal research shows the bar is nutritionally comparable to competitors at the same price. The founder wants to run advertising that says the bar is "the cleanest protein on the market" and "the only bar athletes actually trust."

Evaluate each claim. Is the first claim puffery, a verifiable claim, or a false claim? Is the second a factual assertion or an opinion? What would the firm need to be able to prove to defend each claim if a competitor challenged it under the Lanham Act?

Then write two alternative taglines — honest ones — that make the strongest possible case for the bar's $B$ without crossing the ethical or legal line. Explain what need layer each tagline is addressing.

*Tests: Objectives 1, 2, 8. Difficulty: High.* Forces the student to apply the ethics boundary to real-world language, not just abstract principles. There is no clean right answer — only more and less defensible positions.

**10. Design a marketing concept for a product that doesn't exist yet.** Identify a genuine unmet need in your own life — not a product you wish existed, but a need that you currently solve badly, expensively, or not at all.

Write: the five-layer need breakdown for this problem (stated, real, unstated, delight, secret), a value proposition for a hypothetical product that addresses all five layers, the four-P posture the product would need to take to be consistent with that value proposition, and which one macroenvironment factor would most affect whether this product is viable in 2026.

*Tests: Objectives 1–6. Difficulty: High.* Open-ended synthesis. The constraint is intellectual honesty — the student has to describe a real need, not invent a flattering one.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do now that you could not do before this chapter:**

You can take any market — any category, any competitor, any product decision — and run three models on it simultaneously. First: what is the value ratio the customer is computing, and which of the four value layers is doing the most work? Second: are the firm's four levers — product, price, place, promotion — telling one coherent story or two inconsistent ones? Third: which environment layer is most constraining what the firm can do right now, and is the firm paying attention to it?

**The one idea that matters most from this chapter:**

$V = B/P$ is a ratio the customer computes, *not* a number the firm sets. The firm can influence perception. It cannot dictate it. Every marketing decision is a bet about what will move perception in the customer's head — and the customer's head is doing arithmetic the firm cannot fully observe. This is humbling. It is also the reason marketing exists as a discipline: if perception were transparent and controllable, you would not need marketing; you would just need a product.

**The common mistake to watch for:**

Treating the 4Ps as four independent decisions made by four separate teams. Product gets designed by engineering. Price gets set by finance. Place gets negotiated by sales. Promotion gets run by marketing. In many firms, this is how it actually works — and the result is a mix that tells the customer a different story from each P. The customer does not experience the four Ps as organizational decisions. They experience the mix as one thing. If the one thing is incoherent, the customer moves on.

**The Feynman test:**

Close the book. Can you explain to a friend — someone who has never read a marketing textbook — why Gatorade has 67.7 percent of the sports drink market even though Powerade costs the same and is functionally comparable? Can you explain it in under two minutes, using only the concepts from this chapter? If yes, you have internalized the chapter. If not, the gap tells you exactly what to reread.
