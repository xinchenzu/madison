## A Note about AI

Marketing is the field where the model produces the most professional-looking output and the least defensible strategy. The note examines what each looks like and where the boundary falls.

The model has read every introductory marketing textbook and a substantial library of contemporary marketing literature. It will produce competent paragraphs on the 4Ps, segmentation, positioning, brand strategy, channel design, pricing, and integrated marketing communications. The fluency is genuinely useful for orientation in a field whose vocabulary is dense and whose frameworks are many. It becomes a problem the moment a student or a marketer asks for the strategy itself.

Where the model genuinely helps: explaining frameworks (what the segmentation-targeting-positioning sequence is doing, what makes a value proposition defensible, what the difference between push and pull strategies is, what brand equity means), producing the canonical case studies, surveying the historical evolution of marketing thought, and generating structured alternatives for any strategic question (here are three positioning options for this brand, here are the trade-offs of each).

Where the model does damage: producing the actual marketing strategy. Marketing strategy is a set of commitments — what the brand stands for, who it is for, what it refuses to do — and commitment is the operation the model cannot perform. A model-drafted positioning statement reads as polished and means nothing. A model-drafted brand story is structurally similar to thousands of others. A model-drafted campaign concept is the average of many concepts the model has read about.

A specific failure mode worth naming: the model is particularly fluent in *brand voice* — producing copy in a register the user describes — without the underlying strategic commitments that make a real brand voice distinct. A student who accepts the model's voice as the brand's voice has accepted a generic that will fail to differentiate the brand from any of its competitors.

The rule that covers all three: frameworks and case studies from the model; strategy from your team's commitments. Marketing is the discipline of making choices that exclude alternatives, and the model is structurally biased away from excluding alternatives. Use the model to understand the space. Make the strategy yourself, and own it.
