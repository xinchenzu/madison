# Chapter 13 — Maintaining a Competitive Edge with New Offerings

*New products are how firms grow. The discipline of getting from idea to commercial launch — without losing too much money or stealing someone else's intellectual property — is most of the work.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Distinguish the five categories of "new" products and explain how the category affects marketing strategy.
2. Walk the seven-stage new product development process and identify which stage a real product is in.
3. Apply the consumer adoption hierarchy (awareness → interest → evaluation → trial → adoption) to a new-product launch.
4. Identify the five Rogers adopter categories (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards) and the marketing implications of each.
5. Calculate and interpret common new-product metrics including R&D as a percentage of sales.
6. Identify the factors that contribute to new-product success vs. failure and explain why innovative products with unique benefits succeed at higher rates.
7. Distinguish patents, trademarks, and copyrights, and recognize when a new product development decision creates intellectual property risk.
8. Evaluate an IP-related decision in product development using due-care frameworks (trademark search, application filing, freedom-to-operate analysis).

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **Product P** from Chapter 12 — product classification, lines and mixes, life cycle, branding, packaging. This chapter focuses on the *creation* of new products that enter that life cycle. You should also be familiar with the marketing research process from **Chapter 8** — the same seven-step research scaffolding shows up inside the NPD process.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most firms exist because of a successful new-product launch. Most firms also fail because of unsuccessful ones. The difference between the two is rarely luck — it's the discipline of NPD execution.

This chapter teaches the standard seven-stage process for taking an idea to market, the five-stage hierarchy customers go through when adopting new products, the five categories of customer adoption speed (Rogers' framework), and the IP-protection discipline that prevents the firm from accidentally stealing someone else's patent or letting a competitor steal yours.

By the end of the chapter, you will be able to read any new product launch and assess (a) which NPD stage produced the launch, (b) which adopter category the marketing is targeting first, (c) what success metrics would tell you whether the launch is working, and (d) whether the IP foundation is solid.

## Concept 1 — What "New" Product Means, and Why the Definition Matters

### The motivating puzzle

In China, **Swarovski** launched a holiday Advent calendar — itself a Western tradition adapted for an unfamiliar market. The calendar retailed for **$458** and contained **25 compartments with Swarovski crystals**. Despite the price and the cross-cultural translation challenge, **the calendar sold out quickly**. A digital version paired the physical product with interactive games — players collected unique cards and traded them for gifts.

Two questions the case raises: (a) is the Advent calendar a "new" product or an adapted existing one? (b) what made the launch succeed where many others fail?

### The mechanism — five categories of new products

The phrase *new product* describes more than one kind of thing:

- **New-to-the-world products** — genuinely novel categories. The first iPhone in 2007. Tesla's first Roadster.
- **New product lines** — entry into a category new to the firm but existing in the world. Apple's entry into watches.
- **Additions to existing product lines** — new variants within a familiar line. New Oreo flavors.
- **Improvements / revisions to existing products** — better versions of what's already sold. Each iPhone generation.
- **Repositionings** — same product targeted to a new use or segment. Arm & Hammer baking soda repositioned as a refrigerator deodorizer.
- **Cost reductions** — same product produced more cheaply. Generic versions, low-cost variants.

(Source notes "five different categories of new products" — the exact decomposition varies slightly by reference, but the category set above captures the spectrum.)

The classification matters because each category requires different marketing. New-to-the-world products need heavy customer education; line additions can leverage existing brand awareness; cost reductions compete on price.

### Worked examples — the Shark Tank successes

**Shark Tank** has aired more than **1,000 entrepreneur pitches**. Some have produced large commercial successes:

- **Squatty Potty** — over **$200 million in sales** since appearing on the show.
- **Bombas Socks** — over **$225 million**.
- **Scrub Daddy** — over **$330 million**.

What's instructive is the variance. Most Shark Tank pitches don't produce $200M+ businesses. The ones that do tend to share characteristics — a real customer need, differentiated product features, scalable production, defensible positioning — that this chapter unpacks.

### The trade-off — innovation reward vs. development risk

New product development is expensive, time-consuming, and risk-laden. Most new products fail. Yet firms that don't develop new products eventually decline as their existing products move through the life cycle into the decline stage.

The trade-off forces firms to balance:

- **Investment in NPD** vs. investment in existing products
- **Speed to market** vs. thorough validation
- **Innovative differentiation** vs. proven category positioning

Each of these trade-offs surfaces explicitly in the seven-stage process.

### Common misconceptions

**"Innovation = revolutionary new technology."** Most successful new products are improvements or repositionings, not new-to-the-world breakthroughs. Innovation is broader than invention.

**"A successful pitch on Shark Tank guarantees success."** The 1,000+ pitches include many failures and forgettables. The post-pitch execution determines outcomes more than the pitch itself.

## Concept 2 — The Seven-Stage New Product Development Process

### The motivating puzzle

A food processing company believes there is a market for a high-protein freeze-dried yogurt powder that can be added to juice for a nutritious smoothie. How does the company go from this idea to a product on grocery shelves — without losing millions of dollars to a launch that no one wants?

The seven-stage NPD process is the standard answer.

### The mechanism — seven stages

**Stage 1 — Idea Generation.** Generate as many ideas as possible. Internal sources (R&D, market research, employees outside R&D) and external sources (customers, suppliers, distributors, competitors). The yogurt powder idea may have come from any of these.

**Stage 2 — Idea Screening and Evaluation.** Filter ideas. Drop unprofitable ones early. Reduces development cost downstream.

**Stage 3 — Concept Development and Testing.** Turn each idea into multiple concepts. The yogurt powder might become:
- *Concept 1* — instant on-the-go breakfast drink
- *Concept 2* — high-protein snack vs. juice/milk
- *Concept 3* — post-workout protein replacement for athletes

Each concept defines different competitors. Concept 1 competes with cereal and breakfast bars; Concept 2 with juices; Concept 3 with protein shakes. Concept testing — presenting concepts to target customers and gathering feedback — selects the strongest concept.

**Stage 4 — Marketing Strategy Development.** For the chosen concept, develop a complete marketing plan: target market, positioning, planned price, distribution, promotion budget, projected sales.

**Stage 5 — Business Analysis.** Project sales, costs, profits over a planning horizon. Determine whether the financial case justifies further investment.

**Stage 6 — Product Development.** Create a prototype. Test for functional performance, manufacturability, cost.

**Stage 7 — Market Testing.** Limited rollout to a sample market. Observe real consumer behavior. Gather data to refine before full launch.

**Stage 8 — Commercialization.** Full launch. Production at scale, full marketing investment, distribution rollout.

(Source treats the full process as 8 stages; some references compress to 7. The substantive activity is the same.)

### The trade-off — stage rigor vs. time to market

Each stage adds confidence and adds time. A firm that compresses the process to launch faster risks producing a product that fails because critical validation was skipped. A firm that runs every stage exhaustively misses windows of opportunity.

**Research-based finding from the source:** new products with **well-defined and well-executed predevelopment activities** had a higher success rate than products that skipped predevelopment work. The trade-off favors investing in the early stages even at the cost of slower development.

### New product metrics

**R&D spending as a percentage of sales** is one common metric. Useful for comparing firms within the same industry (pharma and software invest much more heavily than CPG).

**KPIs** for new products include:
- Time to market
- Number of new products launched per year
- Revenue from new products as a percentage of total revenue
- New product success rate (commercialization vs. cancellation)
- Customer acquisition cost for new products
- New product profit margins

The right KPI set depends on the firm's strategy. A firm pursuing first-mover advantage prioritizes time to market. A firm pursuing differentiation prioritizes new-product profit margin.

### Common misconceptions

**"NPD is creative work; structure kills it."** The opposite is closer to true. Structured stages let creativity focus on the right problem at the right time. Without structure, creative work spreads thin.

**"The best products skip market testing."** Almost never the right call. Market testing reveals the gap between what the team thinks the customer wants and what the customer actually does. Skipping it transfers the lesson from cheap (small market test) to expensive (full launch failure).

## Concept 3 — Adoption, Diffusion, and Success/Failure Factors

### The motivating puzzle

In **April 2011**, **Mark Levin and Michael Dubin** — who had met at a party — launched the **Dollar Shave Club** website with a humorous video. They sold razor blades by subscription, much cheaper than market leader **Gillette**. The pitch rested on three customer insights: (1) Gillette's prices were perceived as too high; (2) men didn't enjoy in-store shaving-supply shopping; (3) subscription-box models were gaining traction.

Five years later, **Unilever bought Dollar Shave Club for $1 billion**.

The puzzle: what makes a new product succeed at this magnitude? The frameworks below split the question into two — how do customers adopt new products, and what factors predict success?

### The mechanism — the five-stage consumer adoption process

Customers move through five stages when adopting a new product:

1. **Awareness** — customers learn the product exists. Marketing's first job. (TV commercial during a football game introducing whitening mouthwash.)
2. **Interest** — customers want to know more. Marketing provides accessible information (websites, blogs, tutorials). (Customer calls dentist sibling about the mouthwash.)
3. **Evaluation** — customers compare against alternatives. Reviews, recommendations, social media. (Customer reads Amazon reviews before purchasing.)
4. **Trial** — customers test the product. Free samples, money-back guarantees, low-friction first purchase.
5. **Adoption** — customers commit to ongoing use, ideally as repeat buyers.

The hierarchy — sometimes called the *hierarchy of effects* — applies across product categories with variations. High-involvement purchases (Chapter 5's complex buying behavior) move through all five stages deliberately. Low-involvement purchases compress them.

### The mechanism — Rogers' five adopter categories

**Everett Rogers' diffusion of innovation theory** classifies adopters by how quickly they adopt a new product:

- **Innovators (~2.5%)** — adopt first; high risk tolerance; technology enthusiasts.
- **Early adopters (~13.5%)** — opinion leaders; thoughtful adoption; influence the next groups.
- **Early majority (~34%)** — pragmatic; adopt once a product is proven.
- **Late majority (~34%)** — skeptical; adopt under social or economic pressure.
- **Laggards (~16%)** — last to adopt; tradition-bound; sometimes never adopt.

The marketing implication: target innovators and early adopters first. Their adoption builds the social proof that converts the early majority. The early majority's adoption brings the late majority. The laggards adopt last or not at all.

A common new-product failure is targeting the early majority too early — the early majority looks for proof, and proof comes from the innovators and early adopters who already adopted. Skipping their conversion leaves the early majority unconvinced.

### Success and failure factors

The source notes a research finding: **innovative products that offer unique benefits versus the competition have five times the success rate of products with fewer elements of differentiation.**

Other success factors:

- **Predevelopment planning** — defining target market, concept, customer needs, product requirements before development begins.
- **Speed to market** — launching faster than competitors who could copy.
- **Marketing-team-and-R&D alignment** — products developed in isolation from marketing context fail more often.

Failure factors mirror these in reverse: weak differentiation, inadequate planning, slow execution, organizational silos between R&D and marketing.

### Common misconceptions

**"Marketing creates demand for new products."** Marketing accelerates demand for products that meet real needs. Marketing rarely creates demand from nothing. The Dollar Shave Club video was funny and effective because it confirmed customers' existing frustration with Gillette pricing — it didn't create the frustration.

**"All adopter categories should be marketed to simultaneously."** Targeting late majority before innovators have adopted produces marketing that lacks social proof. The sequence matters.

## Integration — Intellectual Property and Due Care

New product development creates IP — and creates IP risk. The discipline of protecting and respecting IP is part of NPD.

### The mechanism — three IP types

- **Patents** — exclude others from making, using, or selling the invention. **US patents typically last 20 years from filing.**
- **Copyrights** — protect original works of authorship (software, songs, TV shows, films).
- **Trademarks** — words or symbols representing a company or product, legally registered or established by use.

Collectively: **intellectual property (IP)**.

### Real cases

- **March 2021** — a jury ruled against **Apple** for patent infringement, ordering Apple to pay over **$300 million to Personalized Media Communications LLC**.
- **2021** — a judge ruled that **Google** had infringed on five patents owned by **Sonos**. If upheld, the ruling could ban imports of Google Home and Pixel smartphones.

IP infringement consequences include civil damages, lost profits, injunctions, attorney fees, and in some cases felony charges.

### Due-care practices in NPD

Three actions during product development:

1. **Conduct a trademark search** — verify the proposed name isn't already in use. **Takeda Pharmaceuticals had to rename its antidepressant from Brintellix to Trintellix** because Brintellix was confusable with Brilinta, an anti-blood-clotting drug.

2. **File a trademark application** — establish bona fide intent to use the mark within six months.

3. **Conduct freedom-to-operate analysis** — verify the new product doesn't infringe existing patents.

The cost of due care is small relative to the cost of infringement litigation. Firms that skip the discipline risk discovering they've built a product they can't legally sell.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do:** read any new-product launch and identify the development stage, target adopter category, success factors, and IP risks.

**The one idea:** new product development is a discipline, not luck. The firms that consistently launch successful products have predictable structures (seven-to-eight stages, adopter sequencing, IP due care) that the failed launches usually skipped.

**The common mistake:** targeting the early or late majority before innovators have adopted. The social proof needed by the early majority comes from earlier adopters; without it, the marketing lacks credibility.

**The Feynman test:** pick a new product launch you can investigate. In two minutes, can you describe its NPD stage history, its adopter targeting, its success factors, and its IP foundation? If yes, you have internalized the chapter.
