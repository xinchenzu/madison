# Chapter 18 — The Promotion Mix: Personal Selling and Sales Promotion

*Two more promotion mix elements. Personal selling builds relationships one customer at a time. Sales promotion produces immediate purchase action.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define personal selling and identify the products and contexts for which it works best.
2. Distinguish the major roles in personal selling — order takers (inside, outside), order getters, missionary sellers — and recognize when each is appropriate.
3. Walk the seven-step personal selling process from prospecting through follow-up.
4. Apply standard sales force management techniques — structure, recruitment, training, supervision, evaluation — to a real sales organization.
5. Define sales promotion and explain its role in the promotion mix.
6. Distinguish consumer-oriented sales promotions (coupons, samples, contests, BOGO) from trade-oriented promotions (push, allowances, displays).
7. Pick the appropriate sales promotion technique for a given product life cycle stage and objective.
8. Evaluate ethical concerns in personal selling and sales promotion using common-failure-mode frameworks.

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **promotion mix** from Chapter 16 and the **B2B buying center** from Chapter 6 — personal selling is most heavily used in B2B contexts where the buying center plays a significant role.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Of the six promotion mix elements, personal selling is often the largest expense in B2B firms — and the highest-impact when done well. Sales promotion is the element most directly tied to short-term revenue. This chapter unpacks both, with the operational detail needed to design and run them.

By the end, you'll be able to read any sales organization and identify how it's structured, how it should be measured, and where it's at risk of ethical failure.

## Concept 1 — Personal Selling

### The motivating puzzle

**Hilton Hotels & Resorts**, founded in **1919** when **Conrad Hilton bought his first hotel**, now operates more than **6,800 hotels** across multiple sub-brands — **Hampton Inn**, **Conrad**, **DoubleTree** (with the famous warm chocolate-chip welcome cookies). The Hilton Honors loyalty program offers **Silver, Gold, and Diamond tiers** with reward-night incentives.

A separate Hilton brand — **Hilton Grand Vacations (HGV)** — sells time-share ownership in exclusive club properties. HGV's marketing strategy includes offering loyal Hilton customers discounted three-night, four-day hotel stays in exchange for attending an HGV property tour and sales presentation. HGV sales professionals are typically licensed real estate agents; the goal is to close the sale during or immediately after the property tour.

The puzzle: HGV's sales process is meticulously orchestrated — the discount (sales promotion) brings the customer; the property tour (presentation) builds desire; the sales meeting (close) captures the commitment. The combination produces continuous revenue streams that pure advertising couldn't reach.

### The mechanism — what personal selling is

**Personal selling** is one-on-one interaction between a company representative (salesperson) and the buyer (customer or prospect). The interaction's purposes: uncover customer needs, present how the product or service satisfies those needs, build the relationship that converts into a sale.

Personal selling fits products that are:

- **High in price** — justifies the salesperson's per-customer time investment.
- **Complex in nature** — benefits from explanation and customization.
- **Customizable** — customer requirements differ enough that off-the-shelf doesn't work.

Most B2B sales (Chapter 6) use personal selling heavily. Many B2C high-consideration purchases also use it — cars, real estate, financial services.

### Relationship selling

When products are high-priced and customizable, salespeople pursue **relationship selling** — becoming trusted advisors rather than transaction-driven order processors. **TalEx**, a fast-growing HR firm, builds long-term relationships with corporate clients through sales reps who consult on talent strategy alongside placing candidates.

Relationship selling produces lifetime customer value far exceeding single-transaction selling. It also requires longer cycles, deeper training, and patience that pure transactional sales don't.

### Common misconceptions

**"Personal selling is dying as everything goes online."** Online channels add efficiency but don't replace relationship selling for complex high-value sales. B2B enterprise sales still require human interaction.

**"Sales is about closing."** Sales is about uncovering needs, building trust, and matching offerings. Closing is the last step — and only works when the previous steps were done.

## Concept 2 — Selling Roles and the Seven-Step Process

### The motivating puzzle

A B2B software firm has several types of sales roles: an inside-sales team that handles inbound leads, a field-sales team that visits enterprise prospects, a customer-success team that works with existing accounts to expand usage, and a "missionary" team that calls on industry analysts to influence the broader market.

Why so many distinct roles? Because different selling tasks require different skills, time horizons, and relationships. The roles are specialized to the work.

### The mechanism — selling roles

**Order takers** — sales personnel who primarily process orders rather than initiate them.

- **Inside order takers** — handle phone, online, in-person inquiries. Restaurant ordering supplies; cashiers at retail stores.
- **Outside order takers** — visit customer locations to take orders. Common in CPG distribution, some industrial supplies.

**Order getters** — proactive sellers who pursue new business. Hunt for prospects, pitch products, close deals. Most "salesperson" stereotypes describe order getters.

**Missionary sellers** — build relationships and educate without directly selling. Pharmaceutical reps detailing doctors on new drugs (the doctors don't buy directly, but they prescribe). Industry-analyst relations.

Most firms combine several role types. The mix depends on the product, the customer, and the buying process.

### The mechanism — seven-step personal selling process

1. **Prospecting and qualifying.** Find potential customers (prospecting); assess which are worth pursuing (qualifying). Methods: digital lead generation, trade shows, referral programs, purchased lists, networking.
2. **Preapproach.** Research the qualified prospect before contact. What are their needs? Who's in the buying center (Chapter 6)? What are they currently using?
3. **Approach.** Initial contact. First impressions matter; the approach establishes credibility.
4. **Presentation.** Demonstrate how the product satisfies the prospect's needs. Tailored to the prospect's situation, not a generic pitch.
5. **Handling objections.** Address concerns. Common objections: price, timing, fit, alternative options.
6. **Close.** Convert presentation into commitment. Multiple closing techniques exist; the right one depends on the situation.
7. **Follow-up.** Post-sale relationship management. Ensures satisfaction, generates referrals, opens future expansion opportunities.

### Common misconceptions

**"Sales is a numbers game; just call more."** Volume alone doesn't produce revenue without qualification. A qualified-but-smaller pipeline outperforms a large unqualified one.

**"Closing is the hard part."** The hardest part is usually qualification (Step 1). A well-qualified prospect with the right needs and authority closes naturally if Steps 3–6 are done well.

## Concept 3 — Sales Force Management and Sales Promotion

### The motivating puzzle

A regional sales manager has 15 reps across a 5-state territory. Some reps are crushing quotas; others are missing them by significant margins. Customer types vary; products vary; deal sizes vary. How does the manager structure the team, set quotas, motivate performance, and intervene when reps fall behind?

Sales force management is the operational discipline that answers these questions.

### The mechanism — sales force management

**Sales force structure** — how the team is organized:

- **Territorial** — each rep covers a geographic territory.
- **Product** — each rep sells a specific product line.
- **Customer** — each rep handles a specific customer type.
- **Matrix** — combinations of the above.

**Recruitment and selection** — finding the right reps. Sales managers often look for recent college graduates (trainable, lower cost) or experienced sales professionals (faster ramp, higher cost).

**Training** — product knowledge, selling process, customer-specific knowledge.

**Supervision** — quotas, activity tracking, account reviews.

**Evaluation** — performance metrics like revenue, gross margin, win rate, pipeline velocity, customer retention.

**Compensation** — base salary plus commission, bonuses, contests. Structure shapes behavior; reps optimize for what they're paid for.

### The mechanism — sales promotion

**Sales promotion** = short-term incentives for purchase. Direct call to action.

Sales promotion can be:

- **Consumer-oriented** (pull) — directly to consumers, encouraging purchase.
- **Trade-oriented** (push) — to retailers and wholesalers, encouraging stocking and selling.

Use cases:

- **New product launches** — induce trial.
- **Mature product defense** — reward repeat purchase.
- **Competitive response** — offset competitor moves.

### Consumer-oriented sales promotions

**Coupons.** Reduce price; prompt immediate purchase. **HelloFresh** sends direct mail with coupons for **16 free meals** — drives signups and customer acquisition.

**Samples.** Free product trial. Most effective in introduction stage; widely used in food, beauty, software (free trials).

**Contests and sweepstakes.** Engagement and excitement. Can drive social sharing.

**BOGO (buy-one-get-one).** Sargento cheese in grocery stores — drives volume.

**Refunds and rebates.** Post-purchase refund — drives initial purchase by reducing perceived risk.

**Loyalty programs.** Points-based rewards (the Hilton Honors example from Concept 1).

**McDonald's** combines sales promotion with advertising: *"Free fries on Friday when you purchase a menu item through the app"* — drives both Friday traffic and app adoption.

### Trade-oriented sales promotions

**Push allowances.** Discounts to retailers for stocking the product.

**Cooperative advertising.** Manufacturer pays portion of retailer's advertising.

**Trade shows and dealer incentives.** Conferences and contests for distributors.

**In-store displays.** Point-of-purchase promotional materials.

### Common misconceptions

**"Sales promotion is just discounting."** Coupons reduce price; samples don't. Contests engage without discounting. Loyalty programs reward without immediate discount. The category is broader than price reduction.

**"Sales promotion grows long-term loyalty."** Often the opposite. Heavy reliance on promotion can train customers to wait for the next deal, reducing willingness to pay full price. Promotion is short-term tactical; brand-building is long-term strategic.

## Integration — Ethics in Personal Selling and Sales Promotion

### Personal selling ethics

The structural risk in personal selling: sales reps work independently with customers, often with expense accounts and discretionary tools. Without controls, several common failure modes emerge:

**Misuse of company credit cards / expense accounts.** Padding expenses, double billing, personal use of business cards. Audit controls and clear policies are the standard prevention.

**Inflating sales data.** Reps under pressure to hit quotas may overstate progress. Pipeline reviews and CRM data integrity checks are the standard prevention.

**Bribes and kickbacks.** A buyer offers a kickback to a rep in exchange for favorable treatment, or a rep offers a kickback to win business. The FCPA framework (Chapter 6) applies internationally; antitrust and bribery laws apply domestically.

**Dishonest product claims.** Overstating capabilities, hiding limitations, comparing unfairly to competitors. The same Truth in Advertising standards from Chapter 17 apply.

**Slander of competitors.** Negative statements about competitors that could constitute defamation or false advertising.

The discipline: codes of conduct, supervised training, clear escalation paths, and supportive culture that doesn't pressure reps into unethical behavior.

### Sales promotion ethics

**Misleading promotion.** A "buy one get one free" promotion where the firm raises the price of one item just before the promotion is deceptive (the same FTC standard from Chapter 15).

**Promotion fraud.** Customers gaming promotions through fake accounts, multiple uses of single-use coupons, etc. Firms and platforms manage this through verification, but the firm bears regulatory responsibility.

**Bait-and-switch.** Advertising one product to draw customers in, then aggressively pushing them toward a different (more expensive) product. Illegal in many jurisdictions.

The connection back to $V = B/P$ from Chapter 2: ethics violations destroy $B$ over time. A short-term sales promotion that misleads customers may produce one quarter's revenue and several years of lost trust.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do:** read any sales organization or sales promotion campaign and assess its structure, process, and ethical exposure.

**The one idea:** personal selling builds relationships one customer at a time; sales promotion produces immediate purchase action. Both are essential; neither replaces the other.

**The common mistake:** treating sales as a numbers game without qualification, or treating promotion as a price-cut without considering long-term loyalty implications.

**The Feynman test:** pick a sales experience you've had recently. In two minutes, can you describe the role, the selling process steps, the sales promotion involvement, and one ethical risk that was navigated? If yes, you have internalized the chapter.
