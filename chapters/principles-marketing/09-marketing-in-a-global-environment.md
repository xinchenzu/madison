# Chapter 9 — Marketing in a Global Environment

*Crossing a border is not a translation problem. It is a marketing problem in a country where the firm has no instincts.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Identify the five advantages of international trade (revenue, decreased competition, faster growth, risk diversification, surplus disposal) and the four major challenges (business landscape, politics, regulations, culture).
2. Define **direct demand** vs. **derived demand** in international contexts and explain how home-market saturation drives international expansion.
3. Apply the seven characteristics of globally-successful firms (attitude, aptitude, magnitude, latitude, rectitude, exactitude, fortitude) to evaluate a firm's global readiness.
4. Distinguish the three types of consumer income (consumer, disposable, discretionary) and explain why discretionary income matters most to luxury marketers.
5. Compare the six modes of entry into global markets — exporting, franchising, licensing, joint venture, strategic alliance, foreign direct investment — on involvement, cost, and risk.
6. Distinguish standardized from adapted global marketing strategies, and apply each to specific product, price, place, and promotion decisions.
7. Use Hofstede's five cultural dimensions to predict where a firm will face cultural friction in a new market.
8. Evaluate an ethical concern in international marketing — dumping, exploitation, cultural insensitivity — using the frameworks from this and earlier chapters.

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **strategic planning frameworks from Chapter 3** (particularly the four growth strategies — penetration, product development, market development, product diversification) and the **STP frameworks from Chapter 7** (segmentation, particularly international segmentation with Hofstede's PDI introduced as the example). This chapter unpacks the full Hofstede framework that Chapter 7 introduced and connects it to actual market-entry decisions.

You should also be familiar with the **value ratio from Chapter 2** ($V = B/P$) — which works the same way globally as domestically, but with $B$ shaped by very different cultural inputs.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most marketing students will at some point work for a firm doing business across borders. The structural assumptions of domestic marketing — that customers share language, currency, regulatory environment, infrastructure, and broadly similar cultural references — break the moment a product crosses a border. A firm that ignores the breakage produces the kind of failures this chapter documents: **Starbucks losing $105M in Australia**; **Nivea's "White Is Purity"** social media disaster; **Gucci's $890 balaclava** that was pulled within hours of being criticized as evoking blackface.

This chapter teaches the discipline of evaluating whether a global market is worth entering, choosing how to enter, and adapting the marketing mix to the new context. The frameworks compound: the strategic planning loop of Chapter 3, the consumer-behavior frameworks of Chapter 5, the segmentation methods of Chapter 7, and the research methods of Chapter 8 all apply to international contexts — but the *inputs* become much harder to gather, and the *cost of getting them wrong* rises.

By the end of the chapter, you will be able to take any global market-entry question and frame it as a decision tree: is the market opportunity real (Concept 1)? Is the firm ready (Concept 2)? Which entry mode and marketing-mix adaptation strategy fit (Concept 3)? And does the cultural context support what the firm intends to do (Integration)?

## Concept 1 — Why Cross a Border, and Why Many Firms Wish They Hadn't

### The motivating puzzle

In **2020**, Starbucks operated **30,000+ stores across 80 countries**. Each fall, US consumers anticipate the **Pumpkin Spice Latte (PSL)**. In 2021, Starbucks rolled the PSL out across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa — but with formulas and advertisements specifically modified per market. **In Portugal, the *iced* version is more popular than the hot, so it's marketed more heavily.**

Starbucks's global expansion has not been uniformly successful. **In 2000, the company opened its first Australian store. By 2008, the company had reported losses exceeding $105 million and closed 61 of its Australian locations.** The diagnosis: Starbucks asked Australian locals to pay a premium for a brand they hadn't yet developed an appetite for. The marketing approach worked in markets where the brand had already won cultural mindshare; it failed in a market where it hadn't.

In **2018**, Starbucks took a different approach with **Italy** — the country with the world's most established espresso culture. Instead of trying to displace local cafés, Starbucks opened the **Starbucks Reserve Roastery in Milan**, where you cannot order a Frappuccino but can order **wood-fired pizza and cocktails**. Starbucks marketed Milan as *"the crown jewel of Starbucks global retail footprint — a place where Italian customers can come to discover the art and science of coffee."*

The puzzle: same firm, same product category, three radically different marketing approaches across three markets. The frameworks in this chapter explain why.

### The mechanism — five advantages of international trade

Firms expand internationally for five overlapping reasons:

**Increased revenues.** The most obvious advantage. Each new country expands the addressable market.

**Decreased competition.** Some markets are saturated; others are not. The US automobile market is fiercely competitive (most households own at least one vehicle). Developing-country automotive markets, with less infrastructure and lower vehicle penetration, may have less competition. Conversely, **China is the world's largest vehicle market by both sales and manufacturing output**, and is dominated by domestic automakers — making international entry difficult. The **Hongguang Mini EV** (China-based SAIC-GM-Wuling) has been a top EV seller since 2020. **Ford** is responding by launching three new vehicles offered only in China.

**Faster growth.** When the home market is saturated or growing slowly, the international market can sustain a growth strategy that the domestic market can't. **Amazon's Poland fulfillment center employs 25,000 people as of 2022** — a Polish economic effect that didn't exist five years earlier.

**Risk diversification.** A firm with operations in multiple countries can absorb a recession in one market with growth in another. The phrase *"don't put all your eggs in one basket"* applies operationally.

**Surplus disposal.** A useful but often-unsung advantage. Before each Super Bowl, the **NFL produces winning-team merchandise for both teams** (so supply is ready immediately after the game). The losing team's clothing is donated to other countries — disposing of surplus and reducing landfill waste. (Note: surplus disposal differs from *dumping*, which is illegal — see Integration section.)

### The mechanism — four challenges of international trade

Going global also creates four major sources of risk:

**Understanding the business landscape.** The internal-and-external factors of a market: industry structure, competitive intensity, distribution infrastructure, regulatory environment. The most common mistake is **ethnocentrism** — assuming the new market works like the home market. Ethnocentrism produces firms confidently expanding into markets they don't understand and watching the expansion fail.

**Understanding business politics.** Local norms, relationship-building expectations, decision-making protocols. The way deals get done varies dramatically by country. A US sales rep accustomed to direct negotiation will struggle in markets where extended relationship-building is expected before any business is discussed.

**Differences in regulations and marketing rules.** Each market has its own legal and regulatory environment. The **United States and New Zealand are the only two countries that allow direct-to-consumer prescription drug advertising**. A pharmaceutical firm's US marketing playbook is illegal almost everywhere else.

**Cultural differences.** Local cultural context affects everything — what colors are acceptable, what humor lands, what claims are credible, what gestures are taboo. Two cautionary cases:

- **Nivea's 2017 "White Is Purity"** social media campaign was perceived as racially insensitive; the slogan was then co-opted by white supremacists in their propaganda. Nivea apologized and removed the campaign.
- **Gucci's 2019 $890 balaclava knit jumper** featured material that went up around the mouth, modeled by a White woman with bright red lipstick. Critics noted the resemblance to blackface. The company immediately pulled the sweater.

Both failures originated upstream of the launch — in approval processes that didn't include enough cross-cultural perspective. The fix isn't *apologize faster*; it's *don't approve culturally insensitive material in the first place*.

### The trade-off — same-product economics vs. localized-product relevance

The fundamental tension in international marketing is between two opposite strategic logics:

**Same-product everywhere** (later: standardized global marketing) is cheaper. Marketing dollars spread across markets. Production benefits from scale. Brand consistency builds across borders. **Coca-Cola** uses essentially the same brand identity worldwide.

**Different-product-per-market** (adapted global marketing) is more relevant. Customers receive products and messages calibrated to their cultural context. Conversion rates rise. Cultural failures decrease. **Netflix offers different content libraries in each of its markets** based on local demand.

The trade-off forces firms to choose where on the spectrum to operate — and the choice rarely sits at either extreme.

### Common misconceptions

**"Global marketing is just translating the campaign."** A campaign translated word-for-word almost always misses the cultural meaning the original campaign carried. Effective localization recreates the *intent* of the campaign in the new culture's idiom.

**"If a product works in the home market, it will work anywhere with enough marketing investment."** False, in expensive ways. Starbucks Australia is a $105M lesson that some product categories don't translate regardless of marketing spend.

## Concept 2 — Assessing Readiness: Internal Capabilities + External Conditions

### The motivating puzzle

Two firms in the same industry consider expanding to the same new market. Firm A enters and succeeds. Firm B enters and fails. The market hasn't changed. The product category hasn't changed. The marketing dollars are similar.

What distinguishes the firms is **readiness** — both internal capabilities and external assessment quality. Marketing research can describe the new market; the firm's own capabilities determine whether the firm can act on what the research shows.

### The mechanism — seven characteristics of firms that win globally

**Douglas Quackenbos and coauthors**, in a *Harvard Business Review* article *"Does Your Company Have What It Takes to Go Global,"* identified seven internal characteristics:

| Characteristic | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Attitude** | Global expansion is a priority. |
| **Aptitude** | The company has the knowledge and skills to expand. |
| **Magnitude** | The scope of expansion is aligned with capabilities. |
| **Latitude** | The company has the ability to adapt. |
| **Rectitude** | The company has the legal and ethical flexibility to adapt to foreign markets. |
| **Exactitude** | Corporate culture aligns with flexibility and ambiguity of expansion. |
| **Fortitude** | There is a commitment to long-term expansion despite setbacks. |

The seven characteristics form a useful self-audit. A firm with high aptitude but low fortitude — strong skills but weak commitment to the long term — will pull out of a market the moment results disappoint. A firm with high magnitude (big expansion ambitions) but low aptitude will overcommit beyond its capabilities.

### The mechanism — external conditions

The external environment of a target country has four major components for marketing analysis:

**Economic infrastructure.** Roads, railways, ports, telecommunications, internet, electricity. Infrastructure constraints what marketing strategies are even possible. A firm that depends on overnight delivery cannot enter a market without a delivery network.

**Consumer income (three types).**

- **Consumer income** — what a household earns.
- **Disposable income** — what's left after taxes.
- **Discretionary income** — what's left after taxes *and* essential living expenses (rent, groceries, utilities).

The distinction matters because *which* income type a firm cares about depends on the product. Marketers of luxury goods care about **discretionary income**. Marketers of consumer-packaged goods care about **disposable income**. Inflation reshapes this calculus: when essentials cost more, discretionary income shrinks, and luxury marketers feel it first.

**Inflation and shrinkflation.** When inflation rises, consumers cut nonessentials and firms face higher input costs. One response is **shrinkflation** — keeping prices the same while reducing package quantity. **Procter & Gamble's Charmin Ultra Soft 18-count Mega Package was reduced in 2022 from 264 double-ply sheets per roll to 244 two-ply sheets**, with the price either holding or rising slightly. Shrinkflation is hard to detect at point of purchase — which is exactly the marketing point.

**Purchasing power.** How many goods can be purchased with one unit of currency. Varies dramatically across markets. Selling expensive nonessentials into markets with low purchasing power doesn't work regardless of marketing creativity.

**Currency exchange rates.** Affect both the cost of producing for export and the price consumers pay. **In 1997, $1.64 bought 1 British pound (so $1.00 = £0.61). By 2021, $1.37 bought £1.00 (so $1.00 = £0.73).** The dollar strengthened relative to the pound — meaning US imports to the UK got more expensive for British buyers, while UK exports to the US got cheaper for American buyers. Currency volatility is a structural marketing risk that domestic-only firms don't face.

### The mechanism — political and regulatory environment

**Political stability.** Stable governments produce stable economies, predictable regulations, and durable foreign investment. **In 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggered widespread corporate withdrawal — McDonald's, which opened its first Russian location in 1990, was among the first global companies to cease operations there**, citing both values misalignment with the Putin government and untenable operating conditions.

**Trade regulations.**

- **Tariffs** — taxes on imports. **Japan's tariff rate is among the world's lowest at 2.5% for nonagricultural products.** **Seychelles** averaged 50%+ tariffs before joining the World Trade Organization in 2015.
- **Quotas** — limits on the quantity of imports.
- **Trade blocs** — regional agreements (EU, USMCA, ASEAN) that reduce friction within the bloc.
- **Embargoes** — comprehensive prohibitions on trade with specific countries.

Each of these alters the cost of doing business and may make certain markets non-viable.

### The trade-off — assessment thoroughness vs. time to market

A complete external assessment of a target market takes months or years. A first-mover advantage requires speed. The trade-off: do enough assessment to avoid catastrophic failures, but not so much that you arrive after competitors have established positions.

Firms that do this well usually run a layered approach: a fast assessment of the major risks (political stability, regulatory feasibility, basic infrastructure) before committing; a slower, deeper assessment of cultural and economic nuances after committing but before scaling.

### Common misconceptions

**"Discretionary income is the same as disposable income."** No. Disposable = after taxes. Discretionary = after taxes *and* essentials. The distinction matters when forecasting demand for nonessentials.

**"Currency exchange rates are a finance problem, not a marketing problem."** They're both. A 20% currency move can erase a marketing campaign's profitability without changing anything in the consumer's behavior.

## Concept 3 — Modes of Entry and Marketing Mix Adaptation

### The motivating puzzle

A firm has decided to enter a new global market. It now faces a sequence of choices that don't exist in domestic marketing: how to enter (own operations vs. partnership vs. licensing), what to localize (product, price, place, promotion), and how much to invest before testing the market response.

These choices form the operational decisions that turn the strategic question (*should we enter?*) into actual market activity.

### The mechanism — six modes of entry

Six standard entry modes, ordered by increasing involvement and risk:

**Exporting.** Make the product in one country, sell it in others. Lowest risk; least involvement. **The UK's Northampton county** — home to **Alfred Sargent & Sons, Church's, John Lobb** — saw declining domestic shoemaking businesses revived by exporting traditional English craftsmanship to international consumers.

**Franchising.** The franchisor allows another entity to operate using the franchisor's products, branding, and knowledge in exchange for a fee. **Over 90% of McDonald's globally are franchises owned by independent local businesspeople.** Cost varies enormously — a McDonald's franchise requires **$500,000 in liquid assets, $45,000 in franchise fees, and total expected investment of $1.3M–$2.3M**. By contrast, **Jazzercise franchising costs $1,250 + liability insurance**.

**Licensing.** One organization grants another the right to use its name, brand, or trademark on specific items. Lower commitment than franchising; less control as well. Common in fashion: **Armani** licenses its name to companies that produce eyeglass frames, fragrances, and watches. The brand collects royalties; the licensee bears manufacturing risk.

**Joint venture.** Two or more companies create one enterprise or project. Risk-sharing without permanent merger. **In 2012, Kellogg's and Wilmar International Limited formed a joint venture** giving Kellogg's access to Wilmar's Chinese distribution network in exchange for Wilmar's financial gains.

**Strategic alliance.** Two companies invest resources in a mutually beneficial way without forming a single enterprise. **Microsoft** uses alliances heavily when entering new markets. **Uber and Spotify** entered an alliance allowing Uber riders to stream their Spotify accounts during rides — both firms benefited without merging operations.

**Foreign direct investment (FDI).** The riskiest and most-involved mode: establish full operations in the target country. **Tesla invested in a Shanghai factory** and in 2022 announced plans to **double original output targets to 1 million cars annually**.

### The mechanism — three forms of global firms

How a firm operates across borders also classifies it organizationally:

- **International firm** — any firm operating globally, regardless of intensity.
- **Multinational firm** — centralized global structure with major decisions made at headquarters. **Amazon** (HQ Seattle, operations in 50+ countries with most decisions made centrally) is multinational.
- **Transnational firm** — allows higher localization across markets while maintaining brand coherence. **McDonald's**, with global brand consistency plus locally-tailored menus (Maharaja Mac India, Spicy Shrimp Whopper Japan from Chapter 7), is transnational.

The classification matters because it predicts where strategic decisions get made and how much local responsiveness the firm can offer.

### The mechanism — standardized vs. adapted marketing strategy

Once entry mode is chosen, the marketing-mix adaptation question becomes operational:

**Standardized global marketing** — same marketing strategy in all markets. **Coca-Cola** uses the same brand, name, and identity globally. The advantage: marketing cost is amortized across all markets.

**Adapted global marketing** — marketing strategies differ by market. **Netflix** adapts content libraries by country. The advantage: each market gets locally-relevant marketing; the cost: per-market expense.

### The 4Ps in global context

**Product.** Three options:

- **Straight product extension** — same product everywhere. Simplest. Works when the product is universally relevant (e.g., basic consumer electronics).
- **Product adaptation** — modify the product for local needs. McDonald's vegetarian options in India, Burger King's regional variants from Chapter 7.
- **Product invention** — design entirely new products for the local market.

**Price.** Affected by exchange rates, local purchasing power, tariffs, local production costs vs. import costs, and competitive pricing in the local market. The standard global pricing question: do you set one global price (and absorb currency variation) or set local prices (and accept cross-border arbitrage when prices diverge significantly)?

**Place.** Distribution infrastructure varies. The Belt and Road Initiative (Chapter 7) is reshaping geographic accessibility. Countries with weak infrastructure restrict which place strategies are viable.

**Promotion.** The most culturally-sensitive P. Translation, imagery, humor, claims, regulatory environment all change. The Nivea and Gucci failures were promotion failures.

### The trade-off — entry mode involvement vs. risk exposure

The six entry modes form a spectrum. Lower involvement (exporting, licensing) means lower risk but also lower revenue capture and less local responsiveness. Higher involvement (FDI) means more revenue capture and more local control but higher capital exposure and longer payback periods.

Firms typically progress along the spectrum as they gain confidence in a market — start with exporting, move to a joint venture, eventually invest directly. Skipping steps is possible but raises risk.

### Common misconceptions

**"Franchising is just licensing with a bigger fee."** No. Franchising covers all business operations (the franchisee runs the entire business under the franchisor's system). Licensing covers a specific aspect (typically a trademark or brand name) used on otherwise independent products.

**"Standardized marketing is always cheaper."** It's cheaper to *produce*; it can be more expensive to *recover from* when cultural failures emerge. The Nivea and Gucci cases illustrate that standardized creative work can carry expensive recovery costs.

## Integration — Hofstede's Five Cultural Dimensions

Cultural sensitivity in international marketing has a standard framework: **Geert Hofstede's cultural dimensions**, originally developed in the 1970s through analysis of IBM employees in 50+ countries. Five dimensions:

**Power Distance Index (PDI)** — how much power inequality is accepted by a culture. **High PDI** (e.g., many Asian and Latin American countries) means hierarchy is accepted; marketing should respect status, defer to authority figures. **Low PDI** (Nordic countries) means power is shared; marketing can be more egalitarian. **The US scores 40, world average 55**.

**Individualism vs. Collectivism (IDV)** — whether the culture prioritizes individual goals or group cohesion. The US, UK, and Australia score highly individualist; many East Asian and Latin American cultures score collectivist. Marketing in collectivist cultures emphasizes group benefits, family, community; in individualist cultures, personal achievement, self-expression.

**Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)** — whether the culture values competition / achievement / material success (masculine) or cooperation / quality of life / care for others (feminine). High MAS: Japan, Italy. Low MAS: Sweden, Norway. Marketing tone shifts accordingly.

**Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)** — tolerance for ambiguity. High UAI cultures (Greece, Portugal) prefer rules, predictability, established brands. Low UAI cultures (Singapore, Denmark) tolerate ambiguity and accept new options more readily.

**Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation (LTO)** — whether the culture prioritizes future rewards vs. immediate gratification or tradition. High LTO: East Asian cultures. Low LTO: Western cultures generally. Marketing horizons and customer-relationship investments shift.

A firm using the Hofstede framework asks: *for each dimension, where does the home market sit? Where does the target market sit? What's the gap?* Large gaps signal where cultural friction is likely. Small gaps signal where existing marketing might transfer with minimal adaptation.

The framework is a forecasting tool, not a destiny. It helps the firm anticipate friction; the firm still has to do the localization work.

### Failures by category — what cultural mismatch looks like

The OpenStax source for Chapter 5 captured several international failures (deferred to this chapter for treatment). Three are illustrative:

- **Starbucks failed in Australia** (2000–2008, $105M loss) by underestimating the existing local café culture's strength and the difficulty of building premium brand mindshare from scratch in a market with established alternatives.
- **Coca-Cola and PepsiCo** both struggled when they first entered China, requiring major repositioning to adapt to local taste preferences and distribution structures.
- **7-Eleven failed in Indonesia**, where the chain misread which products and convenience patterns local customers valued.

In each case, the firm's home-market playbook didn't transfer. The fix wasn't more marketing spend — it was understanding what specifically didn't transfer and rebuilding for the local context.

### Ethics — dumping, exploitation, and "made in" labeling

International marketing exposes specific ethical risks:

**Dumping** — selling exports below cost of production (or below domestic prices) to gain market share. Distinct from surplus disposal. Dumping is illegal under WTO rules and often counters domestic firms' viability in target markets.

**Exploitation** — sourcing labor or materials from markets with weak labor protections or environmental regulations to lower costs. Legally permissible in many cases; ethically contested. Brands that source this way and don't disclose it expose themselves to consumer backlash when uncovered (the kind of trust failure documented in Chapter 2's $V = B/P$ ethics section).

**"Made in" labeling and country-of-origin** — claims about where products are produced affect perceived quality and ethical positioning. False or misleading country-of-origin claims violate consumer-protection laws in most markets.

The discipline isn't *avoid all of these risks* — it's *understand which ones the firm is exposed to and decide deliberately how to position*.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do now that you could not do before this chapter:**

You can take any global market-entry question and walk a structured analysis: is the market opportunity real (Concept 1)? Is the firm ready (Concept 2)? Which entry mode and marketing-mix adaptation strategy fit (Concept 3)? Where will cultural friction emerge (Hofstede)? You can also recognize when a firm's home-market playbook is being applied ethnocentrically — and predict where the failure will happen.

**The one idea that matters most from this chapter:**

Crossing a border is not a translation problem. A campaign translated word-for-word almost always misses the cultural meaning the original carried. Effective international marketing recreates the *intent* in the new culture's idiom — and that requires the firm to genuinely understand the new culture, not just speak its language.

**The common mistake to watch for:**

Ethnocentrism — assuming the new market works like the home market. Every cultural failure documented in this chapter (Nivea, Gucci, Starbucks Australia, 7-Eleven Indonesia) traces to ethnocentric assumptions that were never tested before launch. The fix is structural — get cross-cultural perspective into the approval process *before* expensive launches, not after.
