# Chapter 10 — Marketing in a Diverse Marketplace

*A domestic market is not a single market. The discipline of marketing across cultural, ethnic, generational, and identity differences within one country is harder than international marketing in some ways — because the boundaries are invisible.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Distinguish standardization from adaptation as marketing strategies, and explain when diversity marketing functions as a domestic adaptation strategy.
2. Define diversity marketing, multicultural marketing, and sociodemographic marketing, and explain how the three relate.
3. Identify the five key parameters of diversity (cultural / racial / ethnic; gender and sexual identity; generational; religion; disability).
4. Apply the tangible-vs-intangible culture model (Hall's iceberg) to predict where cultural friction will emerge in marketing.
5. Describe the major US multicultural segments — Hispanic, Black, Asian — including population size, growth trajectory, sub-ethnic diversity within each, and identity considerations.
6. Apply sociodemographic marketing to specific groups (LGBTQIA+, generational cohorts, consumers with disabilities).
7. Distinguish stereotypes from segmentation, and tokenism from genuine representation.
8. Evaluate an ethical concern in diversity marketing using the frameworks from this chapter (cultural stereotypes, unconscious bias, tokenism, misuse of language or symbols).

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **segmentation frameworks from Chapter 7** — particularly the four consumer segmentation methods and the ADAMS criteria — and the **international marketing frameworks from Chapter 9** (Hofstede's cultural dimensions; standardized vs. adapted strategy). This chapter applies the same logic to diverse domestic markets, where the segments cluster by culture, ethnicity, generation, gender identity, and ability rather than by country.

## Why This Chapter Matters

The United States is becoming a **plurality nation** by approximately 2050 — meaning no single demographic group will hold a sizeable majority. The non-Hispanic White population will remain the largest single group but will lose its majority position by share. The implication for marketing is structural: a single national campaign aimed at "the average American consumer" describes a person who is becoming statistically rarer every year. Marketing that ignores diversity isn't just ethically problematic — it's strategically expensive, missing growth segments worth hundreds of billions of dollars in unmet demand.

This chapter teaches the discipline of marketing that takes diversity seriously without collapsing into segmentation theater. The frameworks from Chapter 7 (STP) and Chapter 9 (Hofstede) apply, but the inputs become more nuanced. The boundaries between segments are often invisible. The same campaign can land beautifully with one sub-population and offend another. The discipline is research-grounded, culturally informed, and structurally inclusive in the firm's own operations — not a final-stage creative tweak.

By the end of the chapter, you will be able to take any campaign and evaluate whether it (a) authentically addresses diverse audiences, (b) avoids the specific failure modes (stereotyping, tokenism, language errors, cultural symbol misuse), and (c) builds the firm's long-term position with the multicultural majority that the US is becoming.

## Concept 1 — Diversity Marketing as Domestic Adaptation Strategy

### The motivating puzzle

**Procter & Gamble** has practiced diversity marketing longer than most companies have had the term. **In 1961, P&G aired one of the first major US television commercials in Spanish** on a Spanish-language channel that became Univisión, targeting Hispanic consumers. **In 1965, the year the EEOC was formed**, P&G launched its own affirmative action plans across all business units — before the EEOC was fully functional. The same year, the company conducted product research with Hispanic consumers in Miami on its Secret deodorant scent.

Six decades later, P&G debuted **"Widen the Screen"** during the **2021 NAACP Image Awards**. The commercial featured a series of short episodes by Black creators challenging stereotypes and negative attitudes. Oprah Winfrey praised the campaign for its creative opportunities, noting that **Black creators account for only 6 percent of all films made**.

The campaign matters not because it was clever advertising but because of what P&G's Chief Brand Officer **Marc Pritchard** described in a 2021 keynote: P&G's commitment extends to *equitable representation throughout the entire business, including up and down the company's supply chain* — subcontracted agencies, production crews, and media companies all reflecting the market's diverse demographics.

The puzzle: most diversity marketing fails not at the ad level but at the supply-chain level. A campaign designed to reach Black consumers, made by an all-White creative team, working with media companies whose newsrooms don't reflect the population, produces marketing that may technically meet diversity criteria but doesn't meaningfully connect with the audiences it claims to serve. P&G's six-decade arc — from a Spanish-language TV ad in 1961 to supply-chain inclusion in 2021 — is what genuine diversity marketing looks like as a discipline rather than as a campaign.

### The mechanism — standardization vs. adaptation revisited

From Chapter 9: **standardization** = same marketing strategy in all markets; **adaptation** = strategy differs by market. Both are also options for marketing within a single country.

**Standardization.** Apple standardizes its iPhone, iPad, and Mac products globally and within each country. Anywhere you go, the devices look and work the same (with minor power-source variations). The advantage: streamlined production, fast launches, brand recognition.

**Adaptation.** Adjusting work efforts, products, or services to match the specific needs of different consumer groups. **Ford adapts its D4 automobile platform** for chassis design and engine configuration across Ford and Lincoln models — saving money while serving different price points. **Zoom's pandemic adaptation** in 2019–2020 grew its customer base from **10 million to 300 million users in five months** through new data centers, partnership extensions, price/service changes, and platform updates.

**Diversity marketing** is adaptation strategy applied to different cultural and sociodemographic segments within a single market. The marketer adjusts product, price, place, or promotion for each segment's preferences. **Nike's "Nike by You"** shoe-customization service lets consumers change colors and materials — a standardized platform that adapts to ethnic, social, and personal preferences at the customer level.

### The trade-off — adaptation cost vs. inclusion benefit

The persistent tension in diversity marketing is between adaptation cost and inclusion benefit.

**Adaptation cost.** Each adapted campaign requires its own creative production, media buy, and measurement — multiplying costs. Custom products for sub-segments require additional SKUs, inventory complexity, and supply-chain coordination.

**Inclusion benefit.** Adobe's 2019 survey of 2,000+ consumers found **61 percent believed diversity in marketing campaigns was important** and **38 percent were more inclined to trust brands that showed good diversity**. Top Design Firms surveyed hundreds of consumers in late 2020: **67 percent would consider making repeat purchases from companies committed to diversity in marketing**. Heat agency analysis: high diversity scores translated to **83 percent higher consumer preference**.

The trade-off: standardization saves cost; adaptation increases relevance and trust. Diversity marketing becomes a financial decision once you can quantify both sides — and the inclusion-benefit data above suggests that the inclusion side has been historically underestimated.

### Common misconceptions

**"Diversity marketing means putting diverse people in ads."** Surface-level inclusion. Genuine diversity marketing requires understanding the cultural context, language, values, and experiences of the audiences being represented — and ideally including them in the production process.

**"One adapted campaign per ethnic group is enough."** No. Each ethnic group is itself diverse. The Hispanic population includes 20+ countries of origin with very different cultural reference points. Treating any group as monolithic produces stereotyping with diverse-imagery cover.

**"Customer-centric marketing automatically produces diversity."** Only if the customer base being centered is itself diverse. Customer-centric marketing in a firm whose customer research over-represents one demographic produces *that demographic*-centric marketing — while claiming customer-centricity.

## Concept 2 — Multicultural Marketing: Hispanic, Black, Asian Identities

### The motivating puzzle

In **early 2018**, **Tarte Cosmetics** launched its highly anticipated **Shape Tape Foundation**. The launch featured 15 shades — almost all of them light tones. Loyal customers were furious about the lack of shades for darker skin tones. Tarte was accused of being a whitewashed brand and of making deeper-skin-toned customers feel inadequate. The company quickly apologized, changed its product, and revised its marketing communication.

The Tarte case is a single-day social media disaster, but the underlying mechanism is structural. A firm whose product development, customer research, and marketing approval all underrepresent dark-skinned customers will produce a product that excludes them. The fix is not a faster apology process — it's diverse representation in the development pipeline before launch.

### The mechanism — Hispanic identity

The **2020 US Census** counted **63+ million Hispanic people** — **1 in 5 Americans** — representing **23 percent growth since the previous census**. Projections estimate the Hispanic population reaches **roughly 100 million by 2050**.

The Hispanic population is not a single ethnicity. It includes people from **20 different countries**. **Five subethnicities account for 83 percent**: **Mexican (62.2%), Puerto Rican (9.5%), Cuban (3.9%), Salvadoran (3.9%), Dominican (3.5%)**. The remaining 17 percent spreads across 15 other Hispanic subethnicities.

Vocabulary matters. Three terms are often used interchangeably but have different meanings:

- **Hispanic** — language-based; describes individuals from Spanish-speaking countries. A person from **Spain** is Hispanic but not Latino (Spain is in Europe).
- **Latino/Latina** — location-based; describes people whose families originate in Latin America regardless of language. A person from **Brazil** is Latino/Latina but not Hispanic (Brazilians speak Portuguese).
- **Latinx** — gender-neutral version of Latino/Latina. **Many Hispanic people dislike or find the term offensive**, despite its adoption among some communities.

Cultural identity is strongly linked to heritage and family country of origin for most Hispanic people. **Target's "Sobremesa" / #SinTraducción campaign** demonstrates effective Hispanic-identity marketing. The *sobremesa* is the Hispanic tradition of additional time at the dinner table after a meal — relaxing, having meaningful conversations. The word has no direct English translation. Target's campaign tagline: *"There will always be a part of you that simply doesn't translate."* The marketing acknowledged a specific cultural practice rather than translating standard messaging into Spanish.

### The mechanism — Black identity

The **2020 US Census** showed **46.8 million Black people** — **14.2 percent of the population** — up from 38.9 million a decade earlier. Black identification "in combination with another race" increased **88.7 percent** from the previous census, the largest growth among racial categories.

Forecasts from **McKinsey** estimate Black consumers will represent **15–17 percent of total US population growth over the next decade**. The Black population currently accounts for **just under 10 percent of spending** despite being 14.2 percent of population, reflecting income disparities. McKinsey's analysis identifies **over $300 billion per year in unmet demand** in the Black consumer market — a structural opportunity that firms targeting this segment well can capture.

A **2019 Pew Research Center survey** found that **nearly 75 percent of Black people believe race is at the center of their identity**. Effective marketing to Black consumers acknowledges this — not by foregrounding race in every campaign, but by ensuring the campaign reflects authentic Black perspectives in its development and production.

### The mechanism — Asian identity

The Asian American community comprises **22 different countries of origin** with distinct cultural, linguistic, and historical contexts. Treating Asian Americans as monolithic — for example, assuming color preferences are uniform — produces stereotyping.

Color symbolism varies sharply: **Red** is considered a lucky color by many Chinese consumers. **Green** symbolizes youth and vital energy in Japanese culture. **Yellow** is associated with vitality and light in Korean culture. A campaign that uses red prominently to signal "Asian-friendly" assumes Chinese cultural preferences extend to Japanese and Korean audiences — which they don't.

The **acculturation** concept matters across all multicultural segments. *Acculturation* describes how individuals from multicultural backgrounds adapt to and integrate dominant cultural practices while maintaining their cultural identity. A second-generation Mexican-American consumer may speak English at home and Spanish with extended family, prefer one set of media in some contexts and a different set in others, and respond to different marketing approaches at different moments. Acculturation is a continuum, not a binary.

### Hall's iceberg — tangible vs. intangible culture

**Anthropologist Edward T. Hall** described culture as an iceberg with external and internal surfaces. Approximately **90 percent of an iceberg's mass is hidden below the waterline**.

**Tangible / external culture** — the visible 10 percent. Traditions, music, languages, artifacts, clothing, literature. Easy to recognize and reproduce.

**Intangible / internal culture** — the 90 percent below. Thought processes, assumptions, skills, beliefs, interpretations, worldviews. Hard to observe; often the source of marketing failures.

The **Burger King "Texican Whopper"** campaign featured a tiny Mexican wrestler in a poncho-style Mexican flag standing next to a tall American cowboy. Some Spanish-speaking customers found it funny. Others were offended — both by the stereotyping and by the use of the flag (a national symbol that commands respect) as costume. Burger King had to revise the ads. The mistake was below the waterline: the team approved the campaign without understanding the depth of feeling around the flag in the cultures being represented.

### Common misconceptions

**"Translating the campaign into Spanish makes it Hispanic-friendly."** Translation is the tip of the iceberg. The Target Sobremesa campaign worked because it engaged a *cultural concept* without an English equivalent — not because it added Spanish words to standard messaging.

**"All Asian American consumers prefer red branding."** Cultural color symbolism varies dramatically by country of origin. Pan-Asian assumptions are stereotyping with diversity-imagery cover.

**"Black identity marketing has to foreground race."** No. Effective Black-identity marketing reflects authentic Black perspectives in development and production; it does not have to be *about* race to honor it.

## Concept 3 — Sociodemographic Marketing: Generations, Gender Identity, Disability

### The motivating puzzle

A radio advertiser decides to focus exclusively on Millennial and Gen Z listeners, on the assumption that Boomers don't listen to radio anymore. The decision is wrong. **Baby Boomers are active radio listeners**. The bias is structural — driven by an incorrect assumption — and produces a media plan that misses one of the largest spending demographics in the country.

This is sociodemographic marketing's central challenge: the segments are real, but the assumptions about them are often wrong, outdated, or stereotyped. The discipline is using sociodemographic data carefully without collapsing into stereotype.

### The mechanism — sociodemographic marketing defined

**Sociodemographic marketing** combines social variables (identity-based — what people identify with) and demographic variables (more specific and quantifiable — gender, age/generation, religion, marital status, income, employment, education, political affiliation, disability). It is a subcategory of diversity marketing focused on these specific dimensions.

The benefits: pattern detection across larger populations with shared variables in common; reach to historically underrepresented groups; product/service/messaging adaptation that's more effective for both businesses and consumers.

### Marketing to LGBTQIA+ consumers

The **LGBTQIA+** acronym stands for **lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer/questioning, intersex, and asexual**, with the **+** signaling other sexual identities and allies.

Demographic trends:

- In **2012**, **3.5 percent** of Americans self-identified as LGBTQIA+.
- By **2020 (Gallup)**, the figure was **5.6 percent**.
- **One in six adults in Generation Z (born 1997–2012) identifies as LGBTQIA+** — suggesting the share will continue rising as older generations age out and younger ones enter adulthood.

The growth is partly self-identification confidence (greater societal acceptance, easier to find community), partly identity formation (acceptance enables fuller expression), partly political and sociological need for representation.

For marketers, the operational guidance from the source:

1. **Review company values honestly** — does leadership genuinely support the LGBTQIA+ community, or is the support performative?
2. **Establish guidelines** on language, tone, imagery, and messaging.
3. **Find experts** — community members involved in the work.
4. **Test and verify** the marketing is perceived as designed.

**IKEA's #ProgressIsMade campaign** ran during US Pride Month 2021. Rainbow-themed products (carrying bags, audio speaker covers) generated up to **$50,000 in proceeds donated to LGBTQIA+ nonprofit GLSEN**. In Canada, IKEA displayed **10 different concept-art love seats inspired by Pride flags** at select stores. The campaign worked because the brand engaged authentically and channeled commercial activity into supporting nonprofit work — not because it added rainbows to standard marketing.

### Marketing to generational cohorts

The standard cohorts:

- **Silent Generation** (~1928–1945)
- **Baby Boomers** (1946–1964)
- **Generation X** (1965–1980)
- **Millennials** (1981–1996)
- **Generation Z** (1997–2012)
- **Generation Alpha** (2010–2025) — **a Generation Alpha is born every nine seconds in the United States; the cohort is projected to reach 2 billion individuals worldwide by 2025**.

Cohort marketing is useful at the macro level (campaigns can target cohorts at scale) and dangerous at the individual level (within each cohort, individuals vary widely). The most consistent failure mode in generational marketing is *over-applying cohort generalizations to individuals*.

### Marketing to consumers with disabilities

The **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** and similar laws govern accessibility and marketing practices toward consumers with disabilities. Marketing implications:

- **Accessibility in advertising channels** — captions on video, alt text on images, accessible website design.
- **Authentic representation** — including consumers with disabilities in product development and creative work.
- **Avoiding "inspiration porn"** — marketing that frames disability primarily as a source of able-bodied inspiration rather than as a normal part of human experience.

The disability community is large — the source notes consumers with disabilities represent significant purchasing power and are systematically underrepresented in marketing.

### The trade-off — segmentation precision vs. stereotype risk

The sociodemographic frameworks let marketers reach historically excluded groups efficiently — and let marketers stereotype efficiently. The same tool produces different outcomes depending on how it's used.

**Segmentation done well** identifies real preferences and behaviors in a sub-population without assuming individuals share all the traits assigned to the segment.

**Segmentation done as stereotyping** treats segment-level patterns as individual-level certainty, producing campaigns that feel insulting to people who don't fit the assumption.

The discipline distinguishes the two. The fix isn't to avoid sociodemographic targeting — that would mean abandoning the historically excluded groups it can reach effectively — but to do it research-grounded rather than assumption-grounded.

### Common misconceptions

**"All Gen Z consumers are digital natives who don't watch TV."** Gen Z's media consumption is more nuanced than this stereotype. Streaming, social media, gaming, and traditional TV all play roles depending on the individual.

**"Marketing to LGBTQIA+ consumers means rainbow imagery during Pride Month."** Rainbow imagery without genuine commitment is performative — and consumers increasingly recognize and call out the performance. The IKEA case worked because the imagery was paired with sustained commitment (donation to GLSEN, year-round inclusion practices).

**"Disability marketing is a separate niche."** Consumers with disabilities are integrated into every other segment — they're also Hispanic, Black, Asian, LGBTQIA+, multi-generational, etc. Treating disability as a separate niche misses the intersectional reality.

## Integration — Ethics in Diversity Marketing

The frameworks of this chapter are useful instruments. They are also instruments that can be misused. The ethics integration covers four major risk categories.

### Stereotypes and unconscious bias

Marketing campaigns reinforce or challenge cultural and social stereotypes. **P&G's 2014 "Like a Girl" campaign** explicitly challenged the use of "like a girl" as a put-down, transforming it into a statement of strength. The campaign worked because it named the stereotype it was challenging.

Stereotypes are common in advertising because they're efficient — a stereotype lets a 30-second ad communicate a character or context quickly. The cost: stereotypes reinforce assumptions that limit how the audience perceives the represented group.

Unconscious bias is the marketing-team equivalent. A team that doesn't include diverse perspectives produces work that reflects the team's own assumptions — which usually means the dominant culture's assumptions.

### Tokenism

**Tokenism** = including individuals from a minority group or underrepresented community to prevent criticism without genuinely engaging with that group's perspectives, needs, or representation.

Tokenism is symbolic. It looks like representation but doesn't deliver it. Consumers have become better at detecting it — particularly younger consumers, who have grown up surrounded by marketing claims and developed sharper instincts for distinguishing authentic engagement from performance.

The fix isn't to *avoid* representation — that would re-exclude the groups that need representation. The fix is to *do real representation*: include diverse voices in development, production, and approval; ensure the firm's supply chain, leadership, and culture reflect the populations the marketing claims to serve.

### Cultural and language errors

There were **7,139 living languages** reported worldwide in 2021. Twenty-three of those languages account for billions of speakers each. A firm marketing across language boundaries has 23+ major linguistic contexts to handle, plus thousands of smaller ones, plus the cultural meaning that doesn't translate cleanly between any of them.

Common errors:

- **Direct translation that misses idiom.** "9Lives" cat food works in English (cats have nine lives in English idiom). In Spanish and Portuguese idiom, cats have *seven* lives — so the brand name doesn't carry the same meaning.
- **Symbol misuse.** The rainbow flag means "LGBTQIA+ community" in the US; it also resembles the Andean rainbow flag (with two blue stripes) that symbolizes the Inca empire to Peruvian and Andean communities. Using one without distinguishing it from the other can produce unintended cross-cultural meaning.
- **Color symbolism errors.** Red as luck (Chinese), green as youth (Japanese), yellow as vitality (Korean) — each has a different meaning, and pan-Asian color schemes carry assumptions that don't hold.

### Ethics-business connection

The **Sherman Antitrust Act** and related US legislation establish the legal foundation for fair and equitable competition. Ethical marketing in a diverse marketplace extends this principle to fair and equitable representation of diverse audiences.

The connection to Chapter 2's value framework is direct: $V = B/P$ is computed in the customer's head, and customers from diverse backgrounds increasingly factor *the firm's authentic engagement with their community* into the $B$ side of the ratio. Tokenism reduces $B$. Genuine engagement increases it. The data from Concept 1 — 67 percent willing to make repeat purchases from diversity-committed brands; 83 percent higher consumer preference for high-diversity brands — is consumer-side evidence that ethical diversity marketing pays back.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do now that you could not do before this chapter:**

You can take any campaign aimed at diverse audiences and evaluate whether it (a) engages real cultural and sociodemographic depth or just surface tangibles, (b) is built on authentic supply-chain inclusion or theatrical representation, (c) avoids the failure modes of stereotyping, tokenism, language errors, and symbol misuse, and (d) builds long-term position with the multicultural majority that the US is becoming. You can also identify when a firm's marketing is at risk of producing the next Tarte Cosmetics or Burger King "Texican Whopper" moment — and propose what would have prevented it.

**The one idea that matters most from this chapter:**

A domestic market is not a single market. The boundaries between sub-populations are invisible but real, and marketing that ignores them addresses an "average consumer" who is becoming statistically rarer every year. The discipline isn't *avoid all generalizations* — segmentation is a useful tool. The discipline is *do the research-grounded, structurally-inclusive work to use segmentation without collapsing into stereotype*.

**The common mistake to watch for:**

Treating diversity marketing as a creative-stage tweak. The Tarte Cosmetics 15-shades failure happened upstream of any marketing decision; the product development team didn't include diverse perspectives. The supply-chain inclusion P&G describes is the standard fix — diverse representation in development, production, and approval, not just in the final ad.

```
     serving them, or as performing to outsiders?
   - What's the worst-case interpretation, and is it defensible?

3. **A live tokenism audit on a REAL campaign.** Pick a real
   identity-targeted campaign from any brand (Nike's Kaepernick
   ad, Pepsi's Kendall Jenner ad, Bud Light's Dylan Mulvaney
   campaign, an LGBTQ Pride campaign you've seen, a Black History
   Month campaign you've seen, a disability-inclusive campaign
   from any brand). Have the assistant run the tokenism check
   against that campaign and produce a verdict with reasoning.

   Then have the assistant pre-audit any identity-relevant
   marketing your own brand might do — what's the riskiest
   identity move the brand could make in the next 12 months, and
   how would the brand know if it had crossed the line.
```

---

**What this produces:** A system-prompt section + a tokenism check + an audit of a real well-known campaign + a pre-audit for your brand's potential identity moves. The pre-audit is the most operationally useful piece.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For your own project:* The campaign chosen for the audit changes what the assistant learns. Pick one you have an opinion about — agreement or disagreement with the assistant is the most useful calibration.
- *For ChatGPT / Gemini:* Works as written.
- *For Claude Code:* Not the primary tool here.
- *For a Claude Project:* The tokenism check is one of the assistant's most ethically-consequential capabilities. Save the system-prompt insert with care; this is one to refine if any of the test outputs feel hollow.
