# Chapter 1 — Setting the Stage

*Marketing is not a department. It is a way of thinking about how products meet people.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Explain why marketing skills are useful in any career, not only in jobs labeled "marketing."
2. Identify the three units of this textbook and describe what each one teaches.
3. Articulate, in one sentence, what marketing is — and what it isn't — without using the word *advertising*.

## Prerequisites

None. This is the opening chapter.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most students arrive at a first marketing course with a hidden assumption: *marketing is what marketers do, and marketers do advertising.* That assumption is going to make the rest of this book harder than it needs to be.

This is a short chapter — by design. It does one thing: it clears the assumption. Marketing is the work of understanding what customers actually value and using that understanding to make decisions about products, prices, channels, and messages. Every business decision that ends with a customer is a marketing decision, regardless of which department made it. Engineers make marketing decisions when they design the screen. Finance makes marketing decisions when it sets a price. Operations makes marketing decisions when it picks a distribution channel. The job title doesn't determine whether the decision is marketing. The structure of the decision does.

If you are planning to work in finance, engineering, operations, healthcare, public policy, the arts, or anything else, you will still be making marketing decisions. This book teaches you how to make them deliberately.

## The shape of this book

The textbook is divided into three units. Each unit answers a different question.

**Unit 1 — Setting the Stage (Chapters 1–3).** *What is marketing, and how do you point it at something?* This unit defines marketing as the work of building, communicating, and capturing value, introduces the central model — customer value as a ratio of perceived benefits to price — and shows how strategic planning turns the ratio into specific decisions about which markets to serve and how to serve them.

**Unit 2 — Understanding the Marketplace (Chapters 4–10).** *Who are you marketing to, and how do you find out?* This unit splits the audience into consumer markets and business markets, develops the discipline of segmentation (dividing a market into useful pieces), targeting (choosing which pieces to serve), and positioning (establishing how the customer should perceive your offer). It also covers marketing research — how firms gather the information they need — and the special considerations of marketing globally and to a diverse domestic marketplace.

**Unit 3 — Product, Promotion, Price, and Place (Chapters 11–22).** *What levers does the firm pull, and how?* This unit unpacks the four elements of the marketing mix one at a time. Product (Chapters 12–14). Price (Chapter 15). Promotion in all its forms (Chapters 16–19). Place — the channels through which products reach customers (Chapters 20–21). The book closes with sustainable marketing as the emerging paradigm (Chapter 22).

## Why three units?

The order is not arbitrary. Marketing decisions sit at the intersection of three domains: *what value is*, *who values it*, and *how to deliver it*. Get any one wrong and the other two cannot rescue the work.

A firm with a brilliant value proposition but no understanding of its audience builds products no one wants. A firm with a clear understanding of its audience but no instruments for delivering value to them stays a research organization. A firm with sophisticated delivery channels but no underlying value proposition is a logistics company looking for a reason to exist.

Unit 1 builds the foundation — the value ratio, the marketing mix, the strategic planning process. Unit 2 builds the audience layer — segmentation, targeting, positioning, research. Unit 3 builds the delivery layer — the four Ps, in detail, across every channel and every category that matters.

Each unit assumes the previous one. By the end of Unit 1 you will have the vocabulary to read any marketing decision; by the end of Unit 2 you will know how to identify whose value ratio you are trying to move; by the end of Unit 3 you will know which lever to pull and what it costs to pull it.

## What this book is not

A few clarifications, since the framings around marketing are often misleading.

**This is not a course in advertising.** Advertising is one form of one of the four Ps (promotion). The book covers it in detail in Chapters 16–19, but advertising is roughly one-twelfth of the material here. If you want a book on advertising specifically, this is not the right book.

**This is not a course in selling.** Selling, like advertising, lives inside promotion. Personal selling gets a chapter (Chapter 18). The other twenty-one chapters do something else.

**This is not a how-to manual for any specific platform.** The current Meta ad-format playbook will be different by the time you read this book. The current Google Ads bidding modes will be different. The book teaches the underlying mechanisms — how value works, how price signals quality, how channels move products — that hold across platforms and across decades. You can learn the current platform tactics in an afternoon once you understand the mechanisms; the reverse is not true.

**This is not skeptical of marketing as a discipline.** Marketing has a reputation problem in some circles, often well-earned by specific marketers behaving badly. The book treats marketing as serious work that is worth doing well, and it treats unethical marketing as a failure mode worth naming. Both happen in this book, in proportion to how often they happen in the world.

## How to use this book

Each chapter follows the same structure. Learning Objectives at the front (specific, testable competencies). A "Why This Chapter Matters" section that names the chapter's payoff in plain terms. Three concept sections, each with a motivating puzzle, the underlying mechanism, the trade-off the mechanism creates, a worked example, and the common misconceptions students arrive at. An integration section that pulls the three concepts together. Graduated exercises — warm-up, application, synthesis, challenge — that get you using the chapter rather than just having read it. A summary that names the one idea most worth keeping. Connections forward to where this material lands later in the book.

Each chapter also includes inline **Dig Deeper** prompts and a chapter-end **LLM Exercise** designed to be used with Claude (or another large language model) as a thinking partner. Chapter 0 — *Claude Basics* — explains how to use those prompts. If you have not yet read Chapter 0, do so before the LLM Exercises in subsequent chapters.

## Connections Forward

**Chapter 2 — Marketing and Customer Value** introduces the central model of the book — customer value as a ratio, $V = B/P$ — and unpacks the four elements of the marketing mix that move that ratio in the firm's favor. Everything in this textbook either feeds into that ratio or is a refinement of how to act on it.

**Chapter 3 — Strategic Planning in Marketing** turns the value model into actionable decisions: where the firm chooses to compete, which products it invests in, which markets it pursues, which goals it sets, and how it measures whether the strategy is working.

By the end of Chapter 3, you will have the foundations — *what is marketing*, *what is value*, *how does a firm decide where to point its marketing*. The remaining nineteen chapters build on this base.

## LLM Exercise — Chapter 1: Setting the Stage

**Project:** AI Marketing Assistant — a Claude Project the student builds across the semester that ends as a deployed, useful marketing tool for one specific brand or product.
**What you're building this chapter:** the project's foundation — the brand/product context, the assistant's scope, and the initial system prompt for the Claude Project.
**Tool:** **Claude Project.** Create one called "Marketing Assistant — [Brand]" — every later chapter adds capabilities to this same Project.

---

**The Prompt:**

```
I'm building an AI Marketing Assistant as a semester-long project.
The assistant will be a Claude Project I keep adding capabilities to
as my marketing course progresses. By Chapter 22 it should be a real
working tool — not a toy.

I need help setting it up. Walk me through this by asking ONE
question at a time and waiting for my answer. Cover:

1. The brand/product the assistant will serve. One specific
   sentence — "an indie sneaker brand making one model, sold direct
   on a single Shopify storefront," not "a footwear company."
2. The user the assistant is for. Is it for the founder? A solo
   marketer? An external consultant? A student writing about this
   brand? (The user shapes the assistant's tone and what it should
   refuse.)
3. The five most consequential marketing decisions this brand
   faces over the next 12 months. Be specific.
4. The three things the assistant should be GOOD at by Chapter 22
   — the capabilities that matter most for this brand.
5. The three things the assistant should explicitly refuse to do.
   (Examples: generate ad copy without strategy context; recommend
   a TikTok strategy without engagement data; claim expertise on a
   regulatory question.)

After all five answers, write the initial **Project Instructions**
(system prompt) for the Claude Project. Structure:

- One-paragraph description of the brand and its current state.
- Who the assistant serves and their typical questions.
- The assistant's core posture (the book's framing applies:
  skeptical-curious, useful for thinking, never authoritative
  without verification, the customer's view of value over the
  firm's view).
- Three concrete capabilities (placeholders for now — they'll be
  filled in across the semester).
- Three "I won't do that" rules.
- The escalation rule: when the assistant should tell the user
  "you need a human expert here, not me."

End with three TEST PROMPTS I can run against the assistant to
verify it's behaving correctly:
- One that should produce a useful answer.
- One that should trigger a refusal.
- One that should trigger an escalation to a human expert.

Output the Project Instructions as a clean markdown block I can
paste directly into Claude's project-level instructions field, plus
the three test prompts below it.
```

---

**What this produces:** A ready-to-paste Claude Project system prompt + three test prompts to verify the assistant is calibrated correctly. Save the system prompt to your Claude Project's project-level instructions field. Run the three test prompts and document the results in your running notes.

**How to adapt this prompt:**

- *For your own project:* Pick a brand you actually know something about. The assistant gets sharper the more domain knowledge you can supply during setup. A real brand you've watched closely beats a fictional one; a fictional brand in a domain you understand beats either of those if the real-brand option is one you don't actually care about.
- *For ChatGPT / Gemini:* Works as written. Build a Custom GPT instead of a Claude Project; the system prompt goes in the GPT's instructions field.
- *For Claude Code:* Wrong tool — this is a setup conversation, not a code task.
- *For a Claude Project:* The whole project IS a Claude Project. The system prompt you produce here is the project's project-level instructions.

**Connection to previous chapters:** This is the project's opening. Every later chapter adds a capability to this assistant.

**Preview of next chapter:** Chapter 2 adds the assistant's first real capability — value analysis. The assistant will be able to compute V = B/P for any product or feature and identify mismatched marketing-mix levers.
