# Chapter 14 — Services: The Intangible Product

*A service is not a product the customer can take home. The marketing implications cascade from there.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define services and explain why service marketing requires different approaches than product marketing.
2. Classify services as people-based or equipment-based, and identify the three sub-categories of people-based services.
3. Identify the four characteristics of services (intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability) and the marketing implications of each.
4. Walk the service-profit chain model and explain how internal service quality drives external profitability.
5. Apply the Services Marketing Triangle to a real service business.
6. Apply the Gap Model of Service Quality (the five gaps) to diagnose service-quality failures.
7. Distinguish the standard 4Ps from the services-marketing 7Ps (Product / Price / Place / Promotion / People / Process / Physical Evidence).
8. Evaluate an ethics question in services marketing using frontline-employee and customer-trust frameworks.

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with the **Product P** treatment from Chapters 12–13. This chapter completes Unit 3's Product cluster by treating services — which behave differently enough from physical products to need their own marketing frameworks.

## Why This Chapter Matters

The US economy is primarily a service economy. **Service industries contributed roughly 67% of US GDP in 2020, versus only 10.8% for manufacturing.** More than half of the US workforce produces intangibles. Marketing students who understand only physical-product marketing miss most of the modern economy.

Services are also harder to market. A consumer can't take a service for a test drive. The customer is usually part of producing the service (you can't get a haircut while the barber is somewhere else). Service quality varies because humans deliver it. Service capacity perishes — an empty airline seat at takeoff can never be sold.

This chapter teaches the frameworks that let firms market services well — service-profit chains, gap models, the 7Ps extension. By the end, you'll be able to read any service business and identify where its marketing is working and where it's broken.

## Concept 1 — What Services Are, Classified, and What Makes Them Different

### The motivating puzzle

**Sara Bullock** ran a conventional childcare business in Maryland for over a decade. When her family relocated to Tennessee, she opened a new business — **MeTime Drop-In Child Care** — with a different model. Instead of monthly commitments, parents could drop children for one to seven hours as needed.

The original business and the new one offered the same fundamental service (childcare). The marketing implications were entirely different. The drop-in model targeted a different customer segment (parents needing flexibility), priced differently (per-hour rather than monthly), and sold a different value proposition (time flexibility for the parent, not just child supervision). The case has been successful enough that Bullock plans a second location.

The puzzle: even within a single service category, the *form of the service* shapes the marketing strategy more than the underlying service does.

### The mechanism — services defined and classified

**Services** are nonphysical, intangible economic activities. Education is a service. Banking, medical treatment, transportation, insurance — all services. The US economy's heavy weight in services (**67% of GDP** vs. 10.8% manufacturing) makes service marketing a discipline most marketing graduates will use professionally.

Two main classifications:

**People-based services** — humans deliver the service. Three sub-categories:

- **Unskilled labor** — parking lot attendants, babysitters, janitors.
- **Skilled labor** — plumbers, caterers, hairstylists.
- **Professionals** — doctors, attorneys, college professors, accountants.

The classification matters for pricing strategy, training investment, brand positioning, and the legal-regulatory environment (professionals usually carry licensing requirements; unskilled labor doesn't).

**Equipment-based services** — machines or technology deliver the service. Vending machines, ATMs, automated car washes, much of cloud software. The marketing implications differ: equipment-based services face different reliability concerns, different customer-experience design considerations, and different cost structures.

### The mechanism — four characteristics of services

Services share four characteristics that physical products typically don't:

**Intangibility.** Services can't be touched, owned, or stored. A customer can't examine a haircut before getting one or take a course home in a box. Marketing implication: tangible cues that signal service quality become disproportionately important. The reception area of a law firm, the cleanliness of a doctor's office, the design of a SaaS app's interface — all serve as proxy signals for the intangible service.

**Inseparability.** Services are usually produced and consumed simultaneously, with the customer present. You can't get a massage without being in the massage. Marketing implication: customer experience during delivery is the product. A bad interaction with the service-delivery employee isn't a customer service problem after the fact — it's a product defect during the sale.

**Variability (heterogeneity).** Service quality varies because humans deliver services and humans vary. The same hotel restaurant serves a great meal Tuesday and a mediocre one Wednesday. Marketing implication: standardization through training, scripts, processes — and acceptance that some variation is unavoidable. Brands that invest heavily in employee training (Ritz-Carlton, Disney) commercialize consistency itself.

**Perishability.** Service capacity expires. An empty airline seat at takeoff cannot be sold later. An unused hotel room tonight is gone. An empty barber's chair at 2pm is permanent revenue loss. Marketing implication: dynamic pricing (Chapter 15), capacity management, demand smoothing through promotions during low-utilization periods.

### Worked example — Wired Coffee Bar

**Wired Coffee Bar**, founded by Lisa in a fast-growing Tennessee community, illustrates services marketing in concentrated form. The business sold coffee, tea, and locally-baked goods alongside community space — Wi-Fi, varied seating, no drive-through.

The four characteristics show up sharply:

- **Intangibility** — Wired's product is partly the coffee but mostly the *experience*. The cafe ambiance, the community feel, the ability to work or meet there — all intangible. Marketing relied on tangible cues (warm lighting, comfortable seating, friendly staff) to signal the quality of the intangible.
- **Inseparability** — every customer interaction is the product. A short-staffed Saturday morning doesn't just disappoint customers; it produces lost regulars who don't come back.
- **Variability** — barista quality varies day-to-day. Lisa invested in barista training to reduce variability — the same investment Starbucks scaled across thousands of locations.
- **Perishability** — empty seats during a slow Tuesday afternoon can't be re-sold; revenue lost.

Wired's competitive challenge intensified when drive-through coffee shops opened nearby. The competitive question became: does Wired's intangible community-and-third-place value justify its premium relative to drive-through speed? That's the kind of strategic question services marketing forces. The answer depends on segmentation and positioning — for some customers (regulars who treat the cafe as office or social space), Wired wins decisively; for others (commuters in a hurry), the drive-through wins regardless.

### Common misconceptions

**"Service marketing is just product marketing with extra steps."** The four characteristics — intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability — change the marketing problem fundamentally. Frameworks designed for physical products fail or distort when applied to services.

**"Service quality is automatic if employees are nice."** Pleasant employees aren't enough if processes are slow, capacity is mismatched to demand, or customer expectations have been miscalibrated. Service quality is systemic.

## Concept 2 — The Service-Profit Chain and the Services Marketing Triangle

### The motivating puzzle

A coffee shop has rude staff. Customers don't return. Profits decline.

A coffee shop has unhappy staff. Why are they unhappy? Usually because the firm doesn't support them well — schedules are chaotic, equipment breaks, training is thin, management ignores complaints. Unhappy staff produce inconsistent service. Inconsistent service drives away customers. Customers don't return. Profits decline.

The problem appears to be a customer service failure. The cause is upstream, in how the firm treats its own people.

### The mechanism — the service-profit chain

A group of Harvard researchers in the 1990s formalized the link between **internal service quality** and **external profitability** — the **service-profit chain**:

1. **Internal service quality** — the support frontline employees receive from the rest of the organization (good policies, working equipment, proper training, fair compensation).
2. → **Employee satisfaction** — employees who feel supported are more engaged.
3. → **Employee loyalty and productivity** — engaged employees stay longer, work harder, and "go the extra mile."
4. → **Value creation** — employees who go the extra mile produce experiences customers genuinely value.
5. → **Customer satisfaction** — value drives satisfaction.
6. → **Customer loyalty** — satisfied customers come back.
7. → **Profitability and growth** — loyal customers, lower acquisition costs, premium pricing power.

The chain is sequential. Skipping links breaks downstream outcomes. A firm that tries to drive profitability without first ensuring internal service quality usually pushes employees into unsustainable behaviors that erode customer experience over time.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Service-profit chain as a horizontal flow of seven links. Each link labeled with the activity. Annotations showing how breaking any link breaks downstream outcomes. -->

### The mechanism — front-of-house vs. back-of-house

Restaurants make the metaphor concrete. **Front-of-house** employees (hosts, servers) interact with customers. **Back-of-house** employees (cooks, dishwashers, bussers) support the customer interaction without direct contact. The customer experience depends on both. A friendly server is undermined by dirty silverware or undercooked steak.

Internal service quality means back-of-house has what front-of-house needs. The metaphor extends to most service businesses. In a software company, the customer-facing engineers depend on the platform, infrastructure, and operations teams. In a hospital, doctors depend on lab technicians, pharmacy, IT systems, and dozens of support roles. Where these systems work, frontline employees can deliver excellent service. Where they fail, frontline employees absorb the friction.

### The mechanism — the Services Marketing Triangle

A second framework for thinking about services: the **Services Marketing Triangle** with three points (firm, employee, customer) and three connecting marketing types:

- **External marketing** (firm → customer) — the brand promise the firm makes through advertising, PR, and other promotional communications. *"Setting the expectation."*
- **Internal marketing** (firm → employee) — the firm marketing the brand and its values to its own employees. *"Enabling the promise."*
- **Interactive marketing** (employee → customer) — the moment of truth where the service is delivered. *"Delivering the promise."*

All three legs must align. External marketing that promises a level of service the firm hasn't enabled internally produces dissatisfied customers. Internal marketing without external promise loses to competitors. Interactive marketing without internal support fails inconsistently.

### Common misconceptions

**"Customer service is the customer service department."** No. Every employee whose work touches the customer experience is part of customer service. The service-profit chain treats this as systemic.

**"Internal marketing is HR."** Overlap, but distinct. Internal marketing specifically aligns employees with the brand promise external marketing makes. HR may handle recruiting and benefits; internal marketing handles brand-internal communication.

## Concept 3 — The Gap Model of Service Quality

### The motivating puzzle

A hotel guest expects a clean, quiet room with reliable Wi-Fi. The actual room is reasonably clean, somewhat quiet, with intermittent Wi-Fi. The guest is dissatisfied.

Why? At one or more places between *what the customer expects* and *what the customer perceives*, a gap opened. The Gap Model of Service Quality identifies five places these gaps emerge.

### The mechanism — five gaps

Proposed in 1985, the model identifies five places service quality fails:

**Gap 1 — Knowledge gap.** Difference between what customers expect and what management *thinks* they expect. A hotel manager assumes guests want a hot breakfast; guests actually care more about room cleanliness and Wi-Fi. The cause: inadequate market research, weak feedback loops, executives disconnected from customer reality.

**Gap 2 — Policy gap.** Difference between management's understanding of customer needs and how it translates into service delivery policies. Management knows the phone shouldn't ring 10 times; the policy says "answer quickly," which doesn't actually specify a measurable standard.

**Gap 3 — Delivery gap.** Difference between specified service standards and actual service delivery. The policy says answer in 3 rings; the front desk averages 7 rings. Causes: inadequate staffing, poor training, equipment failures, employee burnout.

**Gap 4 — Communication gap.** Difference between what's actually delivered and what's communicated to customers externally. Marketing promises 24-hour room service; the hotel actually offers it 6am–10pm. The communication oversells.

**Gap 5 — Customer gap.** Difference between customer expectations and customer perceptions of the experience. Influenced by all four gaps above. The customer's takeaway is the cumulative result.

<!-- → DIAGRAM: Gap Model with five labeled gaps. Customer at center; expectation and perception arcs around them. Management, employees, and external marketing as the three sources where gaps form. -->

### Worked example — Corporate Medical Services (CMS)

**Corporate Medical Services (CMS)** — the Tennessee-based drug and alcohol testing firm introduced in Chapter 6 — illustrates effective service-quality management. CMS's value proposition is concierge-level personal service to the trucking industry, where testing is often needed urgently.

CMS's promises map cleanly onto the gap model's prevention strategy:

- **Unrivaled experience** (closing Gap 1: management understanding of customer needs based on 25+ years and 15,000+ companies served).
- **Concierge level personal service** (closing Gap 2: explicit translation of "service" into specific behavioral commitments).
- **More security, less hassle** (closing Gap 3: developed processes, procedures, performance benchmarks for compliance).
- **Speed of technology** (closing Gap 4: explicit performance promise — *"7-minute turnaround on negative reports once released from the lab"*).
- **Respect** (operating standard that bounds Gap 5 by ensuring the customer perception is consistently positive).

The pattern: each promise is paired with operational specifics that close one of the gaps. Promises without operational backing produce gap failures. Operational excellence without communicated promises wastes the firm's marketing leverage.

### Common misconceptions

**"Customer satisfaction is about the moment of delivery."** Partly. The Gap Model shows that satisfaction depends on the entire chain — what the customer expected, what management understood, what policies were set, what was delivered, what was communicated. Fixing only Gap 3 (delivery) without addressing Gaps 1, 2, or 4 produces inconsistent results.

**"Higher prices reduce expectations."** Often the reverse. Higher prices increase expectations and widen Gap 5 unless service delivery improves correspondingly.

## Integration — The 7Ps Extension and Services Ethics

### From 4Ps to 7Ps

Services marketing extends the standard 4Ps with three additional Ps to handle the four service characteristics:

| Standard 4Ps | + Services 3Ps |
|---|---|
| Product | People |
| Price | Process |
| Place | Physical Evidence |
| Promotion | |

- **People** — the front-of-house employees who deliver the service. The service-profit chain implies that the people are part of the marketing mix; you can't separate the employee from the service experience.
- **Process** — the workflows that produce the service consistently. Standardized processes reduce variability (the third service characteristic). McDonald's standardized burger-making is a process choice.
- **Physical evidence** — the tangible cues that signal service quality. The reception area, the website design, the receipts and packaging, the uniform — all serve as proxies for the intangible service.

The 7Ps extension is not a different framework from the 4Ps; it's the 4Ps plus three additional levers that service businesses need to pull deliberately.

### Services ethics

**Mintel research** referenced earlier in this book: **over half of US consumers stop buying from companies they perceive as unethical**. For services — where the relationship with the firm is often ongoing — the ethics expectation is even higher.

**AT&T's 2019 "Just OK is not OK" campaign** featured commercials of professionals who don't quite "make the grade" — a surgeon "almost reinstated," a tax professional whose "okay" is just okay. The catchphrase: *"Just OK is not OK."* The campaign positioned AT&T as the antithesis of just-okay service, but the broader frame applies to all service ethics — the standard for service trust is well above the regulatory floor.

**Royal Caribbean Group** illustrates institutional commitment to ethics. The firm's **Code of Business Conduct and Ethics** connects core values (fairness, integrity, honesty, trustworthiness) to actions. Chairman and CEO Richard Fain's framing: *"Simply complying with the law is not enough; we need to be ABC (Above and Beyond Compliance)."* The firm operates an **ethics hotline** managed by a third-party provider, available 24/7 for anonymous reporting.

The discipline: services ethics aren't separate from services marketing. They're the foundation that makes the brand promise credible.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do:** Read any service business and identify what makes it different from a product business, where its quality fails, and how to fix it.

**The one idea:** Services marketing is not product marketing with extra steps. The four characteristics — intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability — change the marketing problem fundamentally and require their own frameworks.

**The common mistake:** Treating service quality as a customer-service problem. It's a systemic problem — internal service quality, employee satisfaction, process design, communication alignment — and the customer-facing symptom is downstream of upstream causes.

**The Feynman test:** Pick a service experience you had recently. In two minutes, can you describe its four-characteristics handling, its service-profit chain, the active gaps, and what would change with a 7Ps redesign? If yes, you have internalized the chapter.
