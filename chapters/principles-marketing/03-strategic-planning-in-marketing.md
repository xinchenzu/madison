# Chapter 3 — Strategic Planning in Marketing

*Strategy is the discipline of pointing the levers at something specific. Most plans fail not because the levers are wrong, but because they were never pointed.*

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. Define strategy precisely as a sequence of choices about where to compete, with whom, and how — not as a list of activities or a mission statement.
2. Distinguish corporate-level, business-level, and functional strategy, and explain why a multi-product firm needs all three.
3. Write a vision statement, a mission statement, and SMART objectives that survive the test of being specific enough to act on.
4. Apply the BCG matrix to a portfolio of products and explain what its quadrants do and do not predict.
5. Conduct a SWOT analysis that distinguishes internal factors from external factors and treats it as a scan, not a worksheet.
6. Compare the four core marketing strategies — market penetration, product development, market development, and product diversification — and identify which one a given firm is pursuing.
7. Decompose a marketing plan into its component sections and explain what each section is for.
8. Choose a defensible set of marketing KPIs from a longer list of possible metrics, and explain the difference between a metric and a KPI.

## Prerequisites

You should be familiar with **Chapter 2's** central model — customer value as a ratio, $V = B/P$ — the four Ps of the marketing mix, and the three nested environments (internal, microenvironment, macroenvironment). This chapter takes those tools and asks the next question: *given that you have them, where do you point them?*

Comfort with one piece of arithmetic is helpful: percent change as $(\text{new} / \text{old}) - 1$. The chapter computes a 28.57 percent revenue growth rate and a customer lifetime value across four customer segments. If percentages feel rusty, ask an LLM to walk you through year-over-year growth before continuing.

## Why This Chapter Matters

Chapter 2 ends with the customer holding a value ratio in their head and the firm holding four levers in its hands. The chapter does not say *which customer* and does not say *which way to pull which lever*. Those are strategic questions, and they are the most consequential decisions a firm makes. A firm with a brilliant marketing mix pointed at the wrong segment loses to a firm with a mediocre mix pointed at the right one.

Strategy is the discipline of pointing. It is also the discipline that most firms do badly — not because the frameworks are hard, but because the frameworks are easy to fill in without committing to a real choice. A vision statement that could belong to any company in the industry is not a vision statement; it is decoration. A SWOT analysis that lists everything as both a strength and a threat hasn't done the work of choosing. A marketing plan that doesn't specify whose money is being spent on what, by when, and how success will be measured isn't a plan; it's a wish list.

This chapter teaches the frameworks — vision, mission, gap analysis, SWOT, BCG, the four growth strategies, the marketing plan, KPIs — and the discipline that makes them work: each framework is a *forcing function for choice*, not a checklist for completeness. A SWOT that doesn't lead to a decision is the wrong SWOT. A KPI dashboard that doesn't change behavior is the wrong dashboard. A plan that doesn't constrain options is the wrong plan.

What you should be able to do after this chapter: pick up any company's strategic documents and tell, in five minutes, whether the strategy is real or theatre.

## Concept 1 — Strategy is a sequence of choices, not a list of activities

### The motivating puzzle

Frito-Lay — the producer of Doritos, Lay's, and Cheetos — is a $13 billion business division of PepsiCo. If you walk into a snack aisle in any month of any year, you will find new flavors. Dill pickle. Chesapeake Bay Crab Spice. Chile Limón. The intuition most people have about how those flavors get developed is that food scientists and chefs in PepsiCo R&D labs run experiments and pick the winners.

That intuition is wrong, in an instructive way.

Frito-Lay crowdsources its new flavors through a contest called **"Do Us a Flavor"** — customers submit ideas, the company shortlists three, and the winners earn cash prizes and shelf space. The "Turn Up the Flavor" campaign, run as a related extension, paired the contest with three limited-time flavors inspired by music genres (hip-hop, pop, rock), partnered with singer-songwriter Bebe Rexha to write a theme song, and printed unique codes on chip packets that buyers could redeem to unlock Rexha's new tracks.

Now ask the strategic question: *why would a $13 billion brand outsource a core product decision to its own customers?*

The answer is not that Frito-Lay's R&D lab is incompetent. It isn't. The answer is that in the snack-food category — a mature market with thin margins and well-defended shelf space — the rate-limiting factor on growth is not flavor quality. It is *customer engagement*. New flavors developed in a lab compete on taste; new flavors developed by customers compete on taste *and* on the social-and-psychological value (Chapter 2) of the customer who submitted them seeing their idea on a real bag in a real store. The contest doesn't just produce flavors; it produces a story the customer tells about themselves. That story is a marketing asset that no amount of internal R&D could buy.

This is what strategy looks like when it's working: a specific choice (crowdsource, don't lab-develop) that responds to a specific market reality (mature category, engagement is the constraint) and produces a specific outcome (flavor pipeline plus brand affinity plus earned media) that competitors who chose differently cannot easily match.

### The mechanism — strategy as a chain of progressively concrete choices

Strategy at any organization moves through five steps, each more concrete than the one before:

**Step 1 — Vision.** Where does the organization want to be? The vision statement is forward-looking and aspirational. Examples from the source material:

- **Amazon:** *"To be earth's most customer-centric company; to build a place where people can come to find and discover anything they might want to buy online."*
- **Volkswagen:** *"To make this world a mobile, sustainable place with access to all the citizens."*
- **Fujitsu:** *"Understanding you better — serving you best."*

The Amazon vision is doing more work than the Fujitsu vision. *"To be earth's most customer-centric company"* is specific enough to constrain decisions — Amazon executives, when faced with a choice between optimizing for the customer or for the warehouse, are now obligated to optimize for the customer or to abandon the vision. *"Understanding you better — serving you best"* is so generic it could belong to any company that has heard of customers.

A vision statement that doesn't constrain decisions is not earning its place in the strategy.

**Step 2 — Mission.** Why does the organization exist, and how does it serve customers? The mission statement is more concrete than the vision; it answers *what does the company do, for whom, and what differentiates it.* Two flavors:

- **Customer-oriented missions** define the firm by the customer need it solves. **IKEA:** *"To offer a wide range of well-designed, functional home furnishing products at prices so low that as many people as possible will be able to afford them."* **Netflix:** *"To entertain the world."*
- **Product-oriented missions** define the firm by what it makes. **Genentech:** *"To develop drugs to address significant unmet medical needs."* **eBay:** *"To be the world's favorite destination for discovering great value and unique selection."*

Customer-oriented missions tend to be more durable because customer needs change more slowly than products do. Theodore Levitt's 1960 essay **Marketing Myopia** (the same essay that named the railroads' failure to see themselves as transportation rather than railroads) is the canonical argument here: a firm that defines itself by its product locks itself out of the new product its customer's evolving need will eventually require.

**Step 3 — Gap analysis.** Where is the organization now, where does it want to be, and what is stopping it from getting there? The gap analysis is the diagnostic step. Four sub-steps:

1. Identify the current state. *Market share is growing 5 percent per year.*
2. Identify the desired state. *We want market share growth of 25 percent over the next three years.*
3. Identify the gap. *Why aren't we growing faster? A new lower-priced competitor entered the market last year. Our advertising hasn't refreshed in 18 months. Production costs have risen and pricing hasn't.*
4. Devise improvements. *What specific actions close the gap, and what does each cost?*

**Step 4 — Establish goals and objectives.** Goals are broad, long-term outcomes (3–5 years). Objectives are specific, short-term action targets (12 months or less). Both should be **SMART** — **S**pecific, **M**easurable, **A**ttainable, **R**ealistic, **T**ime-bound.

**Step 5 — Monitor progress.** Goals and objectives are reviewed continuously through marketing dashboards (covered in Concept 3) and adjusted when the underlying environment shifts.

### The trade-off — clarity versus flexibility

Each layer of strategy trades clarity for flexibility, and the trade-off is sharper than it looks.

A vision statement specific enough to constrain decisions ("earth's most customer-centric company") will eventually constrain a decision the firm wishes it could make. Amazon executives who wanted to optimize for warehouse efficiency in the early 2010s could not, because the vision had been written.

A vision statement vague enough to allow any decision ("understanding you better — serving you best") will not produce alignment when the executives disagree. Two managers can both honor that vision while making opposite choices.

The same trade-off exists at every layer. A SMART objective ("Generate $1.7 million in sales in the health care industry by the end of FY2025") is easy to measure and easy to fail. A non-SMART objective ("Grow our health care presence") is impossible to fail and impossible to learn from.

Firms that win sustainably tend to err on the side of clarity. They commit to specific objectives, fail some of them, and learn from the failures. Firms that err on the side of flexibility tend to grow but never to win — they survive every year without ever moving decisively in any direction.

### Worked example — making a goal SMART

Take a goal you understand: "Improve my GPA."

This is a goal, technically. It satisfies the requirement that a goal name a desired outcome. But it fails every SMART criterion.

**Specific?** No. By how many points? Across all classes or specific ones?
**Measurable?** Implicitly, yes — GPA is a number — but the threshold for success is undefined. Improvement of 0.1 counts? 0.5? A full point?
**Attainable / Realistic?** Cannot tell, because the target is unspecified.
**Time-bound?** No. Improve by when?

Now make it SMART: **"Improve my current GPA from 2.8 to 3.5 by the end of the spring semester."**

Specific (what — current GPA, target GPA). Measurable (the GPA at semester end is a number you can read). Attainable and Realistic (depends on context — if it's mid-April and you're failing your current classes, it's not; if it's the start of the semester with all A-track grading possibilities, it is). Time-bound (end of spring semester).

The interesting move is what happens after you write the SMART version. The original goal was unfailable — you could improve your GPA by 0.01 and claim success. The SMART version forces you to confront, immediately, whether 3.5 by semester end is achievable. If it isn't, you have to revise the target before the semester starts, not at the end. *That confrontation* is the value of SMART. The acronym is forcing you to be specific enough to be wrong.

### Common misconceptions

**"Strategy is the document."** Most strategy documents in most companies sit in shared drives and never affect a decision. The document is the artifact, not the strategy. The strategy is the pattern of decisions the document is supposed to govern. If a firm's pricing decisions, hiring decisions, and channel decisions don't trace to its written strategy, the firm has a strategy document and no strategy.

**"Vision and mission are the same thing."** They aren't. Vision is where you're going (aspirational). Mission is why you exist and how you serve customers (operational). A firm that conflates them ends up with a vague aspiration that doesn't constrain operations or a tactical mission that doesn't motivate.

**"SBUs are for big companies."** A *strategic business unit* is any operating unit with its own strategic plan. Procter & Gamble has five formal SBUs (baby/feminine/family care, beauty, health care, grooming, fabric and home care). But a small business with two distinct customer segments and two distinct product lines is also running two implicit SBUs — and would benefit from making them explicit. The framework scales down better than its corporate origins suggest.

## Concept 2 — Portfolio strategy: when you have multiple products, you have multiple strategies

### The motivating puzzle

Apple sells phones, tablets, laptops, watches, headphones, services, and historically, music players. Each product line operates in a different market, against different competitors, at a different stage of growth, with different margins. Apple's strategic decisions about the iPhone bear almost no resemblance to its strategic decisions about the iPod.

In Apple's most recent reported full year before this textbook's source date, **the iPhone produced 52 percent of Apple's revenue — $192 billion in 2021 alone**. The **iPad held 31.5 percent of the global tablet market** in the first quarter of 2022 (down from 38 percent the previous quarter). The **MacBook held 15.3 percent** of the laptop market. The **Apple Watch led the smartwatch category**, which grew 13 percent in Q1 2022. And **the iPod Touch was discontinued in May 2022**, with remaining inventory sold through.

If you applied the same strategy to all five products — same investment, same growth target, same marketing mix — you would over-invest in the iPod (which the company is exiting) and under-invest in the iPhone (which is funding the rest of the company). Different products need different strategies.

The portfolio question is: *how does a firm decide which products to invest in, which to milk, which to question, and which to retire?*

### The mechanism — the BCG matrix

The Boston Consulting Group developed a 2×2 matrix that classifies products on two axes:

- **Vertical axis:** market growth rate (high or low).
- **Horizontal axis:** the firm's market share within the category (high or low).

The four quadrants:

- **Stars** — high market share in a high-growth market. Apple's iPhone. Invest aggressively; these are the firm's future.
- **Cash cows** — high market share in a low-growth market. Apple's iPad and MacBook. Generate cash to fund stars and question marks; require minimal new investment.
- **Question marks** (sometimes "problem children") — low market share in a high-growth market. Apple's Watch fits here, on the source material's reading. The market is growing, but the firm's share is low; the question is whether to invest more (to convert to a star) or to divest. Both answers are defensible; deferring the decision is not.
- **Dogs** — low market share in a low-growth market. Apple's iPod. Divest, harvest, or kill. Capital tied up here earns nothing the firm could earn elsewhere.

### The trade-off — the matrix is binary; the world is gradient

Every framework that simplifies the world by classifying it loses information. The BCG matrix collapses two continuous variables (share, growth) into four boxes. The cost of the simplification is that the boundaries are arbitrary — a product on the line between cash cow and dog is treated identically to one safely inside the dog quadrant, even though the strategic implications could be wildly different.

The matrix also assumes that the right action is determined by the quadrant. It often is, but not always. A "dog" with a small but loyal customer base may be the right product to keep — at minimal investment — because losing it would hurt the firm's relationship with a segment that buys other products. A "star" in a market about to be disrupted by a new technology may be the wrong place to invest, even though the matrix says invest.

The discipline the matrix forces — *make a deliberate decision about each product line based on share and growth, not on attachment or habit* — is more valuable than the specific recommendations the matrix produces.

### A second 2×2 — SWOT

A different 2×2 looks at the firm itself rather than its product portfolio. **SWOT** examines:

- **Strengths** — internal, positive. What the firm does well.
- **Weaknesses** — internal, negative. Where the firm is vulnerable.
- **Opportunities** — external, positive. What the environment makes possible.
- **Threats** — external, negative. What the environment endangers.

Source material's worked example for **Starbucks**:

- **Strengths:** strong brand image, solid financial performance, impressive store growth, extensive international supply chain.
- **Weaknesses:** high prices versus competition, imitability of products.
- **Opportunities:** expansion in developing markets, coffee subscription service (à la Panera Bread), new product / holiday flavor introductions.
- **Threats:** competition from lower-cost coffee sellers, tightening discretionary spending due to inflation, rising coffee bean prices.

### Common misconceptions

**"The dog quadrant means kill it."** Often, but not always. Some dogs serve a strategic role that the matrix doesn't see — keeping a relationship with a customer segment, blocking a competitor from a beachhead, sustaining a brand that signals quality across the rest of the line.

**"BCG predicts the future."** It doesn't. BCG describes the current state. The classification depends on share and growth *as they are*, not as they will be. A star can become a dog in five years if the technology is disrupted; a question mark can become a star if the firm invests well. The matrix is a snapshot, not a forecast.

**"SWOT is a brainstorm."** A SWOT that lists everything in every quadrant has not done the work. A useful SWOT *prioritizes within each quadrant* — which strengths matter most, which weaknesses are most exposed, which opportunities are most reachable, which threats are most pressing. Then it pairs strengths against opportunities (where can we win?) and weaknesses against threats (where are we exposed?). A SWOT that just lists is not yet a SWOT.

### The four growth strategies — what to do with the analysis

Once a firm has classified its products (BCG) and assessed itself (SWOT), it picks a growth strategy. There are four canonical options:

**Market penetration** — grow share in existing markets with existing products. Mature category, defended shelf space. **Procter & Gamble spent $4.7 billion on advertising in 2020** to defend market share in categories like detergent and personal care. Penetration is expensive and slow; the alternative is losing share, which is also expensive.

**Product development** — sell new or improved products to existing markets. **Tide laundry detergent has had over 50 formulation changes in 40 years**, marketed as "new and improved" with each release. The brand stays; the product evolves. Auto manufacturers run the same playbook on a longer cycle, refreshing models every few years.

**Market development** — sell existing products to new markets (geographic, demographic, channel). **Facebook started as a Harvard headshots platform**, expanded to other colleges, then to the public, then internationally — and is now the largest social network in the world with nearly 3 billion users. Each step was a market-development decision.

**Product diversification** — sell new products to new markets. The riskiest strategy because both variables shift simultaneously. Three sub-types:

- **Concentric diversification** — new products related to the existing line. A book publisher adding e-books and audiobooks to print.
- **Horizontal diversification** — new, unrelated products to existing customers. A toothbrush company adding oral irrigators to the same dental customers.
- **Conglomerate diversification** — new products to new markets. **General Electric** started as a lighting business and now makes medical devices, household appliances, aircraft engines, and financial services.

The four-strategy frame is a *forcing question for any growth initiative*: *which of these four are we doing?* If a firm cannot answer that question for a given initiative, the firm is doing something else — usually expanding without strategic intent, which is more expensive than choosing.

## Concept 3 — The marketing plan and the discipline of measurement

### The motivating puzzle

Imagine your firm reports 10 percent sales growth this year. The marketing dashboard glows green. The team celebrates.

Now imagine you learn the *category* grew 20 percent and your *largest competitor grew 25 percent*. The same 10 percent now reads differently. You didn't grow; you lost share.

The number that matters is rarely the number you reach for first. The discipline of strategy at the operational level is the discipline of choosing — and tracking — the right numbers.

### The mechanism — marketing plans and KPIs

A **marketing plan** is the playbook that translates strategy into specific actions. The OpenStax source material lists 12 elements. We can compress to eight that do most of the work:

1. **Executive summary** — a one-to-two-page synopsis. Written last (you can't summarize what you haven't drafted), read first.
2. **Mission statement** — copied from the strategic plan; if the marketing plan diverges from the mission, one of them is wrong.
3. **SWOT analysis** — the firm's strategic situation at this moment.
4. **Objectives** — SMART, with named owners.
5. **Market segmentation, target market, buyer personas, and positioning** — *whose* value ratio is the plan trying to move? Buyer personas (semi-fictional but data-grounded representations of the ideal customer) are the discipline of forcing this from "our customer" to a specific imagined human. Most plans benefit from 3–8 personas.
6. **Marketing strategy** — the specific moves on the four Ps. Product strategy, pricing strategy, promotion strategy, distribution strategy.
7. **Action programs** — *what specifically gets done, by whom, by when, at what cost*. Without this, the plan is theatre.
8. **Budget and controls** — projected P&L plus the metrics that will tell you whether the plan is working.

A plan with the first six elements but not the last two is a marketing essay. A plan with all eight is a marketing plan.

### The trade-off — too few KPIs hides problems; too many obscures them

**Marketing metrics** measure *anything* a marketer can measure. **Key performance indicators (KPIs)** are the subset that tie directly to business objectives, with targets and time frames. Every KPI is a metric; not every metric is a KPI.

The trade-off in choosing KPIs is structural. Too few KPIs miss problems — a firm that tracks only revenue can be losing market share, churning customers, and burning loyalty without seeing it. Too many KPIs *also* miss problems — a dashboard with 200 indicators is a spreadsheet no one reads, and the four indicators that matter get lost in the noise.

The discipline is *choosing five to ten*, tying each to a specific objective, and ignoring the rest until they become relevant. The right five to ten depend on the firm's stage, category, and strategy. A startup tracks customer acquisition cost (CAC) obsessively; a mature firm tracks customer lifetime value (CLV) and churn. The metrics aren't different; the prioritization is.

### Worked example #1 — revenue growth

Revenue growth is calculated as a year-over-year percentage change:

$$\text{Revenue Growth \%} = \left( \frac{\text{This Year's Revenue}}{\text{Last Year's Revenue}} - 1 \right) \times 100$$

If 2022 revenue is $1.8 billion and 2021 revenue is $1.4 billion:

$$\frac{1.8}{1.4} = 1.2857$$

$$1.2857 - 1 = 0.2857$$

$$0.2857 \times 100 = 28.57\%$$

The firm grew 28.57 percent year over year.

This is the simplest KPI in the book and is wrong by itself in the way the motivating puzzle showed. 28.57 percent is impressive in a flat market and unimpressive in a market growing 50 percent. The KPI to pair it with is **relative market share** — the firm's market share divided by the leading competitor's. If the firm has 5 percent share and the leading competitor has 8 percent, relative market share is $5\% \div 8\% = 0.625$, or 62.5 percent. A relative market share below 1.0 means you are not the leader; how far below tells you how much catching-up you have to do.

### Worked example #2 — Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

CLV is the forward-looking estimate of how much profit a customer will generate over their entire relationship with the firm. The simplified formula:

$$\text{CLV} = (\text{Annual Profit per Customer} \times \text{Average Years Retained}) - \text{Customer Acquisition Cost}$$

Now apply it to four hypothetical segments:

| Segment | Annual Profit | Years Retained | CAC | CLV |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Millennial parents | $10,000 | 12 | $750 | **$119,250** |
| Millennial singles | $18,000 | 12 | $750 | **$215,250** |
| Working moms | $22,000 | 8 | $1,200 | **$174,800** |
| Gen Z emerging professionals | $34,000 | 2 | $1,600 | **$66,400** |

If you were tasked with maximizing CLV, you would prioritize Millennial singles ($215,250). Working moms come second. Gen Z professionals — despite the highest annual profit per customer — produce the lowest CLV because they are retained for only two years.

Now ask the strategic question: *what changes that conclusion?*

Suppose the customer experience team can extend Gen Z retention from 2 years to 8 years. Recompute:

$$\text{CLV}_{\text{Gen Z (revised)}} = (\$34{,}000 \times 8) - \$1{,}600 = \$272{,}000 - \$1{,}600 = \$270{,}400$$

Now Gen Z has the highest CLV. The 6-year retention extension is worth $204,000 per customer.

This is the kind of calculation that separates strategic firms from tactical ones. A firm that tracks only annual profit per customer would over-invest in Gen Z based on top-line numbers. A firm that tracks CLV correctly would notice that retention is the lever that matters most for that segment, and would invest in retention rather than acquisition.

### Common misconceptions

**"More metrics is better."** A dashboard with 50 indicators is a wall of decoration. The firm acts on 5–10. Choose them deliberately.

**"KPIs replace judgment."** They don't. KPIs flag where to look. Judgment decides what to do about it. A revenue-growth KPI hitting target is not evidence that the strategy is working; it could mean the strategy is working *or* that the macro environment is favorable *or* that the metric is gameable. KPIs are a starting point for inquiry, not a destination.

**"Growth equals success."** Sometimes. If the market is growing 20 percent and your firm is growing 10 percent, growth is failure dressed as success. Always pair an absolute KPI (revenue growth) with a relative KPI (market share, relative market share) before celebrating.

## Integration — Strategy is where Chapter 2's tools first get pointed somewhere

Chapter 2 gave you the value ratio and the four levers. This chapter gives you the discipline of pointing them.

The five-step strategic planning process — vision, mission, gap analysis, objectives, monitor — maps onto the five-step marketing process from Chapter 2 (understand → strategize → deliver → grow relationships → capture value). The two loops are the same loop at different altitudes. The strategic loop runs annually or every few years; the marketing loop runs continuously. They reinforce each other when they're aligned and contradict each other when they aren't.

The portfolio frame (BCG) and the situational frame (SWOT) point the firm at *which products* to push and *what posture* to take. The four growth strategies (penetration / product development / market development / diversification) name *what kind of growth* the firm is trying to produce. The marketing plan turns those choices into action programs and budget. KPIs then measure whether the action programs are working.

### A note on ethics

Strategy without ethics produces concrete failures, not abstract ones. The source material names two:

**E-cigarettes and Juul.** As of 2021, approximately 6 percent of US adults reported using e-cigarettes weekly, despite mounting health warnings about vaping. Marketing to that population — particularly to younger demographics — is a strategic choice with ethical content. The Juul case is the cautionary case in the source: aggressive growth strategy met regulatory and public-health pushback that the firm was unprepared for.

**Toyota's accelerator scandal.** During the 2000s, Toyota vehicles experienced unintended-acceleration problems that produced **over 6,200 complaints to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration** and were linked to **more than 89 deaths over five years**. Toyota initially blamed driver error, then issued recalls for floor mats, and ultimately concealed a flawed gas-pedal design while lying to regulators, Congress, and the public. The Justice Department imposed a **$1.2 billion criminal fine** — the largest of its kind in US automotive history — finding that Toyota's effort to protect its corporate image directly led to preventable deaths.

The strategic mistake at Toyota wasn't insufficient marketing. It was *prioritizing brand image over customer safety*, which is a strategic choice — made implicitly, never written into a vision statement, but consistently honored in operational decisions until it became a $1.2 billion scandal and lost lives.

The customer side of this is shifting. **Millennials (born 1981–1996) and Gen Z (born 1997–2012) make up roughly 65 percent of the world's population**, and they buy differently. **A First Insight report found that 62 percent of Millennials and Gen Z are willing to pay more for sustainable products, compared to 54 percent of Gen X and 39 percent of Boomers.** **Nielsen's survey of 30,000+ consumers across 60 countries found 66 percent of global consumers willing to pay more for sustainable products; 73 percent of Millennials specifically.**

Ethics, in other words, is increasingly a strategic variable in its own right. **Fortune Global 500 firms now spend approximately $20 billion per year on corporate social responsibility (CSR) initiatives**, not because they are charitable but because the math of $V = B/P$ has shifted in CSR's favor. Younger customers' $B$ now includes a measurable sustainability and ethics component. A strategic plan that ignores that variable is no longer a complete plan.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1. Spot the missing SMART criterion.** For each of the following objectives, identify which SMART criterion it fails most badly and rewrite it to pass:

- "Improve our brand awareness."
- "Sell more units in Q3."
- "Be the best in our category by next year."
- "Increase customer retention by 5 percent."

*Tests: Objective 3. Difficulty: Low.* The exercise forces the student to apply SMART as a diagnostic rather than recite the acronym.

**2. Vision vs. mission.** For each of the following statements, identify whether it is a vision statement or a mission statement, and explain why. (Some statements may be poorly written — name the problem if you spot one.)

- "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy."
- "We sell coffee."
- "To be the most innovative tech company in the world."
- "To help every small business in our city find affordable insurance, in plain language, in under 30 minutes."

*Tests: Objectives 3, 8. Difficulty: Low.* The mission/vision distinction is one of the most consistently muddled concepts in marketing; this exercise forces a real call.

**3. Classify the strategy.** A neighborhood pizzeria with two locations is considering each of the following moves. For each, name which of the four growth strategies it is pursuing (market penetration, product development, market development, or product diversification):

- Adding a third location in a new neighborhood across town.
- Launching a line of frozen pizzas sold through local grocery stores.
- Running a TV ad campaign promoting current pies to current customers.
- Adding gluten-free crusts to the existing menu.

*Tests: Objective 6. Difficulty: Low.* Tests recognition of strategy types in disguised form.

### Application

**4. Run a BCG matrix on a real firm.** Pick a publicly traded firm with at least three product lines. Place each product line on the BCG matrix using publicly available information about market share and market growth. Justify each placement in one sentence.

Then answer two questions: (a) Which product line should receive *more* investment than it currently does, and why? (b) Which should receive *less*, and why? Be specific about what "more" or "less" investment looks like.

*Tests: Objective 4. Difficulty: Medium.* Forces the student to work the matrix on real (not stylized) data and to commit to a recommendation.

**5. Compute revenue growth and relative market share.** A firm reports the following for the last fiscal year: revenue $850 million (last year $720 million); category market size $42 billion; leading competitor's revenue $1.4 billion.

Compute year-over-year revenue growth. Compute the firm's market share. Compute relative market share (firm's share divided by leader's share). Then write one paragraph: how do you read this firm's strategic position?

*Tests: Objective 8. Difficulty: Medium.* Tests the discipline of using paired metrics rather than single metrics.

**6. Critique a SWOT.** Find a SWOT analysis online for any firm (a published one, an MBA case study, or a corporate strategy document). Identify three problems with how it was constructed. Common problems include: items listed as both strengths *and* threats; items that are not actually internal-internal-external-external; missing prioritization; vague items that wouldn't help any decision.

Then rewrite one quadrant of the SWOT to fix the problems you identified.

*Tests: Objective 5. Difficulty: Medium.* SWOT is one of the easiest frameworks to do badly; this exercise teaches the student to recognize bad SWOT in the wild.

### Synthesis

**7. The CLV strategic decision.** Use the four-segment CLV table from Concept 3. Suppose the firm has $500,000 to invest in *one* of the following options:

- Reduce CAC by 30 percent across all segments.
- Increase Gen Z retention from 2 years to 5 years.
- Increase Working Moms' annual profit per customer by 20 percent.

Compute the change in CLV for each option (across all relevant segments) and recommend which investment to make. Justify the recommendation in 200 words. Be explicit about what assumptions you are making and what evidence would change your recommendation.

*Tests: Objectives 1, 8. Difficulty: Medium-high.* Forces multi-step quantitative reasoning combined with strategic judgment.

**8. Connect the strategy chain.** Pick a company you know well. Write a one-sentence vision statement, a one-sentence mission statement, three SMART objectives, and one growth strategy (penetration / product development / market development / diversification). Then check the chain for consistency: does each level constrain the next? Does the strategy you chose fit the objectives you wrote? Would the mission survive a year of executing on those objectives?

If you find inconsistencies, fix them. The harder version: notice when the inconsistency reveals that the firm has the wrong vision, not the wrong objectives.

*Tests: Objectives 1, 3, 6. Difficulty: Medium-high.* Forces the student to feel the constraint propagation that makes strategy real.

### Challenge

**9. The ethics-and-strategy stress test.** A consumer-electronics startup is launching a smart-home device. Its leadership wants aggressive growth: 50 percent year-over-year for the next three years. Its target market is Gen Z and younger Millennials. Its current pricing strategy assumes premium positioning ($X per unit), but its competitors are pricing 30 percent lower with comparable specs.

The CEO proposes one of two paths:

- **Path A:** Hold the premium price; double down on a sustainability narrative — recycled materials, carbon-neutral shipping, donation of 1 percent of revenue to environmental causes.
- **Path B:** Cut price to match competitors; cut the sustainability features that drove cost; rely on share growth for valuation.

Use the data on Millennial/Gen Z willingness to pay for sustainable products. Compute (or estimate) which path is more likely to hit the 50 percent growth target. Then evaluate: is the more likely-to-succeed path also the more ethical one? If they diverge, what would the firm need to believe about its customers, its competitors, and its long-term position to choose the less-likely-but-more-ethical path?

*Tests: Objectives 1, 6. Difficulty: High.* Forces integration of strategy, ethics, and willingness-to-pay data into a single recommendation that is genuinely contestable.

**10. Audit the strategy theatre.** Find the public strategic-plan or annual-report language of a firm you suspect is doing strategy theatre — language that sounds strategic but doesn't actually constrain decisions. Document three specific phrases from the public materials. For each, explain *what decision the phrase was supposed to constrain* and *what the firm actually did*. If the phrases failed to constrain decisions, rewrite each phrase in one sentence so that it would constrain a real decision.

This exercise is harder than it sounds. Strategy theatre is well-defended by general-purpose corporate language. The win condition is identifying *what was missing* in the original.

*Tests: Objectives 1, 3, 7. Difficulty: High.* The most pedagogically valuable exercise in this chapter; teaches the student to read strategy critically rather than reverently.

## Chapter Summary

**What you can do now that you could not do before this chapter:**

You can take any organization's strategy materials — vision, mission, SWOT, marketing plan, KPI dashboard — and tell, in five to ten minutes of careful reading, whether the strategy is real or theatre. You can recognize a vision that constrains decisions versus one that decorates a website. You can read a SWOT and tell whether it has been prioritized or just brainstormed. You can look at a KPI list and tell whether the firm is measuring the right five to ten things. You can identify which of the four growth strategies a firm is pursuing, even when the firm does not name it.

**The one idea that matters most from this chapter:**

Strategy is the discipline of *forcing choice*. Every strategic framework — vision, mission, SMART, BCG, SWOT, the four growth strategies, the marketing plan, the KPI list — is a forcing function for a specific kind of choice the firm would otherwise defer. Frameworks done well land hard commitments that the firm has to live with. Frameworks done badly produce documents that allow every option, satisfy every stakeholder, and constrain no decision. The first kind of strategy works. The second is theatre.

**The common mistake to watch for:**

Treating the deliverables as the strategy. The vision statement is not the vision; the document is the artifact of a process the firm should have *gone through* and the constraint the firm should *now feel*. A firm with a beautifully written strategy document and unchanged daily decisions has done strategy theatre. A firm with sloppy documentation but visibly different decisions has done strategy.

**The Feynman test:**

Close the book. Pick a firm you know — Apple, your favorite restaurant chain, your university. Can you describe, in two minutes, what its strategy actually is — *not* the public mission statement, but the pattern of decisions the firm appears to make? If yes, you can now read strategy. If no, the gap tells you which framework to revisit: were the goals visible? was the portfolio explicit? did the metrics matter?
