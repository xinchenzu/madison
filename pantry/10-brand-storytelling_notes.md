## A. Conceptual foundations

### Story as meaning structure

Brand storytelling gives an audience a causal and emotional structure: who has a problem, what changed, what was tried, what proof exists, and what transformation became possible. The chapter should teach story as disciplined selection, not embellishment.

**Common misconception:** Storytelling means making the brand sound dramatic. Better: story makes the value proposition memorable and testable.

**Worked example:** "I built a tool" becomes "A technical student needed a way to make audience research visible; the tool turned messy interviews into a positioning map."

**Source(s):** TIKTOC.md; local `_lib_How_to_Deliver_a_TED_Talk.md`.

### Customer as hero

StoryBrand's widely used framing makes the customer the hero and the brand the guide. The chapter can use this as a corrective to founder-centered storytelling.

**Common misconception:** The builder is always the hero. For most product stories, the user/customer should be the protagonist.

**Worked example:** Portfolio case study: "The user needed X; I built Y; evidence showed Z."

**Source(s):** StoryBrand framework overview https://www.impactplus.com/learn/what-is-the-storybrand-framework

### Hero's Journey with caution

Joseph Campbell's monomyth is influential but should not be treated as the only story shape. It is useful for transformation narratives but can become formulaic.

**Common misconception:** Every story should be Hero's Journey. Brand stories should fit archetype, audience, and evidence.

**Source(s):** Campbell overview https://www.supersummary.com/the-heros-journey/summary/; critical overview in search results.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:** Story structures help audiences remember and evaluate meaning.

**Contested or emerging:** Formulaic storytelling frameworks can flatten cultural/contextual specificity. AI-generated stories increase this risk.

**Key references:**
1. Donald Miller, *Building a StoryBrand* — customer-as-hero.
2. Joseph Campbell, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* / monomyth tradition.

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students get stuck:** They write autobiography instead of audience-relevant proof.

**Analogies and framings that work:** "Story is evidence with sequence and stakes."

**Exercises that build the target skill:** Write one origin story, one customer-as-hero mini-case, and one evidence note listing what must be verified before publication.
