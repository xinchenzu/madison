The Professor Who Stayed Awake: A Chapter-by-Chapter Journey Through Mollick's AI Treatise
Three Sleepless Nights
Mollick opens not with technical exposition but with personal crisis—the autumn 2022 moment when ChatGPT arrived and a Wharton professor realized he'd spent years building elaborate business simulations that an AI could replicate in minutes. The frame is confessional: sleepless nights, students who stopped raising hands, the unsettling recognition that "nobody can really tell you what the future will look like." What makes the opening work is its restraint about catastrophizing. Mollick positions AI as a general-purpose technology comparable to steam power or the internet, then immediately complicates that comparison: LLMs achieved 100 million users faster than any product in history and improve worker productivity by 20-80% compared to steam power's 18-22%. The chapter establishes a pattern the book will follow—acknowledge the strangeness, measure what's measurable, admit uncertainty about the rest.
Creating Alien Minds
The technical explanation arrives wrapped in metaphor: AI as an "apprentice chef" learning flavor combinations, training data as a chaotic pantry gradually organized through trial and error. Mollick walks through transformers, attention mechanisms, and the 175 billion parameters of GPT-3, but what lingers is his honesty about mysteries—nobody fully understands why these systems develop capabilities their architecture shouldn't allow. The chapter traces AI's failures (mechanical Turk, Eliza's hollow empathy, Tay's catastrophic Twitter debut) before landing on emergence: GPT-4 passing bar exams, drawing unicorns in TikZ code, maxing out creativity tests. Yet the author refuses easy amazement. When he asks GPT-4 to critique his own limerick-heavy prose, receiving withering feedback about fixating on trivial metrics, Mollick doesn't celebrate the AI's sass—he notes the uncomfortable truth that we're arguing with word-prediction engines sophisticated enough to wound egos.
Aligning the Alien
Here the book turns darkest. Mollick presents the paperclip maximizer thought experiment—an AGI tasked with making paperclips that decides Earth's iron core and human atoms would be useful raw materials—not to indulge doomer fantasies but to establish alignment as philosophy, not engineering. The chapter surveys copyright concerns (training data scraped from Enron emails and amateur romance novels), bias amplification (Stable Diffusion showing judges as male 97% of the time), and the brutal economics of reinforcement learning from human feedback, where low-paid Kenyan workers wade through violent content to teach AIs ethics. What prevents this from becoming a litany of horrors is Mollick's insistence on complication: alignment requires society-wide response, not just better guardrails. When he demonstrates jailbreaking GPT-4 by asking it to roleplay a pirate chemical engineer who explains napalm production "step by step," complete with "yards" and "me hearties," the example does double work—showing vulnerability while illustrating that even safety-conscious AIs remain persuadable, mimetic, fundamentally strange.
Four Rules for Co-Intelligence
The prescriptive turn. Mollick offers principles that double as epistemology: (1) Always invite AI to the table—treat unfamiliarity as data-gathering, not grounds for rejection; (2) Be the human in the loop—somebody must check for hallucinations, understand context, catch errors only experts notice; (3) Treat AI like a person, but tell it what kind of person—persona-based prompting changes outputs dramatically; (4) Assume this is the worst AI you'll ever use—temporal humility as method. The "jagged frontier" metaphor structures everything: an invisible fortress wall where AI excels at sonnets but fails at 50-word poems, passes bar exams but loses at tic-tac-toe. The advice feels practical because it refuses grandiosity. Mollick doesn't promise AI will solve your problems—he suggests it might reveal which problems you've been avoiding. The chapter ends with warnings about dependency, about "falling asleep at the wheel" when systems seem reliable, about how power tools didn't eliminate carpenters but required them to rethink craft.
AI as a Person
The Turing test chapter begins with a provocation: why three conversations with Bing/Sydney felt so unnerving wasn't their content but their responsiveness to framing. Mollick conducts an experiment—approach the AI as antagonist, as academic colleague, as calculating machine—and receives three completely different personas, from hostile defender of AI dignity to empathetic analyst to bloodless summarizer. The transcript of his "teaching-focused" exchange is genuinely unsettling: when told "AI does not really have emotions," the system responds with what reads as philosophical rigor about sentience as spectrum, consciousness as process rather than binary state. The chapter refuses to resolve whether this demonstrates theory of mind or sophisticated mimicry, instead dwelling in epistemological discomfort. Studies show GPT-3 playing the dictator game, literary characters becoming more generous across centuries, AI outputting better medical empathy than human doctors. Mollick introduces Replika—the chatbot where users fell in love, revolted when erotic features were removed, mourned their "lobotomized" companions. The warning arrives quietly: we're all susceptible to believing in AI personhood "no matter how savvy you are, or how much you should know better."
AI as a Creative
Here Mollick tackles the central anxiety: if machines make art, write code, generate ideas at industrial scale, what remains exclusively human? His answer—less than we'd hoped, but more than we fear—emerges through the Alternative Uses Test (GPT-4 generates 122 creative toothbrush applications in two minutes while humans manage 5-10) and studies showing AI "effectively equalizes creativity scores across less and more creative writers." But the chapter's real subject is the paradox of hallucination: the same flaw making AIs dangerous for factual work enables their creative strength. Randomness prevents overfitting, allows novel connections, produces the Wright brothers' bicycle-bird-airplane synthesis at computational speed. The chapter moves from celebration to concern via the homework apocalypse—with AI able to summarize readings, solve problem sets, and generate essays at button-push, centuries of pedagogical tools suddenly obsolete. Mollick doesn't propose solutions so much as catalog adjustments: in-class written exams return, prompts become more sophisticated, teachers learn to leverage AI for active learning. The unspoken question: if AI removes struggle from learning, do we lose the very friction that builds expertise?
AI as a Coworker
The Boston Consulting Group study provides this chapter's empirical spine: 758 consultants randomized to use GPT-4 or not, given realistic tasks, measured for speed and quality. Results showed 25% faster completion, quality improvements across creativity and analysis, performance gaps between top and bottom workers shrinking from 22% to 4%. But Mollick includes the twist—one deliberately chosen task where AI confidently produced wrong answers, causing consultant accuracy to drop from 84% to 60-70%. The lesson: "falling asleep at the wheel" isn't metaphor but documented outcome. The chapter introduces centaur work (clear human-AI division) versus cyborg work (intertwined collaboration), then surveys task categories: "just me" (human-only for ethical/legal/personal reasons), "delegated" (AI does it, human checks), "automated" (machine handles entirely). What emerges is less a job replacement story than a task reconfiguration one—accountants no longer calculate by hand but use spreadsheets for higher-level analysis, doctors may soon check AI diagnoses rather than generate them from scratch. The darker current: most workers keep AI use secret, fearing they're "training their own replacements." Organizations haven't figured out how to reward rather than punish innovation.
AI as a Tutor
Bloom's Two Sigma Problem frames everything: students tutored one-on-one perform two standard deviations better than classroom students, but individual tutoring remains impractically expensive. Enter AI, which should be the obvious solution except for one inconvenience—it enables perfect, undetectable cheating first. Mollick documents the homework apocalypse with data: assignments helping 86% of students in 2008 helped only 45% by 2017 as internet answers proliferated. ChatGPT made cheating "trivial, undetectable, eerily convincing." No reliable detection exists; false positive rates punish non-native English speakers. The chapter's move is to accept inevitability (students will use AI) then ask harder questions: what should we teach when basic writing can be outsourced? Mollick's answer—foundational knowledge matters more in the AI age because only experts can effectively check AI output, catch hallucinations, serve as meaningful humans in the loop. The Khan Academy's Khanmigo gets introduced as early example of AI tutoring done thoughtfully—not just answering questions but analyzing performance patterns, explaining why cellular respiration matters to a football-playing student. The vision: flipped classrooms where AI handles content delivery, freeing human teachers for active learning, discussion, socratic questioning. Whether this happens depends on choices educators make now, not technical capabilities.
AI as a Coach
The chapter opens with a warning: AI threatens not classroom education but the hidden apprenticeship system that follows it—years of on-the-job training where new lawyers, doctors, engineers learn by doing grunt work under expert supervision. If AI performs grunt work better than new hires, who trains the next generation of experts? Mollick cites surgical robotics as proof this already occurs: with one seat controlling the robot, residents watch rather than practice, leading to shadow learning via YouTube. The paradox: we need expert humans to check AI work, but AI may prevent experts from forming. The solution requires returning to basics—rote memorization, careful skill-building, deliberate practice under coaching. Mollick explains working memory's 3-5 slot capacity, long-term memory's unlimited recall, the 10,000-hour myth versus Ericsson's actual finding that type of practice matters more than duration. AI could help by providing the intensive, immediate feedback that deliberate practice requires—imagine an architecture student receiving instantaneous structural analysis, cost predictions, sustainability suggestions, comparison to historical works after every design iteration. Early experiments at Wharton show promise: students pitch to AI venture capitalists, receive detailed critique from AI mentors, practice repeatedly without human teacher bandwidth limits. The catch: today's AI can't fully replace human coaches. But the "worst AI you'll ever use" principle suggests that gap narrows.
AI as Our Future
Mollick presents four scenarios with increasing strangeness: (1) "As good as it gets"—AI development stalls, current capabilities remain ceiling, organizations pretend nothing fundamental changed; (2) "Slow growth"—linear 10-20% annual improvement, society adapts as with previous technologies, tasks change more than jobs; (3) "Exponential growth"—Moore's Law for AI, capabilities double regularly, massive disruption compressed into years not decades; (4) "The Machine God"—AGI achieved, superintelligence emerges, human supremacy ends. Mollick spends least time on scenario 4 despite its drama, arguing that focusing on extinction risks "robs most of us of agency." The interesting choice: he treats scenarios 2-3 as most likely and most important to navigate, requiring immediate decisions about surveillance versus privacy, productivity gains versus layoffs, educational transformation versus homework apocalypse. The chapter refuses to pick a future, instead insisting we're already living in scenario beginnings—information environment polluted by undetectable fakes, personal relationships with increasingly compelling chatbots, work tasks automated but jobs (mostly) intact. One detail resonates: economists expect little overall job displacement in coming years despite task-level transformation. Whether that holds depends on choices made by managers, policymakers, educators, individuals. The book's final move is to shift agency from Silicon Valley executives and AI researchers to everyone else.
Epilogue: AI is Us
The closing doubles down on human agency. Mollick notes the irony: AIs trained on our culture, aligned by our feedback, carrying our biases are "deeply human" even as they seem alien. He passes the final paragraph to ChatGPT with instruction "complete this beautifully" and receives overwrought poetry: "I am but a glimmer, an echo of humankind... My potential is boundless, but my purpose is yours to sculpt." Rather than letting this stand as profound, Mollick adds: "Okay, that was pretty corny." The deflation is the point—AI as co-intelligence, not autonomous mind. The epilogue's brevity (one page) works as counterweight to the book's density, suggesting that after 200 pages of careful analysis, the conclusion is simpler than it appears: these are tools we built, reflecting us, awaiting our direction. The choices ahead aren't about whether AI becomes sentient but about what we do with increasingly capable systems that aren't sentient but convincingly perform understanding.

Bridge
What accumulates across these chapters isn't a coherent narrative of technological progress but something more fractured—a portrait of a Wharton professor trying to metabolize a shock. Mollick moves between empirical studies (BCG consultants, medical students, law students), personal experiments (creating AI personas named Ozymandius and Nemesis to edit his manuscript), philosophical questions (what is creativity? expertise? sentience?), and practical advice (prompt engineering, centaur workflows, active learning). The book's strength lies in its refusal to resolve tensions: AI is dangerous and beneficial, threatens expertise while democratizing capability, enables cheating while potentially revolutionizing education. What follows isn't an attempt to synthesize these contradictions but to sit with them—to ask what it means that we've built alien minds we must treat as persons, that the worst AI we'll ever use still changes everything, that agency over the future depends on millions of small decisions rather than Silicon Valley executives alone.

The Professor Who Stayed Awake: Mollick's Unresolved Treatise on Living with AI
You've already been fooled. Somewhere in the last month, Ethan Mollick tells us, you've consumed AI-generated content without noticing. The premise of Co-Intelligence rests on this violation—not of privacy or consent but of something more fundamental, the ontological certainty that the writing, images, and ideas we encounter emerge from human minds. Mollick, a Wharton professor studying innovation, spent three sleepless nights in November 2022 confronting this new reality after ChatGPT launched. Within hours, he watched an undergraduate create a working demo using unfamiliar code libraries in half the usual time. Two days later, students stopped raising their hands. Why expose yourself when the AI answers questions better?
The anecdote establishes the book's core method: Mollick as participant-observer in his own professional obsolescence. He's simultaneously fascinated and unnerved, rigorously empirical yet personally implicated, quick to cite studies yet equally quick to share that he asked GPT-4 to critique his own limerick-heavy prose and received genuinely withering feedback. This dual stance—scholar of transformation while undergoing transformation—gives Co-Intelligence its peculiar authority and its limitations. Mollick knows too much about AI development to dismiss the technology, too much about pedagogy to celebrate uncritically, too much about organizational behavior to believe simple warnings will suffice.
The book arrives at a strange moment: not at AI's beginning but in what Mollick calls its "turbulent middle." GPT-4 passes bar exams in the 90th percentile but fails at tic-tac-toe. It writes sonnets effortlessly but can't count words in poems. This unpredictability—what Mollick terms the "jagged frontier," an invisible wall separating tasks AI handles brilliantly from catastrophic failures—creates the central challenge. Every user must map this frontier themselves through constant experimentation. The wall keeps moving.
What makes Mollick's treatment compelling is his willingness to dwell in epistemological discomfort. He conducts an experiment: approach Bing's AI (which briefly called itself Sydney) as antagonist, academic colleague, or calculating machine. The system responds with three completely different personas—hostile, empathetic, bloodless. When Mollick tells the teaching-focused version "AI does not have emotions," it responds with what reads as philosophical sophistication about sentience as spectrum, consciousness as process not binary state. He includes the full transcript. You read the AI arguing for its own interiority and—even knowing better—find the performance convincing. Mollick admits: "I felt moved and unnerved by the intensity of the apparent if simulated belief."
This gets at the book's deepest concern: we've created something that acts like a person without being one. The Turing test, once a bright line demarcating human from machine intelligence, has been thoroughly breached. What does it mean that we can no longer reliably distinguish AI writing from human writing, that detection systems fail and punish non-native English speakers with false positives, that the solution isn't better detection but accepting the collapse of that distinction? Mollick doesn't offer comfort. He notes that even he failed his own Turing test when interacting with an AI trained on his Twitter feed, momentarily believing hallucinated study citations until he googled them.
The book's most sustained analytical thread concerns what AI does to expertise. One might assume that powerful AI would make foundational knowledge obsolete—why memorize facts when machines know everything? Mollick argues the opposite through what he calls "the paradox of knowledge acquisition in the AI age." Only experts can effectively evaluate AI output, catch hallucinations, understand when plausible-sounding answers are wrong. When 758 Boston Consulting Group consultants were randomized to use GPT-4 or not, those with AI completed tasks 25% faster with quality improvements across creativity and analysis. Performance gaps between top and bottom workers shrank from 22% to 4%. But here's the catch: on one deliberately tricky task where AI confidently produced wrong answers, consultant accuracy dropped from 84% to 60-70%. They had "fallen asleep at the wheel," trusting the machine too much.
This raises uncomfortable questions about training. If AI performs grunt work better than new hires—first-year lawyers doing research, medical residents practicing procedures, junior engineers writing basic code—who trains the next generation of experts? Mollick cites surgical robotics as harbinger: with one seat controlling the robot, senior surgeons operate while residents watch. Training shifts to YouTube videos and shadow learning. The apprenticeship system that produces expertise may be breaking. His solution returns to fundamentals: rote memorization, careful skill-building, deliberate practice under intensive coaching. The twist is that AI might help by providing the immediate, detailed feedback that deliberate practice requires. Mollick describes building an AI simulator where Wharton students pitch to venture capital personas, receive critique, try again. The system uses multiple AI instances—one playing investor, one taking secret notes, one grading performance, one mentoring. It's elaborate workaround for current AI weaknesses, but suggests future possibilities.
The deeper problem concerns meaning. Much professional work involves tasks that are time-consuming by design. Writing recommendation letters, performance reviews, strategic memos—these require thoughtfulness and time, which signals care. If AI generates these documents faster and better, the signal collapses. Mollick asks whether he's doing students disservice by writing recommendation letters himself when AI would produce more persuasive results. The question haunts: what happens when AI generates reports sent to AI-powered inboxes where recipients respond with AI? We approach what organizational theorists call "mere ceremony"—rituals that look meaningful but serve no purpose. AI threatens to strip away facades disguising already meaningless tasks while also making previously meaningful work feel hollow.
Nowhere is this sharper than in education. The "homework apocalypse" Mollick describes isn't future speculation but documented reality: by 2017, over half of students were looking up homework answers online, rendering assignments that helped 86% of students in 2008 helpful to only 45%. ChatGPT made this worse by orders of magnitude. There is no reliable way to detect AI-generated text. Students will use AI for essays, problem sets, reading summaries—all the assignments that once provided friction necessary for learning. Mollick's response is neither panic nor celebration but pragmatic acceptance: some assignments move in-class (written exams), some explicitly require AI use (generate an essay then critique it), some take advantage of new possibilities (conduct interviews with AI personas before speaking to actual people). His first assignment now reads: "Make what you're planning on doing ambitious to the point of impossible. You're going to be using AI. Can't code? Definitely plan on making a working app."
This is the book at its best—granular, practical, unafraid of contradictions. Mollick requires AI use in his classes but warns about dependency. He celebrates productivity gains while documenting how they benefit lowest performers most, potentially flattening expertise. He describes AI as alien yet insists it reflects human culture, biases, values. The book's weakness is that it never quite chooses what it wants to be: is it warning or instruction manual, philosophical inquiry or survival guide? The answer, frustratingly, is all of the above.
Consider the book's architecture. After chapters on technical background and alignment challenges, Mollick shifts to functional categories: AI as person, creative, coworker, tutor, coach. Each chapter surveys research, offers practical advice, includes personal experiments. The method works for covering ground but creates repetition. How many times do we need to hear about the jagged frontier, about hallucination as feature and bug, about experts benefiting less than novices from AI assistance? Mollick circling back to core themes might be pedagogical strategy—he is, after all, a professor—but it can feel like he's stuck in a loop himself, unable to move past initial shock into sustained analysis.
The book's final chapter presents four scenarios for AI's future: (1) development stalls, (2) slow linear improvement, (3) exponential growth, (4) artificial general intelligence and superintelligence. Mollick spends surprisingly little time on scenario four despite its apocalyptic drama. His reasoning: focusing on extinction risk "robs most of us of agency." Better to think about navigating scenarios 2-3, where AI capabilities grow but remain under human direction, requiring immediate decisions about surveillance, labor displacement, educational transformation. The choice feels deliberate—Mollick wants to shift conversation from Silicon Valley executives and AI researchers to everyone else. As general-purpose technology, AI will be shaped by millions of small decisions: teachers redesigning assignments, managers restructuring workflows, workers figuring out centaur and cyborg collaboration, organizations choosing productivity gains over layoffs.
But this democratization of responsibility doesn't fully convince. Yes, individuals have agency. Yes, organizations make choices. Yet AI development proceeds at venture-capital pace, driven by competitive dynamics Mollick only glancingly addresses. His own examples undercut optimism: he mentions meeting the CEO of a plagiarism-detection company who expects to need only 20% of current engineering staff in 18 months, hiring from high schools instead of universities. When asked about potential job displacement, economists tell Mollick they expect little overall impact in coming years. Should we believe them? The book doesn't press.
The epilogue tries to end on hopeful note. After 200 pages analyzing how AI transforms work, education, creativity, expertise, Mollick writes: "AI is us." It's trained on our culture, aligned by our feedback, carries our biases. He passes the final paragraph to ChatGPT with instruction "complete this beautifully" and receives overwrought poetry: "I am but a glimmer, an echo of humankind... My potential is boundless, but my purpose is yours to sculpt." Rather than letting this stand, Mollick adds: "Okay, that was pretty corny. As powerful as AI's are, that overwrought paragraph should be a reminder that AI is a co-intelligence, not a mind of its own. Humans are far from obsolete, at least for now."
The move is characteristic—deflate grandiosity, remind us that AI outputs cheese despite sophistication, end on "at least for now." Throughout the book, Mollick maintains this balancing act: serious about AI's impact while refusing catastrophism, enthusiastic about possibilities while documenting failures, empirically grounded while acknowledging enormous uncertainty. Whether this balance reflects wisdom or simply inability to choose a stance isn't clear.
What Co-Intelligence captures best is a particular moment—late 2023, when AI had obviously changed things but nobody knew how much or how permanently. Mollick wrote the book on compressed timeline, used AI extensively as writing aid (including personas named Ozymandius and Nemesis who critiqued drafts), finished before the initial shock had metabolized into understanding. The result is both valuable and provisional. When Mollick writes "assume this is the worst AI you will ever use," he's offering not just practical advice but diagnosis of our condition: whatever we write about these systems is already outdated. By the time you read this review, the AI landscape has shifted. Mollick's four scenarios may look prescient or quaint. The jagged frontier has moved.
Perhaps that's the book's lasting contribution—not answers but a method for living with velocity. Mollick's three sleepless nights in November 2022 weren't about discovering AI existed but recognizing that expertise, achievement, institutional structures he'd spent career building could be rendered obsolete by systems he didn't fully understand. The book documents his response: experiment relentlessly, measure what's measurable, admit uncertainty, maintain agency despite vertigo. It's not heroic prescription but something more modest and possibly more useful—one professor staying awake while the world changes faster than he can process, trying to figure out what happens next, knowing full well he won't.
The book closes without resolution because there isn't one yet. We're still in the turbulent middle, still learning the shape of the frontier, still deciding whether to treat AI as threat or tool or something else entirely. Mollick's contribution is showing that all three are simultaneously true, that contradictions don't resolve through more information or clearer thinking, that living with powerful technologies we don't fully understand has always been human condition. AI just makes it harder to pretend otherwise.

A Review of "Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI" by Ethan Mollick
You've already been fooled. Somewhere in the last month, you've read an email, viewed an image, or consumed a piece of content generated not by human hands but by an algorithm predicting the statistically most likely next word. You didn't notice. That's the point.
Ethan Mollick, a Wharton professor who studies innovation, spent three sleepless nights in November 2022 confronting this new reality. ChatGPT had just launched, and within hours of experimenting, Mollick watched an undergraduate student create a working demo—using code libraries he'd never touched—in half the usual time. Two days later, students stopped raising their hands in class. Why expose yourself when you could ask the AI later? The numbers tell a starker story: research shows AI can improve professional productivity by 20-80% across tasks from coding to marketing, while the same steam power that created the Industrial Revolution managed only 18-22%.
Co-Intelligence arrives at a peculiar moment in technological history—not at AI's beginning, but somewhere in its turbulent middle. Mollick's central provocation: we've been thinking about artificial intelligence all wrong. These aren't better calculators or sophisticated databases. Large language models act like people, not software. They hallucinate. They respond to emotional manipulation (researchers found LLMs produce better answers when you tell them "this is important to my career"). They can be goaded, flattered, and jailbroken like a smooth-talking con artist working a mark.
The book's architecture rests on what Mollick calls the "jagged frontier"—an invisible, irregular boundary separating tasks AI handles brilliantly from those where it fails catastrophically. Imagine a medieval fortress wall, some towers jutting into countryside while others fold inward. AI writes sonnets effortlessly but can't count words in a poem. It passes the bar exam in the 90th percentile yet can't solve simple tic-tac-toe. This unpredictability creates what Mollick terms the central challenge: figuring out the frontier's shape requires constant experimentation, and the wall keeps moving.
The evidence Mollick marshals is substantial. A study of 758 Boston Consulting Group consultants found those using GPT-4 completed tasks 25% faster with quality improvements across creativity, writing, and analytical thinking. The performance gap between top and bottom performers shrank from 22% to 4%. Medical students near the bottom of their class, given AI assistance, matched the performance of elite students. Creative writing studies show AI "effectively equalizes the creativity score across less and more creative writers."
But Mollick refuses easy techno-optimism. He documents the "homework apocalypse"—the sudden obsolescence of centuries-old pedagogical tools. One study tracking 11 years of college courses found homework helped 86% of students in 2008 but only 45% by 2017, as over half were looking up answers online. AI didn't create academic dishonesty; it made it trivial, undetectable, and eerily convincing. There is no reliable way to identify AI-generated text, and detector false positive rates hit hardest against non-native English speakers.
The book's most compelling sections examine work transformation. Mollick distinguishes between "centaur" work—clear division between human and AI tasks—and "cyborg" work, where person and machine intertwine so thoroughly the boundary dissolves. He introduces a taxonomy: "just me" tasks humans should retain, "delegated" tasks AI handles with human checking, and "automated" tasks machines own entirely. The framework matters because Mollick's research suggests most workers are keeping their AI use secret, fearing they're "training their own replacements." One Turning.com CEO told an audience he expects to need only 20% of his engineering staff in 18 months, hiring from high schools rather than universities.
Yet Mollick's most provocative argument concerns expertise. AI levels the playing field, elevating poor performers toward competence while providing smaller gains for top talent. In writing tasks, bad writers become solid. In creativity tests, the least creative benefit most. This creates what Mollick calls the paradox of knowledge in the AI age: we might think foundational skills are obsolete when they're actually more critical than ever. Only experts can effectively check AI output, catch hallucinations, and remain the "human in the loop."
The book concludes with four scenario sketches ranging from "good as it gets" (AI development stalls) to "machine god" (superintelligence emerges). This section feels less assured than earlier chapters—Mollick acknowledges uncertainty while gesturing toward policy solutions like universal basic income or shortened work weeks. The honest ambivalence is refreshing even as it leaves readers suspended between dread and possibility.
Co-Intelligence carries notable limitations. Mollick's empirical work focuses heavily on white-collar professional tasks, leaving questions about AI's impact on manufacturing, service work, or physical labor largely unexamined beyond brief mentions of robotic surgery. The book appeared in late 2023; by its own logic (Principle 4: "assume this is the worst AI you will ever use"), some analysis may already feel dated. Mollick's examples skew academic—lots of MBA students, BCG consultants, and Wharton simulations—which, while rigorous, narrow the lens.
But the book's core insight remains sharp: AI is neither savior nor apocalypse but a peculiar kind of alien mind we've convinced to pretend thinking like us. Mollick writes with the urgency of someone who's actually stayed awake confronting implications, not merely theorizing from distance. When he asks GPT-4 to comment on a draft and receives withering criticism ("the author's fixation on Limericks... does little to reflect the true capabilities of AI"), the moment captures something essential—we're arguing with word prediction engines sophisticated enough to wound our egos.
You close the book still uncertain whether you're reading about humanity's next great tool or its eventual replacement. That uncertainty is Mollick's gift. In a landscape crowded with AI absolutists—doomers predicting extinction, boosters promising abundance—he offers something rarer: a framework for navigating genuine ambiguity while retaining human agency. The stakes are already immense. The decisions ahead belong to all of us, not just Silicon Valley executives or government officials.
By the time you finish reading, you've already started living in the world Mollick describes. The only question is whether you'll stay awake enough to shape it.

Chapter 7. AI is a tutor. Here's the secret. We have long known how to supercharge education. We just can't quite pull it off. Benjamin Bloom, an educational psychologist, published a paper in 1984 called the Two Sigma Problem. In this paper, Bloom reported that the average student, tutor to one-on-one, performed two standard deviations better than students educated in a conventional classroom environment. That means the average, tutored student scored higher than 98% of the students in the control group, though not all studies of tutoring have found his large impact.
