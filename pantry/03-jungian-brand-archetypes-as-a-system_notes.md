## A. Conceptual foundations

### Archetype as strategic constraint

Mark and Pearson's brand archetype approach popularized a twelve-archetype system for brand meaning. The chapter should treat archetypes as constraints on promise, tone, evidence, story, and refusal, not as personality quizzes.

**Common misconception:** Students pick archetypes by preference. Better: choose the archetype that fits audience need, product proof, and credible behavior.

**Worked example:** A Sage brand must demonstrate method and evidence; a Hero brand must demonstrate challenge and achievement.

**Source(s):** Mark and Pearson overview https://thebrandarchetypes.com/history/pearson-mark.html; archetype list https://thebrandarchetypes.com/archetypes.html

### Archetype drift

Archetype drift occurs when a brand's signals move away from the meaning customers recognize. Drift can be visual, verbal, behavioral, or strategic.

**Common misconception:** "Freshness" is always good. Freshness that destroys recognition can damage brand equity.

**Worked example:** A Caregiver brand using aggressive challenger language creates tone conflict.

**Source(s):** Tropicana redesign case https://www.packworld.com/trends/package-design/article/22931061/tropicana-weathers-blowback-from-shrinquivelence-pack-redesign; Gap redesign backlash https://www.thebrandingjournal.com/2021/04/learnings-gap-logo-redesign-fail/

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case: Tropicana packaging redesign

Tropicana's 2009 packaging redesign is widely used as a recognition-risk case; reports commonly cite a roughly 20% sales drop and quick rollback. Use it to show visual identity severing memory.

### Case: Gap 2010 logo redesign

Gap's 2010 redesign was rolled back after rapid backlash. The teaching point is not "people hate change"; it is that identity assets carry recognition capital.

### Failure case: Archetype without proof behavior

A brand can claim "Creator" or "Sage," but if its artifacts do not demonstrate craft or evidence, the archetype becomes empty costume.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:** Archetypes are useful brand-strategy heuristics and teaching tools, especially for consistency and recognition.

**Contested or emerging:** Archetype systems can oversimplify audiences and cultures. The chapter should teach them as decision constraints, not universal psychology.

**Key references:**
1. Margaret Mark and Carol Pearson, *The Hero and the Outlaw*, 2001 — canonical brand archetype source.
2. Brand Archetypes history page — concise framework context.
3. Tropicana and Gap redesign cases — recognition-risk cases.

**Recent developments:** AI makes voice and visual generation cheap, increasing the risk of archetype drift through inconsistent generated variants.

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students get stuck:** They confuse archetype labels with strategy.

**Analogies and framings that work:** "An archetype is a constraint file for brand behavior."

**Exercises that build the target skill:** Archetype brief: promise, proof, tone, visual implications, shadow risk, and three things the brand refuses.
