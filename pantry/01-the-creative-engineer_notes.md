## A. Conceptual foundations

### Creative Engineer as integrated role

The Creative Engineer combines problem selection, technical building, brand positioning, and shipping. This is not "engineer plus decoration"; it is a role definition for a market where AI compresses implementation and raises the premium on judgment, visibility, and evidence.

**Common misconception:** Students believe branding starts after the product works. The chapter should show that scope and positioning shape what gets built.

**Worked example:** Two students build similar AI dashboards. One ships a generic dashboard; the other names the audience, pain, evidence, and proof path. The second is more legible to employers and users.

**Source(s):** TIKTOC.md; GitHub Copilot study https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590; Stack Overflow 2024 AI survey https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2024/ai

### Signaling and legibility

If AI makes artifacts easier to produce, artifacts must carry clearer signals: who the work serves, what constraint it solves, what evidence supports it, and why the builder's judgment is visible. This is compatible with signaling theory even if the chapter does not need a full economics digression.

**Common misconception:** "More projects" equals stronger portfolio. The better principle is "clearer proof beats more artifacts."

**Worked example:** A GitHub repo without README, target user, screenshots, and case narrative signals less than a smaller but well-positioned project.

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case: Copilot productivity and the value of non-code judgment

Copilot's measured speedup supports the claim that code production is being repriced. The teaching move is not "coding is dead"; it is "coding alone is less legible."

### Case: MKBHD-style technical credibility as brand

Use local `_lib_MKBHD.md` if relevant: the lesson is that technical explanation, consistency, taste, and audience trust can become a professional moat.

### Failure case: The undifferentiated AI project

Students often build chatbots or dashboards with no audience, no problem statement, and no proof. The chapter can use this as a recurring anti-example.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:** AI coding and production tools are widely used and can accelerate routine output.

**Contested or emerging:** Whether AI reduces demand for technical workers or changes the mix of valued skills remains contested. Teach as labor-market risk, not deterministic prophecy.

**Key references:**

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students get stuck:** They write role labels instead of value propositions.

**Analogies and framings that work:** "A project is a product sample. A Creative Engineer explains why the sample matters."

**Exercises that build the target skill:** Write a positioning statement with audience, artifact, proof, and refusal: "I build X for Y so they can Z; I will not claim A until I can prove B."
